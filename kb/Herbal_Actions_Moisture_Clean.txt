This document is part of The Herbal Conservatory’s Herbal Actions Mini Course. It discusses moisture as a constitutional and energetic principle and compares moistening and drying herbs.
We don’t often think about the role moisture plays in herbal medicine. But maybe we should.
Because when you look closely—really closely—you begin to see that how an herb influences the moisture in the body can be the line between healing… and harm.
This isn’t just about dehydration or drinking enough water. It’s about something more nuanced. More constitutional. Because two people can walk into your clinic with the same disease, and yet need completely different remedies—simply because one of them runs dry and the other damp.
And if you miss that difference? That’s where things can start to go wrong. It’s one of the most common mistakes herbalists make. Not because they don’t care—but because they haven’t been taught to look for it.
Now, when we talk about moisture, there’s a tendency to think of hydration strictly in terms of water. But that’s only half the story. The other half? Oils and fats. They coat the nerves. They form the membrane of every single cell in your body. They lubricate your joints. They’re part of your moisture makeup—just as essential as water, and just as vulnerable to imbalance.
We'll go deeper into how these patterns express in different constitutions later. For now, let’s shift our focus—to the plants themselves.
There’s something important to notice here. A kind of pattern hiding in plain sight.
Most herbal remedies are drying.
It’s easy to overlook, but once you see it, it’s everywhere. Diuretics, for instance, draw fluids out of the tissues and into the bladder. Drying. Diaphoretics pull moisture out through the skin via sweat. Again—drying. Bitters ramp up digestion, and those secretions leave the body. Drying. Astringents tighten and tone, reducing fluid and puckering tissue. Drying. Even stimulating expectorants can dry you out.
And here’s the thing: in today’s world, many people already run dry—either by nature or by habit. Stress. Screen time. Stimulants. Dehydrating diets. It all adds up. So when you pile on drying herbs without noticing the baseline? You run the risk of making things worse.
Now, what about the other side of the spectrum?
This is where it gets interesting. Because there’s really just one major category of herbs that add moisture back in: the demulcents.
Sometimes called sweet tonics. Sometimes described as yin supplements. Their defining feature is mucilage—those slippery, gooey polysaccharides that have a soft spot for your mucous membranes. They coat. They soothe. They replenish.
The systems they support—digestive, respiratory, urinary—are often the first to show signs of dryness. But it doesn’t stop there. Joints can dry out too, as synovial fluid diminishes. And the nervous system? That’s where oils do their quiet work, and when those oils are gone, things don’t run so smoothly.
So where does this leave us?
With a simple, overlooked question: Is the body too dry… or too damp?
Because once you start asking that—really asking—it changes the way you choose your herbs.
Let’s take a look at what those moistening and drying qualities actually look like in plants.
Moisture: The Quiet Power of Softness
We don’t usually think of dampness as a strength. But in the world of herbal medicine, it’s precisely that. Subtle. Passive. But powerful.
Let’s start with a passage from herbalist Matthew Wood. In The Practice of Traditional Western Herbalism, he describes dampness as “the passive capacity”—something that gives pliancy, flexibility, and ease of movement. But also, something that doesn’t hold its shape unless something else contains it. Dampness, he says, spreads until it meets a boundary. If there’s no boundary, it simply keeps going.
What does that mean for us?
It means that moistening herbs—those that restore or reinforce this damp quality—play a surprisingly foundational role in herbal therapeutics. They’re the unsung heroes in cases of tightness, constriction, and dryness. They bring back movement. They allow tissues to bend, soften, and open up again.
Think about the nature of water. It dissolves. It carries. It soothes. And when the body lacks that quality—whether from a loss of water or oils—things start to stick. Nutrients don’t flow. Waste products don’t clear out. The system slows down. Stagnates.
And the oils? These are especially critical for the endocrine system. Many hormones are fat-soluble. Without the proper oil-based transport, they don’t travel effectively. They don’t signal. They don’t do their work. That’s how deep the effect of moisture goes—it reaches into the very communication systems of the body.
Matthew outlines this in degrees. In the first degree, moisture helps substances move. In the second, it lubricates surfaces. In the third, it softens hardness. And in the fourth, it nourishes the flesh. It’s a simple framework—but an illuminating one.
Because if you’ve ever worked with someone who’s dry—truly dry—you’ve seen how the body hardens. How tissues become brittle, resistant, undernourished. And how a moistening herb can begin to soften that hardness. That’s not just theory—that’s the definition of an emollient. A softener of tissues.
Now, let’s look at where this quality lives in the materia medica.
Demulcents are the go-to category. They moisten dryness. Often they’re the plants you’d expect: marshmallow (Althaea officinalis), slippery elm (Ulmus rubra)—the mucilaginous, slimy ones. But not all demulcents are so obviously slippery. Some are sweet. Some oily. Some “meaty” in their taste and texture.
Most share a common affinity for the body’s mucosal systems: the lungs, the digestive tract, the urinary system. Others focus on joints or nerves. But they all do something similar: they restore pliancy where rigidity has taken hold.
And that’s key. Because once a tissue becomes too dry, it becomes undernourished. Atrophic. Weak. Moistening herbs don’t just coat—they rebuild. They nourish depleted organs and tissues from the inside out.
That said, moistening herbs aren’t universally helpful. There are times when they’re not just unhelpful—but counterproductive.
If there’s already too much dampness in the system—if there’s swelling, edema, nausea, a boggy tongue, a damp cough, or any of the classic signs of fluid accumulation—then adding more moisture can worsen the picture. It’s like pouring water into a sponge that’s already saturated.
So while moistening remedies are essential, they require discernment. The right plant, for the right pattern, in the right person.
Next, we’ll look at a list of moistening plants and the specific organs they tend to support. Because patterns are nothing without practical application.
Drying Herbs: The Hidden Majority
We’ve said it before, but it bears repeating: most herbs are drying.
Not just some. Not many. But nearly all.
It’s not something we always talk about in herbal circles. The tendency is to focus on the plant’s name, its tradition, or its action—less often on its influence on the body’s moisture. But if there’s one general rule that quietly governs herbal medicine, it’s this: herbs move fluids. And usually, they move them out.
The list of moistening herbs we discussed earlier isn’t exhaustive—but it’s close. In Western herbalism, if an herb doesn’t show up there, chances are it dries. Sometimes slowly. Sometimes subtly. But the direction is the same.
So how exactly do plants dry us out?
The mechanisms are varied, but the end result is remarkably consistent. Diuretics increase urination. Diaphoretics induce sweating. Astringents reduce swellings and tighten tissue. Bitters stimulate digestive secretions that eventually leave the body. Expectorants clear mucus from the lungs. These are all fluid-moving, fluid-losing actions.
And that’s just on the physiological level. Energetically, many drying herbs also happen to be warming. And warmth, as we know, evaporates.
Among these, one action stands out: astringency.
Astringents are drying in the most immediate, visceral way. You can taste it. That puckering sensation on the tongue? That’s astringency pulling fluid from tissue—and it’s doing the same thing wherever else it goes in the body. Contracting. Constricting. Drying up excess. Preventing loss. And while most astringents are drying, their temperature is less predictable. Some are warming. Some are cooling. (You’ll explore this more deeply in the astringents lesson under Herbal Actions.)
So, what’s the takeaway?
It’s this: Know your moistening allies.
Because when nearly everything else in the toolkit is drying, the few that restore fluid balance become indispensable. These demulcents aren’t rare—they’re just essential. And you’ll find yourself returning to them again and again.
In fact, many herbalists will add moistening herbs to their formulas almost by default. Marshmallow root (Althaea officinalis), for example, might be given as a tea alongside a drier combination, or added in powdered form to soften the edges of an otherwise intense formula. It’s not just a preference—it’s a strategy.
So what happens when we overlook this?
We miss patterns. We create imbalance. And sometimes, we simply don’t get results. You can have the right herb, the right action—but if the person is dry, and you haven’t addressed it, things just don’t work as well.
Look for the signs.
Dry eyes. Dry skin. Brittle hair and nails. Constipation. Nervousness. Fatigue. Cracking joints. A dry tongue. These aren’t isolated complaints. They’re all part of the same constitutional picture: dryness. Sometimes referred to as yin deficiency.
And it’s common. So common, in fact, that many protocols in Western herbalism fall short simply because this pattern wasn’t accounted for. Without moisture, the body struggles to absorb and distribute the very remedies we’re offering. The message doesn’t get through.
We’ll go into more detail about this under the constitutions section, and in the discussion of the dry/atrophy tissue state. But for now, remember this:
Moisture isn’t just a side note in herbalism.
It’s the medium through which everything else works.
