Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica)
We tend to think of powerful medicines as things that are hidden away—rare, exotic, or expensive. But sometimes, the most profound remedies are simply lying low, growing quietly in the mud.
Gotu kola is one of those. A perennial creeper with fan-shaped leaves, it thrives in waterlogged places—riverbanks, rice paddies, and swamps across India, Sri Lanka, Japan, China, and even parts of South Africa and the South Pacific. It’s found at sea level and, surprisingly, at altitudes up to 2000m. In a way, it’s hard to avoid it—especially if you’re in Asia. It shows up in salads, fresh juices, and traditional recipes. You might not notice it. But it’s there.
Its Latin name, Centella asiatica, gives us two clues. “Centella” refers to the tiny, needle-like appearance of its fruit—like two little prickles. “Asiatica” points, of course, to its home territory.
But the plant’s story stretches far beyond Latin. Gotu kola has been used for thousands of years in Ayurvedic medicine. It appears in the Sushruta Samhita, a foundational text that some historians date back to 1000 BCE. It’s also a staple in Traditional Chinese Medicine, recorded in the Shennong Herbal, which goes back as far as the first or second century CE.
Over time, it has gathered many names. In India, it’s sometimes called Jal Brahmi. In Mandarin, Ji Xue Cao. In Hawaii, Pohe kula. In Sri Lanka, it’s part of a popular salad dish called Sambol. And in many places, it has become known as the “miracle elixir of life.”
Why?
Well, part of it is legend. In Sri Lanka, locals reportedly observed elephants—those famously long-lived and sharp-minded animals—snacking on gotu kola leaves. Inspired, they followed suit. A leaf a day, they said, gave you 25 more years of life. Two leaves gave you 50. Three made you ageless.
But beyond the folklore, there’s science and tradition. Gotu kola has been used to support mental clarity and brain balance. In Ayurvedic thought, it’s considered one of the most spiritual herbs—associated with the crown chakra and the subtle balance between the left and right hemispheres of the brain. In fact, if you look closely at the leaf, it resembles the shape of a brain. It’s a visual metaphor that aligns with the ancient Doctrine of Signatures—the idea that a plant’s appearance hints at its function.
Its traditional uses span from treating leprosy and psoriasis to promoting wound healing and longevity. Its popularity in skincare today likely owes to its antioxidant profile. In Ayurvedic terms, it’s a tri-doshic herb, which means it balances all three doshas—Vata, Pitta, and Kapha. Few herbs can claim that.
There is a small caveat. Because gotu kola often grows in wild, water-heavy environments, sourcing matters. In India especially, some rivers are polluted. So it’s not just what you take—it’s where it’s from.
But taken wisely, this humble creeper becomes something far more than a salad green. It becomes a link—a thread that ties together ancient wisdom, modern science, and the quiet persistence of plants that grow in the wettest, most overlooked corners of the world.
The 5 Pillars of Gotu Kola
Taste
Taste, when it comes to herbs, is not just about flavor. It’s information. It tells us how a plant might act in the body—how it moves, where it goes, what it does when it gets there.
With Gotu kola, herbalists notice a range of impressions. Some describe it as fragrant and even slightly spicy. But most agree on three dominant notes: pungent, bitter, and sweet. There’s also a subtle astringency—thanks to the presence of tannins—that lingers in the background. You may not catch it right away. But it’s there, shaping the experience in quiet, tactile ways.
Interestingly, for all its medicinal power, Gotu kola is nearly odorless. Unlike something like Lavender or Rosemary, with their bold, aromatic signatures, Gotu kola keeps its scent close. Its volatile oils simply aren’t as expressive. And that, too, is a kind of message. This is a plant that works beneath the surface—quietly, steadily—without calling attention to itself.
Primary Actions
When we talk about herbal actions, we often look for one defining trait—a single mechanism that explains everything. But some plants resist that simplicity. They operate on multiple levels. Gotu kola is one of them.
Let’s start with the idea of an alterative. Herbalists use this word to describe plants that assist in the body’s elimination processes—supporting detoxification by “opening the channels” through which waste exits. But that’s still vague. What does it really mean?
With Gotu kola, the action is surprisingly specific. It helps remove excess moisture and fat—places where toxins tend to accumulate. Michael Tierra, in The Way of Herbs, places Gotu kola in a class of antitoxin herbs—especially helpful in cases of infectious or contagious disease. Think sore throats, hepatitis, tonsillitis, even urinary tract infections. It also clears urea from the blood, preventing too much acidity. In other words, it’s not just gently supportive—it’s actively clearing.
Then, there’s its role as an antioxidant. Here, the focus shifts to the brain. Gotu kola increases blood flow to the brain, enriching it with oxygen and nutrients. It supports the circulatory system more broadly, and contains flavonoids—compounds known to prevent oxidative damage. This might help explain its reputation for enhancing mental clarity and protecting neural function. It’s subtle, but profound. Not a burst of energy—more like turning on the lights in a dim room.
But where things really get interesting is in its action on connective tissue. Gotu kola has a notable affinity for these structures—tendons, ligaments, the vasculature. Its astringency plays a key role here. It helps tone and tighten. So when there’s laxity—say, in the sphincter muscle between the esophagus and stomach, leading to GERD—or weakness in blood vessel walls, as with varicose veins or hemorrhoids, Gotu kola steps in. It strengthens. It firms. It restores integrity.
This also explains its topical use. As a vulnerary, Gotu kola supports wound healing. As a cicatrizant, it encourages scar formation. As a venotonic, it boosts collagen production and enhances vascular elasticity. That’s a lot for one humble plant.
But it doesn’t stop there.
Gotu kola is also a nervine tonic—more specifically, a trophorestorative. That’s a fancy way of saying it helps rebuild and restore a worn-down nervous system. It doesn’t sedate. It doesn’t stimulate. It replenishes. In Ayurvedic medicine, it’s considered one of the most sattvic herbs—those that promote clarity, calm, and inner balance. It’s used for stress, insomnia, and nervous exhaustion. It uplifts when the mind is heavy, and grounds when the mind is scattered. This is why it’s classified as a “shen tonic” in Chinese medicine—an herb that supports the spirit.
And finally, we arrive at what may be one of its most talked-about qualities: Gotu kola as a nootropic. That is, a cognitive enhancer. It’s not stimulating like caffeine. And it’s not sedating like valerian. It does something different—it sharpens. It helps with memory, learning, and focus. And again, the mechanism seems to be circulation. Improved blood flow to the brain means more oxygen, more nutrients, more clarity. It’s why Gotu kola is just as appropriate for study as it is for meditation. It doesn’t just clear the mind—it stills it.
Affinities
Herbalists talk a lot about affinities—the natural draw a plant has toward certain tissues, organs, or systems in the body. It’s not about isolated chemistry. It’s about patterns. Relationships. The places where a plant shows up and consistently makes a difference.
Gotu kola has several of these. What’s striking isn’t just the number, but how interconnected they are. This isn’t a single-action herb. It’s a systems herb.
Let’s begin with the circulatory system. One way to describe Gotu kola’s action here is diffusive. It moves. It spreads. It increases blood flow to the body’s periphery and, most notably, to the brain. That circulation boost enhances memory, concentration, and mental clarity—part of why Gotu kola is classed as a nootropic. But there’s another layer. Its astringency tones the veins and blood vessels. So it’s not just about more blood flow—it’s also about better vascular integrity.
In the digestive system, the story continues. Astringency shows up again—this time on the gastric mucosa. That tightening, toning effect helps protect and strengthen. It’s useful in everything from ulcers and diarrhea to leaky gut, hernias, and hemorrhoids. And because it supports the connective tissues, it addresses not just symptoms, but underlying structural weaknesses.
Then there’s the skin—and here, Gotu kola really shines. It promotes wound healing. It stimulates collagen production. It improves blood flow to damaged tissues. That’s what makes it relevant in inflammatory skin conditions like psoriasis, scleroderma, and even leprosy. Its ability to reduce decay, scarring, and septic wounds isn’t just cosmetic—it’s clinical.
The nervous system is perhaps its most famous domain. Gotu kola is a nervine tonic—a rejuvenator. In Eastern traditions, it’s also seen as a gentle sedative. But rather than dulling the mind, it clears it. It has both antioxidant and anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) effects. It’s the kind of herb that steadies the nerves rather than numbing them.
In the muscular and skeletal system, its reach is equally impressive. Matthew Wood describes its use for arthritis, rheumatism, and damaged ligaments and tendons. This makes sense, given Gotu kola’s strong connection to connective tissue—those structures that bind muscle to bone, hold organs in place, and give form and mobility to the entire body. Again, this isn’t isolated action. It’s structural support.
Gotu kola also touches the kidneys and bladder, where its astringent and vulnerary actions help soothe inflamed mucosa. In cases of bleeding or painful UTIs, it lends protection and promotes healing.
And finally, the endocrine system. Here, its influence may be more indirect. Gotu kola has been associated with support for the thyroid—especially in cases of sluggishness, depression, and slow digestion. It’s also considered nourishing to the adrenals, though this may stem from its broader nervine effects. The nervous and endocrine systems are deeply intertwined. Gotu kola understands that.
Energetics
Some herbs are easy to categorize. They’re warming or drying, stimulating or sedating—clear-cut, one-note players. Gotu kola is not one of them.
Let’s start with temperature, moisture, and tone. Gotu kola is considered a cooling herb. That’s immediately apparent in its bitter taste, which is often nature’s way of signaling that a plant will help dispel heat. But there’s more to it. It doesn’t just cool—it moves heat. Specifically, it moves it up and out. So what you get is a circulatory stimulant that, paradoxically, cools the system. In that way, Gotu kola behaves a bit like Reishi—both invigorating and calming, clearing heat while improving flow. It’s a cooling stimulant, a category that feels almost contradictory until you see it in action.
When it comes to moisture, Gotu kola tends to be neutral to lightly drying. Its astringency brings just enough tone to tighten tissues, but not so much that it overwhelms. That’s part of what makes it balancing for all three doshas in Ayurvedic practice. It doesn’t push the body in one direction—it restores equilibrium.
And then there’s its effect on tissue states. Here’s where things get especially interesting.
According to herbalist Matthew Wood, Gotu kola is indicated for three of the most deteriorative tissue states: cold/depressed, damp/relaxed, and dry/atrophied. These are the moments when the body is not just out of balance—it’s losing ground. Systems are slowing. Tissues are losing tone. Function is diminishing.
Take the cold/depressed tissue state. In this context, “depressed” isn’t about emotion—though that can be part of it. It’s about stagnation. Nothing’s moving. Blood flow is reduced, and the tissues begin to break down. Gotu kola’s circulatory action shines here. It stimulates blood flow while simultaneously tightening vascular tone and reducing leakage. This makes it especially helpful in conditions like varicose veins, hemorrhoids, and dysmenorrhea—where congestion results from flaccid, over-relaxed tissue.
Then there’s the damp/relaxed tissue state—where tissues lose tone and structure. Gotu kola helps restore that tone. It brings the tissues back online. And that alterative action—clearing toxins from the blood—promotes better flow through areas of stagnation.
The dry/atrophy state shows up in a different way. Think sluggish thyroid. Dry skin. Low metabolism. Poor nutrient absorption. The body simply isn’t building or repairing the way it should. Gotu kola helps here too—not because it’s rich in polysaccharides, but because of its saponins. These compounds nourish, lubricate, and cleanse. They gently stimulate endocrine function and support the integrity of joints, muscles, and connective tissue.
And then there’s what might be called the wind/tension pattern. Not the kind of wind that causes cramps or spasms, but something more psychological. Mental tightness. Worry. Anxiety. Nervous tension that never quite resolves. Gotu kola doesn’t force relaxation—it guides the system toward steadiness. Groundedness.
There’s a poetic symmetry in the way it works. Gotu kola prefers hot, damp environments. It grows in riverbanks, sometimes even in polluted waters. And yet, that’s precisely the terrain it seems to understand best in the human body—states of excess heat, toxicity, congestion, and stagnation. It meets the body in those conditions not with force, but with familiarity.
It clears what is heavy. It cools what is inflamed. It restores what has begun to collapse.

That leads us to the psychological picture. If you want to understand the kind of person who responds well to Gotu kola, don’t just look at the symptoms. Look at the state they’re in.
This is a remedy for people who are unraveling under pressure. The mind is racing—there’s too much going on, too much to process. Focus is gone. Thoughts are scattered. They’re exhausted but can’t rest. Wired and tired.
But Gotu kola doesn’t just soothe the overstimulated. It also reaches the other end of the spectrum—the stagnant mind. Foggy thinking. Heaviness. A lack of clarity. No movement. No spark. It’s not so much that the mind is spinning too fast, it’s that it’s barely moving at all.
And here’s what’s remarkable: Gotu kola helps with both.
It calms the scattered mind through its nervine and trophorestorative effects. It brings coherence to a system that’s overwhelmed. And it wakes the stagnant mind through its circulatory and alterative actions. It clears out what’s stuck and helps restore flow—mentally, emotionally, and physically.
There’s even a way to think about this energetically. As if Gotu kola acts like an astringent for the mind itself—tightening the loose ends, drawing things back into focus.
So when you’re looking for the Gotu kola person, you’re not just looking for anxiety or depression or fatigue. You’re looking for a deeper story—one of fragmentation, stagnation, and the slow wear of stress over time. And when you find it, Gotu kola offers something rare: the possibility of restoration, not just relief.
Clinical Patterns
1.Skin Regeneration: We tend to think of skin care in terms of the surface—what we can see, feel, moisturize, or treat with a salve. But Gotu kola operates on a much deeper level. Its influence starts at the skin, yes—but it doesn’t stop there.
This herb has long been used for everything from eczema and psoriasis to wounds, leprosy, and even general dryness. It’s one of those plants that seems to understand the skin—both its vulnerabilities and its remarkable capacity to regenerate. And regeneration is key here. Gotu kola doesn’t just soothe or protect. It rebuilds.
The mechanism? Much of it centers around collagen synthesis and vascular repair. These are deep processes—ones that affect not just the skin’s appearance, but its function. Blood vessels are strengthened. Wound healing is accelerated. Scars fade.
At the center of this is a class of compounds called flavonoids. These act as circulatory stimulants, improving blood flow to the skin and encouraging the development of healthy blood vessels. They also enhance keratinization—a process that helps reinforce the outer protective layer of the skin. This becomes especially useful in cases of chronic inflammation or conditions like cellulite, where skin structure begins to break down.
There’s clinical evidence to back this up.
In one study, researchers examined a total triterpenic fraction—an extract composed of asiatic acid, madecassic acid, and asiaticoside, all derived from Centella asiatica. The results? A significant increase in fibronectin and collagen production in scar tissue. But it wasn’t just about boosting growth. The extract also reduced inflammation and myofibroblast activity, which, if left unchecked, can cause excessive tension and lead to hypertrophic scars—raised, rigid, and often long-lasting. Gotu kola helped avoid that. Healing occurred with minimal scarring.
And it doesn’t stop with wounds.
These same properties make Gotu kola an increasingly popular ingredient in anti-aging and cosmetic products. As we age, type I collagen in the skin declines—leading to loss of firmness and elasticity. Gotu kola helps counter that decline, promoting tensile strength in newly formed skin, reducing the visible effects of photoaging, and improving skin’s resilience.
One study looked at 20 women between 45 and 60 years old, all with photoaged skin. They applied a topical blend containing 0.1% madecassoside and 5% vitamin C for six months. The outcome? Noticeable improvements in firmness, elasticity, and hydration—validated not just by appearance, but by biometrological testing.
What’s especially compelling is how these compounds work together. Madecassoside stimulates collagen production by activating the SMAD signaling pathway. Vitamin C, on the other hand, supports collagen synthesis while inhibiting the enzymes that break collagen down—enzymes that are often elevated in photoaged skin. The researchers concluded that the two compounds—working through different mechanisms—had a synergistic effect, helping to remodel the superficial dermis.
In other words, this isn’t superficial skincare. It’s structural repair. And it’s happening quietly, cell by cell, layer by layer, beneath the surface.
When you trace the story of Gotu kola through the centuries, one theme keeps surfacing: healing. Not just easing symptoms or covering damage, but actual healing—of wounds, of skin, of deep tissue structures that too often resist repair.
It makes sense, then, that wound healing is one of Gotu kola’s most celebrated roles. In one clinical trial, wounds treated with Centella asiatica extract (CAE) not only epithelialized faster—meaning the skin closed more quickly—but also contracted more efficiently. The triterpenes in the extract did more than accelerate healing; they also strengthened the new skin, setting the stage for deeper, longer-term recovery.
There’s a natural assumption that antioxidants play a central role here. And they do—at least at first. Gotu kola has been shown to increase antioxidant levels, which can certainly help during the early stages of wound repair. But there’s a catch. In one study, continued application didn’t maintain that same antioxidant boost over time. The effect plateaued. It’s a subtle reminder: herbs don’t work in a straight line. Their effects shift with context, timing, and need.
Still, the overall picture remains: Gotu kola enhances recovery—especially when it comes to the skin.
And this is nothing new.
In fact, you can find this idea embedded in 19th-century American medical literature. The 1898 edition of Felter and Lloyd's King’s American Dispensatory includes a story that predates most modern herbal research by decades. It’s the account of Dr. Boileau, a physician in India who had long suffered from leprosy. In 1852, while waiting for a remedy called Chinchunchulli, he began experimenting with Hydrocotyle asiatica—the older synonym for Gotu kola. He recovered. He went on to treat others with the same plant. His findings were later echoed by Jules Lepine in the Madras Gazette, who confirmed the plant’s efficacy in treating leprosy. Long before peer-reviewed journals and placebo-controlled trials, there was already evidence—and confidence—in the plant’s healing power.
Of course, modern practitioners are unlikely to encounter leprosy. But the point remains: this is not a surface-level herb. It’s used for minor burns, flesh wounds, scarring, and photoaging, yes. But it’s also been entrusted, historically, with some of the most difficult and degenerative skin conditions known to medicine.
Gotu kola operates on multiple layers. It helps build collagen, tighten tissue, improve circulation, and modulate inflammation. It’s not just about repair—it’s about how that repair happens. The difference between healing and scarring. Between sealing a wound and actually restoring the integrity of the skin.
And that’s what makes it remarkable.
2. Digestive Relaxation and Inflammatory Conditions: In herbal medicine, it’s easy to draw a sharp line between what happens on the surface and what happens deep inside the body. But some plants blur that line entirely. Gotu kola is one of them.
We’ve seen its capacity to heal wounds, to close the skin, to restore damaged tissue and reduce inflammation. Now take that same action—and move it inward. Into the gut. Into the inflamed, ulcerated, often-neglected tissues of the digestive tract.
Gotu kola has traditionally been used in Ayurveda for conditions like gastric ulceration, leaky gut, heartburn, and inflammatory bowel diseases such as ulcerative colitis and Crohn’s disease. It’s even relevant for the mouth—gingivitis and periodontal disease—and all the way down to hemorrhoids. It works across the full length of the digestive system.
What links these conditions together is a particular kind of tissue state: relaxed, inflamed, and damaged. And here, Gotu kola’s dual nature comes into play. It’s a tonic astringent—which means it tones loose, weakened tissue—and a vulnerary, which means it heals.
In other words, the way Gotu kola supports external wound healing? The same logic applies to internal wounds. Peptic ulcers, in particular, benefit from this action. Especially when stress is involved.
There’s some speculation that its effectiveness here stems from its nervine tonic properties. But the evidence suggests it’s more than that. Gotu kola appears to increase gastric mucosal secretions, which directly protect the lining of the stomach. At the same time, it reduces local inflammation and strengthens connective tissue—a combination that restores both structure and function.
The research backs this up.
In animal models, researchers used alcohol, NSAIDs, and physical stress to induce ulcers. Gotu kola—whether administered as fresh juice or oral extract—showed clear ulcer-protective effects, along with increased angiogenesis. That’s the formation of new blood vessels, which accelerates healing by delivering oxygen and nutrients to the damaged tissue.
The World Health Organization cites three Korean studies that explored Gotu kola’s effect on gastric and duodenal ulcers in humans. In one trial, 15 patients with peptic or duodenal ulcers were given a daily oral extract. The results were notable: 73% of ulcers healed, and 93% showed symptom improvement.
Other studies offer further insight. The compound asiaticoside, found in Gotu kola, has been shown to reduce inflammation by inhibiting nitric oxide (NO)—a key player in inflammatory damage. And in cold-induced ulcer models, Gotu kola demonstrated protective effects through GABA modulation, suggesting not just a digestive remedy, but a neurological one as well.
And that’s the thing: Gotu kola doesn’t work on just one system. It improves digestion by calming the nervous system. It helps nerves by strengthening the gut. It blurs the boundaries between categories. Everything is connected.
This makes it especially promising for conditions like leaky gut, diverticulitis, herniation, and even ulcerative colitis. But there’s one important consideration: delivery matters. For these kinds of tissue-level effects, the remedy needs direct contact. That means infusions or powders, not tinctures. Alcohol-based extracts simply don’t linger long enough in the gut to have the desired effect.
So once again, we return to the idea of Gotu kola as a quiet restorer—one that works where things have broken down, not just to ease discomfort, but to rebuild what’s been lost. From the skin to the stomach, its actions are remarkably consistent. And that consistency is where its power lies.
3. Nervous System Disorders: There are moments in herbalism when a plant goes from helpful to essential. For me—and for many practitioners—that moment often happens with Gotu kola and the nervous system.
It begins with a simple observation: stress doesn't just live in the mind. It moves into the body—into the joints, the skin, the immune system. People walk into a clinic complaining of eczema, psoriasis, or even acne, and underneath it all is a current of nervous tension—a system stuck in overdrive.
Gotu kola meets that tension directly.
At its core, Gotu kola is a nerve tonic. But it’s not simply calming. It’s restorative. It helps the nervous system rebound—not just from acute stress, but from prolonged strain: insomnia, burnout, even the long tail of substance use. Its action is gentle but comprehensive. And part of that has to do with GABA.
GABA—gamma-aminobutyric acid—is a neurotransmitter in the central nervous system. Its job is inhibition. It blocks excess nerve activity, creating space for rest, quiet, and focus. Gotu kola appears to elevate GABA levels. That makes it useful in a wide spectrum of conditions: anxiety, mood disorders, epilepsy, chronic pain, and even sexual dysfunction. The herb’s GABA-mimetic effect makes it a natural CNS (Central Nervous System) depressant—not in the sense of dulling the system, but in the sense of softening overstimulation.
There’s data to support this.
A 2005 study explored Gotu kola’s effect on damaged nerve tissue. The results were striking: Gotu kola facilitated axon regrowth and remyelination—two critical processes in nervous system repair. Even more intriguing: the ethanol extract (a tincture) outperformed water-based preparations. The tincture produced higher functional recovery and faster axonal regeneration compared to controls.
That kind of regeneration isn’t just clinical—it’s symbolic. Gotu kola gives back what’s been worn down. It nourishes. It rebuilds.
Which is why this herb becomes so important in conditions of long-term depletion. Think: sleep debt, chronic stress, emotional exhaustion. Think: multiple sclerosis, Lyme disease, Alzheimer’s, dementia. In all of these, the nervous system is under siege—and Gotu kola brings relief, not just symptomatically, but structurally. It contains B vitamins, which support nerve health directly. And its connective tissue tonic properties—so central to its action on the skin and vasculature—also play a role in nerve sheath integrity.
So yes, it calms. But more than that, Gotu kola restores capacity. It doesn’t just put the brakes on. It helps you rebuild the system that lost control in the first place.
4. Impaired Mental Performance: There’s a point at which we stop talking about stress and start talking about erosion. When the brain has been pushed too far—by trauma, degeneration, or the slow wear of modern life—something deeper begins to change. Focus dulls. Memory slips. The ability to hold a thought, to stay with it, falters.
This is where Gotu kola steps in—not just as a nervine, not just as a tonic, but as a rejuvenator of cerebral function.
It’s known for increasing mental performance. More specifically: memory, learning, and recall. But the how is just as interesting as the what. Gotu kola doesn’t push the brain harder. It restores what’s missing—chiefly, blood flow and protection from oxidative stress.
There’s evidence for this.
In one study, researchers induced cognitive impairment in rats using streptozotocin (STZ)—a compound injected into the brain to simulate the kind of damage associated with sporadic Alzheimer’s disease. What they found was telling. Rats treated with Centella asiatica (Gotu kola) extract showed dose-dependent cognitive improvements. They performed better in behavioral tests measuring memory and learning. And biochemically, the treatment reduced oxidative stress, lowering malondialdehyde (MDA) while increasing glutathione and catalase—two critical antioxidants.
This matters. Because in Alzheimer’s, in age-related decline, and in many stress-related cognitive impairments, the common thread is oxidative damage. Free radicals overwhelm the brain’s ability to defend itself. Gotu kola helps restore that balance.
There’s more.
The herb is also remarkably safe, even for children. Herbalists have used it with success for kids who struggle with focus, attention, and what modern labels often call ADD or ADHD. But here’s the thing: not everyone agrees those are diseases. From another perspective, they’re just temperamental variations—ways of being that don’t fit neatly into an educational system built around stillness and rationality. And in those cases, Gotu kola offers something gentle but tangible: a calmer mind, a clearer focus. It helps children stay with a thought—long enough, at least, to engage with the world around them on their own terms.
And then, there’s meditation.
In Ayurveda, Gotu kola is revered for this. It enhances mental clarity and cerebral circulation, while simultaneously calming the nervous system. Not sedation. Not stimulation. Something else. A state of attentive stillness. The mind becomes clear—but not sharp in the cutting sense. More like a lens brought into focus. There’s a quiet intensity to it, a feeling that the mind is no longer in conflict with itself.
That’s rare.
Most herbs either calm or sharpen. Gotu kola does both. It focuses without narrowing. It relaxes without dulling. In that, it creates the ideal conditions—not just for productivity, but for presence. For the kind of quiet mental coherence that so often feels out of reach in a noisy world.
5. Connective Tissue and Cardiovascular Insufficiency: When we talk about circulation, we tend to picture blood flow—arteries, veins, heartbeats, pressure. But there’s a second story running alongside that one, and it’s made of connective tissue.
Gotu kola sits right at the intersection of these two systems. It’s not just a circulatory stimulant. It’s a structural healer, working as much on the vessels themselves as on the tissue that supports and surrounds them. That’s what makes it so effective in conditions like varicose veins, hypertensive microangiopathy, and even scleroderma—a rare disease involving the hardening and tightening of connective tissue.
What’s striking is how these two systems—circulation and connective tissue—don’t just work in parallel. They shape one another. The state of the blood vessels is directly linked to the integrity of the connective tissue. One influences the other. And Gotu kola influences both.
Studies show that Centella asiatica helps stabilize tissue growth, strengthen veins, and preserve connective tissue integrity. In scleroderma, for example, Gotu kola was found to reduce overgrowth by stimulating the production of hyaluronidase and chondroitin sulfate—two agents that help regulate the formation and remodeling of connective tissues.
And then there’s the vascular wall itself.
Gotu kola improves microcirculatory parameters, decreasing capillary filtration rate, and making blood vessels more resilient. This is especially useful in venous insufficiency, where weak vessel walls contribute to stagnation, edema, and pressure buildup. Its action on the small blood vessels, in particular, makes it effective in treating hypertensive microangiopathy—a condition marked by high blood pressure in the tiniest vessels.
One study looked at vascular function during air travel, a context where small-vessel issues and swelling often flare. Gotu kola helped stabilize microcirculatory changes and reduce edema. That’s not just relief—it’s prevention.
The benefits ripple outward.
By healing the endothelial lining of the vasculature, Gotu kola also contributes to the stabilization of carotid plaques—those hypoechoic, low-density formations associated with thrombosis and atherosclerosis. It improves microcirculation in the retina, the kidneys, and the nervous system—all places where vascular fragility can lead to chronic damage.
It’s a compelling combination: a plant that increases blood flow, tonifies weakened tissue, reduces oxidative damage, and calms the nervous system—all at once.
That’s why it’s so well-suited to conditions involving connective tissue weakness: hernias, varicosities, organ prolapse, ulcerations, joint pain. And why its cardiovascular benefits extend beyond just blood pressure or flow. Gotu kola also helps with stress-related hypertension through its nervine sedative effect—addressing not only the mechanics of pressure, but the emotional weight that drives it.
There’s even a note—small, but intriguing—from David Bruce Leonard, who writes that Gotu kola has been used for impotence when vascular disease is the root cause. And it makes sense. Better circulation. Stronger vessels. Calmer nerves. It’s all connected. Libido, like so many other things, depends on flow.
So, what are we seeing here?
Not a miracle. Not a cure-all. But something better—a pattern. Gotu kola works where things have slackened, thinned, broken down. It restores tone, encourages flow, and rebuilds balance. Whether it’s the vasculature, the nerves, or the connective tissue holding it all together, Gotu kola offers a quiet, persistent kind of strength. The kind that makes the body feel whole again.
Pharmacological Data
If you want to understand why Gotu kola does what it does, you need to look beneath the surface—into the chemistry. That’s where the real story unfolds.
At the center of Gotu kola’s activity are its triterpenes. These are complex plant compounds with long, carbon-heavy structures—thirty-carbon chains, arranged in pentacyclic forms. That detail might seem technical, but it matters. Because it’s these very structures—specifically asiatic acid, madecassic acid, and their glycoside forms, asiaticoside and madecassoside—that drive the herb’s most important effects.
These triterpenes are responsible for wound healing, anti-inflammatory action, anti-ulcer effects, and even CNS depression—the calming effect on the central nervous system. They’re also the most studied components of Centella asiatica, with both isolated compounds and whole plant extracts showing consistent bioactivity in lab and animal models.
This is the part of herbalism that often gets overlooked—not the tradition, not the folklore, but the mechanism. The how. And in Gotu kola, the how is triterpenes.
But there’s more.
The plant also contains a mix of essential oils, tannins, flavonoids (including quercetin and flavanols), bitters, hydrocotylin, brahmic acid, brahminoside, phytosterols like stigmasterol, and even polyacetylenes. Some of these, like the flavonoids, play a critical role in protecting the brain—counteracting neurotoxicity and oxidative stress, as shown in studies by Ponnusamy (2008) and Hussin (2009). It’s a broad spectrum of action, aimed at deep-seated dysfunction.
And yet, the triterpenes remain central.
These saponins aren’t just potent—they’re also versatile. They appear throughout the plant, though may be concentrated in the roots or foliage. What makes them especially intriguing is their ability to form complexes with cholesterol. That could suggest a potential for cholesterol-lowering effects, though this area is still under investigation.
There’s also a pharmacokinetic piece to the puzzle.
A study by Grimaldi et al. examined what happens after asiaticoside is ingested. It turns out the body doesn’t absorb it as-is. Instead, it gets converted in the digestive system into asiatic acid—a more bioavailable form. Blood plasma levels of asiatic acid peaked around five hours after administration at a dose of 30 or 60 mg in healthy volunteers. In other words, Gotu kola takes time. But once absorbed, it gets to work.
And there's something else: because saponins are fat-soluble, part of them are absorbed in the small intestine, while the rest continue to the large intestine, where they’re transformed by the gut microbiome into aglycones—smaller, absorbable forms that enter the bloodstream. This dual-pathway mechanism speaks to the plant’s depth of interaction with the body—not just what it contains, but how it's broken down, transformed, and ultimately used.
So when we talk about Gotu kola’s broad reach—across the nervous system, skin, vasculature, connective tissue—this is the engine behind it. Not a single compound, not a simple trigger, but a constellation of actions that unfold over time, across systems, through conversion and circulation.
That’s how this quiet little herb manages to be so powerful. It’s not just what it contains. It’s how those constituents behave—how they move, change, and restore.
Safety
With any plant that has the kind of systemic reach that Gotu kola does, a conversation about safety is not just responsible—it’s necessary. The deeper a remedy works in the body, the more attention we owe to how and when it’s used.
Start with the contraindications. Gotu kola is not recommended during pregnancy. That’s due to its reported emmenagogue and abortifacient effects—properties that stimulate uterine activity and could, in theory, lead to miscarriage. It’s also generally advised against during lactation, likely because of its astringency, which might affect milk production.
There’s another layer here, too—interactions with sedatives, hypnotics, or antihypertensives. Because of Gotu kola’s natural CNS depressant effects, combining it with similar drugs or herbs without professional guidance is discouraged. There’s a potential for amplification—effects that are too strong, too fast, or simply unpredictable.
And then there’s skin sensitivity. While not common, Gotu kola may contain sesquiterpenoid lactones—compounds known to cause contact dermatitis, especially in individuals sensitive to the Asteraceae family. Think dandelions, ragweed, chamomile. It’s not a deal-breaker, but it’s something to watch for.
A more unusual note comes from a 2014 case study, where a young teenager reportedly developed liver damage after taking a Gotu kola–based supplement for six weeks. It’s worth pointing out: the brand was unknown, and there’s no confirmation of adulteration. It’s entirely possible that other ingredients in the formula—not Gotu kola itself—were to blame. But even so, it reminds us of the complexity of herbal formulas. When something goes wrong, the plant isn’t always the problem—but it will always be questioned.
From an energetic perspective, Gotu kola is considered tridoshic—well-balanced in most constitutional systems. Still, in large doses, some people may experience a “vata” headache—light, dizzy, and slightly scattered. It’s a subtle side effect, but worth noting for anyone with a constitution prone to dryness or overstimulation. The plant’s mildly drying nature can also interact with environmental or pathological conditions, especially in colder, windier climates.
Then there’s the issue of herb-drug interactions.
One area of concern is Gotu kola’s potential to inhibit liver enzymes involved in metabolizing barbiturates. This suggests caution when used alongside benzodiazepines or other sedative drugs. It may intensify or prolong their effects.
Gotu kola also shows promise in lowering blood sugar and cholesterol—but that promise comes with a warning. If someone’s already on hypoglycemic or cholesterol-lowering medication, adding Gotu kola could compound the effect, unintentionally driving levels too low.
And then there’s the NSAID connection.
Common over-the-counter drugs—Aspirin, Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Celebrex—fall into the ulcerogenic category. They can damage the gastric mucosa, especially with long-term use. Gotu kola, interestingly, may counteract this effect by increasing mucosal secretions in the stomach. Some WHO-cited studies suggest this could help prevent ulcer formation. But even so, more research is needed before replacing well-established agents like deglycyrrhizinated licorice or aloe.
One final note: blood thinners. There isn’t a lot of data on this yet, but caution is advised. Gotu kola's influence on circulation could, theoretically, interfere with anticoagulant medications—though again, conclusive evidence is limited.
So what’s the takeaway?
Gotu kola is broadly safe, remarkably gentle, and deeply nourishing. But like any potent herb, it operates in systems—nervous, vascular, digestive—that are already tightly regulated by modern pharmaceuticals. Respecting those intersections isn’t about fear. It’s about precision. And precision is what makes an herbal remedy effective—not just powerful.
Doses and Preparation
There’s something almost poetic about Gotu kola’s favorite habitat—wet, low places, often along polluted drainage ditches in India. It thrives in difficult environments. But that very resilience raises an important issue: quality control.
When a plant soaks up everything from its surroundings—water, minerals, toxins—the source matters. Especially with Gotu kola, sourcing from clean, organic environments is not just ideal, it’s essential. Fresh Gotu kola from Hawaii, organically grown, is one of the best options we’ve found.
That said, dried herb still works. It always has. Ayurveda has used dried Gotu kola for centuries. So while fresh may offer vibrancy and subtlety, don’t underestimate the power of dried leaves—especially when prepared correctly.
Let’s talk preparation.
Infusion
This isn’t one of those herbs you steep a teaspoon of in a mug and sip casually. Traditionally, large quantities are used. Matthew Wood recommends an infusion using 30-90g of the aerial parts—the leaves. Boil your water, then let it cool slightly before pouring over the plant material. That preserves the volatile and delicate constituents. 30mg per dose is not unusual in Ayurvedic practice—and it’s at that level you really start to notice the deeper effects.
Medicated Oil
Michael Tierra offers a classic Ayurvedic formula for medicated oil, adaptable to your skin type. Coconut oil for a cooling effect, sesame if you need warmth. The basic ratio:
1 part herb
16 parts water
4 parts oil
Decoct gently until all the water is gone. For example, 60g of herbs, 4 cups water, 1 cup oil yields 1 cup of medicated oil. But overheating can weaken some herbs—so an alternative is to macerate powdered herb directly into the oil, let it stand a few days, then strain. This oil can be used for wound care, massage, or even scalp application.
In fact, Brahmi oil—traditionally used for mental clarity and nervous system support—is made with half Gotu kola, half Calamus (Acorus calamus). Apply it liberally to the scalp, leave it for an hour, then wash it off. In Ayurvedic practice, this oil can also be used as a nasya—medicated oil administered through the nose, targeting the mind and nervous system.
Medicated Ghee
Preparation follows the same method as oil, but uses ghee instead of oil. Once ready, the dose is one teaspoon, taken in water or with food. Used a couple of times per day, this medicated ghee has a long-standing reputation for supporting memory.
Tincture
Start with 12–30 drops, taken in the morning and mid-afternoon. That’s a good baseline, but higher doses are often well tolerated. Interestingly, while Gotu kola may feel like a sedative, some people report restlessness if taken too late in the evening—likely due to its action on cerebral circulation.
As for preparation, the triterpene saponins—those crucial active compounds—are more soluble in alcohol than water. That means tinctures are especially effective if made with a high enough proof:
60–70% alcohol for fresh herb
40–50% for dried
Ideal ratio: 1:2, though with Gotu kola’s light bulk, you may end up with 1:5
The higher the proof, the better the extraction.
For Trauma
In more acute situations—physical trauma, stress recovery—David Bruce Leonard suggests:
1.5 grams dried herb, three times a day
Or 1–3 fresh leaves daily
That’s it. Simple, direct, grounded in tradition and practice.
Formulation
Sometimes, the best way to understand an herb isn’t by looking at it in isolation, but by seeing how it behaves in a community—a formula. The company it keeps tells us a great deal about its purpose, its strengths, and how it collaborates with other remedies to bring about change.
Let’s begin with the Ulcer Formula.
20% Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica)
20% Calendula (Calendula officinalis)
20% Plantain (Plantago major)
15% Chamomile (Matricaria recutita)
15% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)
10% Meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria)
Marshmallow (Althea officinalis), steeped cold
This formula is an elegant solution to an internal wound. Each plant plays a role. Gotu kola, Calendula, and Plantain bring their vulnerary, tissue-repairing qualities to the gastric lining. Chamomile, Yarrow, and Meadowsweet work to calm inflammation, reduce spasms, and soothe nerves. And Marshmallow, steeped cold, offers demulcency—coating the raw edges with moist protection.
Notably, there’s a choice point here. Some sources suggest that Gotu kola’s properties may degrade with heat, prompting the suggestion to add it with the Marshmallow, as part of the cold steep. It’s a reminder that even in traditional medicine, how you prepare a plant matters just as much as what you’re using it for.
Then there’s the Circulatory Tonic, from Sharol Tilgner:
25% Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba)
25% Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.)
25% Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica)
15% Burdock (Arctium lappa)
10% Ginger (Zingiber officinale)
This is not a flashy formula. It’s sturdy. Purposeful. Ginkgo and Gotu kola increase cerebral and peripheral circulation, strengthen blood vessels, and offer antioxidant support. Hawthorn builds and stabilizes the heart itself. Burdock cleans the blood and supports liver detoxification, while Ginger drives the formula, moving blood, warming the core, and improving absorption.
It’s a circulatory formula that doesn’t just get things moving—it strengthens the system doing the moving. And Gotu kola’s place here is unmistakable: stabilizer, vessel-toner, bridge between blood and nerve.
Next, the Stress Formula with Anxiety:
20% Ashwaganda (Withania somnifera)
20% Shatavari (Asparagus racemosus)
20% Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica)
20% Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora)
20% Milky Oats (Avena sativa)
This is a trophorestorative dream team—five herbs with deep affinity for a worn-out nervous system. The trio of Ashwaganda, Shatavari, and Gotu kola reflects classic Ayurvedic practice—used together to rebuild the neuroendocrine axis after prolonged stress, exhaustion, or emotional burnout.
Skullcap and Milky Oats bring the Western nervine approach: calming, nourishing, repairing. Energetically, the formula is balanced. Ashwaganda brings warmth. The others cool gently. Shatavari and Milky Oats also help soften the potentially drying effects of Gotu kola and Skullcap.
It’s best taken as a tincture, where synergy emerges not just from the herbs themselves, but from the medium that delivers them—fast, absorbable, subtle.
Continnue with a formula Paul Bergner calls the Spiritual Heart Formula, or the Exhilarant Formula:
25% Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica)
25% Holy Basil (Ocimum sanctum)
25% Coriander (Coriandrum sativum)
12.5% Cinnamon (Cinnamomum cassia)
12.5% Sweet Violet (Viola odorata)
This is not about treating disease in the conventional sense. This is about gladdening the vital heart—that subtle space where emotion and energy meet. Gotu kola and Holy Basil are central here. Both are nervine sedatives, yes, but that’s not quite the whole picture. They don’t just calm—they uplift. They clear the fog, sharpen the senses, and support what Chinese medicine would call the shen, or spirit.
Coriander and Cinnamon contribute flavor—but also circulation and digestive warmth. And Violet? Violet brings the softness. It soothes the heart tissue, physically and emotionally. It adds moisture, sweetness, and balance to what might otherwise be too dry or too sharp. Together, they create something greater than the sum of their parts—a formula not just for relief, but for restoration of joy.
Now consider something more grounded: Vata Hypertension, from David Frawley.
33% Ashwaganda (Withania somnifera)
33% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)
33% Gotu kola (Centella asiatica)
This is for a pattern that’s deeply constitutional—blood pressure that rises and drops, mind racing with worry, overwork, anxiety, and insomnia. It’s the picture of Vata in excess: light, ungrounded, overstimulated. The strategy here is tonification.
Ashwaganda replenishes and rebuilds. Valerian warms, circulates, and sedates. Gotu kola, once again, helps focus awareness, calm the nervous system, and refine perception. The delivery is simple: 1–3 grams of powdered herb, taken with warm water or ghee. And yet the action is profound—nourishing the nerves while calming the pulse.
And finally the Connective Tissue Restoration Formula:
20% Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica)
20% Solomon’s Seal (Polygonum multiflorum)
20% Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)
20% Horsetail (Equisetum arvense)
10% Turmeric (Curcuma longa)
10% Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra)
This is about structure. Joints, ligaments, fascia—the scaffolding that holds the body together. Gotu kola shines here not just as a circulatory stimulant, but as a connective tissue tonic. It’s paired with Solomon’s Seal, Comfrey, and Horsetail—all known for restoring fluidity, integrity, and mineral density to tissue that’s been worn thin.
Turmeric brings inflammation down and delivers the formula, keeping blood moving. Licorice, with its soothing demulcency, buffers heat and further reduces irritation.
