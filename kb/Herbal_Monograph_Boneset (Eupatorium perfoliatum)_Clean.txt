Boneset (Eupatorium perfoliatum)
In the off chance that you’ve ever wandered through the low, wet meadows of North America in late summer, you may have brushed against a plant that, at first glance, looks like the stem has pierced clean through its own leaves. That’s Boneset—or Eupatorium perfoliatum—a plant whose structure is just as curious as its history.
Its leaves are opposite, lance-shaped, and joined at the base, forming what looks like a sleeve around the stem. This detail—the way the stem seems to pass directly through the leaf—is more than a botanical quirk. It’s one of the reasons why the plant picked up so many evocative names over the centuries: Thoroughwort, Thorough-stem, Cross-wort, Indian Sage, Ague Weed, Herba Eupatorii Perfoliati. Even its folk names in French and German—Herbe parfaite, Berbe à fièvre, Durchwachsener Wasserhanf—hint at its long-held reputation as a remedy of near perfection for fever and flu.
Boneset belongs to the sunflower family, Asteraceae, though its appearance is a little less cheerful than the flower it’s grouped with. It’s a perennial herb, growing anywhere from one to five feet tall, with stems that are notably hairy and branch out at the top into clusters of small, pale flowers. From July to September, these flowers bloom in cymose paniculate inflorescences—a technical way of saying that they open in a kind of spray at the top, a sign that harvest time has arrived. But wait too long, and you’ll find its seeds everywhere during drying. The trick is to catch it just as it begins to flower.
What’s especially compelling about Boneset isn’t just its structure or its flowers, but how widely—and how effectively—it’s been used. The indigenous peoples of the northeastern United States were the first to recognize its value. The Menominee turned to it for fever, the Creek for general body pain. The Iroquois and Mohegans used it to treat colds and chills. And in the case of breakbone fever—now known as dengue—Boneset earned its name through its action: it helped the body sweat, break the fever, and ease the wrenching pain in the bones.
By the time European settlers had arrived, Boneset was already a cornerstone of traditional medicine. It entered the U.S. Pharmacopoeia in 1820, stayed there until 1916, and remained in the National Formulary until 1950. That’s a long tenure for an herbal remedy—and a mark of the trust it had earned.
Boneset grows in the kind of places people tend to overlook: low ground, damp meadows, soggy stretches along streams and swamps. It stretches across a wide swath of North America—from Nova Scotia to Florida, west to Texas and the Dakotas. It’s a plant that thrives on the margins. And yet, in the hands of herbalists—Thomsonians, Eclectics, Physiomedicalists—it stood front and center.
It’s a reminder that healing often comes not from what stands tall, but from what quietly endures.
The 5 Pillars of Boneset
Taste
Let’s start with a simple truth: Boneset tastes awful.
Not in a mild, tolerable way. Not in the way that makes you wrinkle your nose but push through. No—Boneset is, quite possibly, one of the most bitter plants you’ll ever meet. Even seasoned herbalists—the ones who make a hobby of chewing on pungent roots and bark—have been known to wince at its flavor.
It shares this distinction with a handful of other plants infamous in the herbal world for their intense bitterness: Blue Vervain (Verbena hastata), Gentian (Gentiana lutea), and Motherwort (Leonurus cardiaca). These are plants that don’t hide their medicinal intent. They announce it. Loudly.
But bitterness in herbal medicine isn’t just a sensory experience—it’s a signpost. And in the case of Boneset, that overwhelming bitter taste tells us quite a lot.
Bitter plants, traditionally, have a downward pull. They help move things out—fluids, heat, stagnation. They’re draining, in the most therapeutic sense of the word. They encourage the body to let go, to release what’s no longer needed, and to restore balance through movement rather than force.
There’s more. Beneath the bitterness, if you pay close attention, there’s a hint of something else. Boneset is also slightly astringent—tightening tissues, drying dampness. It’s pungent and even a little acrid. That sharp edge at the back of your throat? That’s the acrid quality. It’s not just a taste, it’s a sensation. A signal.
In herbal energetics, acrid plants are almost always associated with relaxation. They calm spasms. Loosen what’s tight. And if you catch the scent—subtle though it may be—you’ll notice it’s faintly aromatic. Not enough to soften the bitterness, but just enough to let you know there’s more going on here than a one-note punch to the tongue.
In short, Boneset tells you exactly what it is the moment you taste it. And if you can get past that first shock of bitterness, you’ll start to understand why that matters.
Primary Actions
Relaxant Diaphoretic: During a fever, something subtle but important happens just beneath the skin.
The pores tighten. The capillaries narrow. And the body, in a desperate attempt to keep heat in, creates a kind of internal lockdown. It’s not just that you're hot—it’s that your body refuses to let the heat out. You feel tense, flushed, irritable. Your skin feels closed off. And this is precisely where Boneset steps in.
In herbal medicine, Boneset is known as a relaxant diaphoretic. That’s a complicated way of saying it helps you sweat—but it does so by unlocking the doors your body has slammed shut. It eases neuromuscular tension, particularly in the vasculature, allowing blood to return to the surface and heat to exit through the skin. In Chinese medicine, this type of herb would be called a “surface relieving agent.” The term feels just right.
The most effective moment for a relaxant diaphoretic isn’t at the very beginning of a fever. It’s in the later stage—when the person is already red-faced, restless, and burning up. Not just physically, but emotionally too. Boneset is for the person who’s not just feverish, but frustrated. Not just aching, but angry. It helps the heat move. And in doing so, helps the whole person soften.
Spasmolytic: And then there’s another layer.
Boneset’s ability to ease tension doesn’t stop at the skin. It moves deeper, into the muscles. The same action that opens the pores also calms the body’s larger, more stubborn spasms. Which is why Boneset isn’t just a relaxant diaphoretic—it’s also a spasmolytic. That is, it helps release spasms and cramping.
But here’s the nuance: unlike other classic antispasmodics—Crampbark, Lobelia, Black Cohosh—Boneset isn’t something you’d reach for during a random muscle cramp or a sudden tension headache. Its specialty is fever. Specifically, the muscle aches that accompany a high fever—the kind of deep, pulling, bone-aching pain that gave rise to the old nickname breakbone fever.
In this context, Boneset is unmatched. It releases the grip—not just in the pores, not just in the vessels, but in the very muscles themselves.
Bitter Tonic: Then we return to that bitterness.
Boneset, as we’ve already seen, is bitter enough to make even the most seasoned herbalist pause. And in the world of herbal medicine, that level of bitterness puts it in a very specific category: nauseating bitters.
These are herbs so bitter, so profoundly unpalatable, that they can induce nausea—or even vomiting—if taken in large enough doses. Boneset doesn’t always go that far, mostly because it’s so hard to take enough of it in the first place. But even in small amounts, its bitterness gets to work.
It stimulates digestion. It wakes up the liver. It nudges the parasympathetic nervous system—the one responsible for rest and digestion—back into rhythm. And for those who’ve never quite felt the same after a long, burning fever, Boneset can be something of a reset button.
That same bitter action even travels beyond the gut. It reaches the upper respiratory tract, nudging mucous out of the sinuses, the throat, even the bronchioles. It’s as if the plant’s bitterness isn’t just about flavor—it’s about motion. Pushing things out. Helping the body let go.
Emetic
Sometimes, the medicine works by getting things out.
That was the belief of the Physiomedicalist doctors in the late 1800s. Faced with the chaos of a rising fever, they didn’t just sit and wait—they acted. And one of their first moves was often to induce vomiting.
The idea was simple, if a bit startling by today’s standards: empty the stomach, cleanse the system, and the fever might resolve itself more quickly. For this, they turned to emetics—plants that cause the body to purge. And among them, Boneset held a special place.
Drink a strong Boneset tea, and you’ll understand this action firsthand. It doesn’t take much. Too strong a brew and the body pushes back—hard. Which is why, even today, herbalists tend to recommend gentler preparations if you’re aiming for support rather than purgation.
There’s a curious pattern here. Lobelia inflata, another well-known emetic, also happens to be a go-to herb for fevers. It raises a question: is there something about the act of releasing—whether through sweat, bile, or even vomit—that helps the body reset itself in times of illness?
Laxative: Boneset also moves the body in quieter, less dramatic ways.
Its bitterness, so prominent on the tongue, doesn’t just signal its strength—it reveals its direction. Bitter herbs almost always interact with the liver. And when they do, something important happens: bile begins to flow.
Bile is one of the body’s natural laxatives. Produced by the liver and released by the gallbladder, it greases the gears of the digestive tract. When we don’t produce enough, the signs are easy to spot—pale stools, sluggish digestion, mild constipation. Boneset, by stimulating bile production, gently nudges this system back into motion.
It doesn’t force. It supports. And that’s a subtle but important difference.
Immunostimulant: And then, finally, there’s the immune system.
Modern research—never quite as poetic as traditional herbalism, but often just as revealing—has confirmed what herbalists long suspected. Boneset doesn’t just soothe, or purge, or cleanse. It stimulates.
More specifically, some of its constituents have been shown to activate macrophages and white blood cells—the body’s internal defense line. Paired with its ability to open the skin, drain tension, and stimulate release, Boneset becomes something more than a single-action herb. It becomes a system-wide signal.
A call to wake up. To push out. To let go.
Affinities
Every plant has a pattern—an internal logic to the way it interacts with the human body. Boneset, more than most, seems to follow its own map. Its reach isn’t limited to one organ or one symptom. Instead, it seems to trace the outlines of the body’s deeper responses—tension, fever, stagnation, ache—and meet them head-on.
Let’s follow that map.
Febrile Mechanism: Start with the fever.
Boneset’s primary relationship is with the body’s febrile response. And that makes sense when you consider its defining action: the relaxant diaphoretic. By softening the tension in the skin, vasculature, and muscles, it helps the body do something essential—sweat. But more than that, it supports the immune system’s own strategy. Raising the body’s temperature is one of the oldest defenses we have. It makes the environment less hospitable for viruses and bacteria, which are notoriously sensitive to heat. Boneset doesn’t fight the fever. It partners with it.
Stomach and Digestive System: From the skin to the stomach—the bitter tonic effect brings us there next.
Boneset stimulates digestive function in a very direct way. The bitterness encourages gastric secretions, sharpens digestion, and—if you’re not careful—can provoke a purge. The emetic effect is real, and it comes with a warning: don’t push the dosage unless that’s what you’re aiming for. Even at moderate levels, the herb promotes bowel evacuation. It’s a cleansing force, top to bottom.
Liver and Gallbladder: Then, deeper still: the liver.
Boneset’s bitterness reaches into this system with gentler intent. It acts as an alterative—a tonic that nudges function back into rhythm. In cases where liver stagnation is paired with nervous tension or poor bile flow, this plant begins to shine. It may not be as forceful as some liver herbs, but its influence is steady. A slight pressure applied in just the right place.
Respiratory System: And then, quite naturally, it makes its way to the lungs.
As a stimulating tonic, anti-inflammatory, and relaxing diaphoretic, Boneset is especially helpful in conditions marked by catarrh—bronchitis, sinus congestion, the slow drag of mucous that never quite clears. It moves things along. Clears the passageways. Encourages resolution without force.
Musculoskeletal System: But perhaps the most surprising affinity is in the name itself.
Boneset. It sounds like folklore—like a name given without clinical basis. And yet, there’s something to it. While modern herbal literature has often overlooked this application, traditional knowledge tells a different story. Boneset wasn’t just used to soothe the aches around the bones during fever. It was also used, quite literally, to help heal the bones themselves.
The name wasn’t metaphor. It was instruction.
Energetics
Bitterness has a temperature. And in the case of Boneset, that temperature is unmistakably cool.
Like most pure bitters, Boneset brings a cooling, drying quality to the body. It encourages fluids to move—out through the skin as sweat, out through the bowels, out from the lungs as catarrh. And while it may begin by stimulating secretions—in the stomach, liver, and lungs—that’s just the first act. Eventually, those fluids leave. The result is something that herbalists recognize well: short-term moistening, long-term drying.
But here’s where it gets interesting.
The dominant picture—cooling, draining, soothing excess heat—makes perfect sense. Boneset excels in conditions marked by inflammation, irritability, and that unmistakable fire-in-the-tissues quality. And yet, not everyone agrees on its temperature. Some sources suggest Boneset is warming. They point to its use for chills, to the presence of volatile oils, to the way it’s employed in fever states that include coldness, not just heat.
And both views are right.
Matthew Wood, in his Earthwise Herbal, suggests Boneset is suited to the cold/depression tissue state—aching joints, dull muscle pain, sluggish circulation. Not the hot, red, inflamed kind of discomfort. But the slow, dragging kind. The kind that feels like a cold front settling into your bones.
Which brings us to one of the more peculiar things about this plant: it seems to live in two worlds.
Boneset is used for both hot and cold conditions. It cools fever, but it’s also used for chills. It dries up fluids, but it starts by producing more of them. It relaxes, but it also tones. That contradiction isn’t a flaw—it’s a clue. Because what Boneset seems to address most clearly are the shifting conditions. The in-between states. The patterns that come and go, flare up and settle down, move restlessly from one part of the body to another.
Old doctors had a word for this: antiperiodic. They used it to describe remedies that break cycles—of fever, of pain, of recurrent, changeable illness. Today, we might call this quality wind or tension in energetic terms. Either way, Boneset meets it head-on.
And what about tone?
Boneset is, first and foremost, a relaxant. That much is clear in the way it loosens muscles, softens vascular tension, and opens the pores. It’s what makes it so reliable during fever—the body is tight, and Boneset helps it let go. But it’s not entirely without grip. There is some astringency at play too, especially in the mucous membranes. It’s minor, but present. Enough to offer just a bit of structure amid all the release.
So what do you do with a plant that cools and warms, relaxes and tones, dries and stimulates secretions?
You learn to work with it like you would a person: not by pinning it to one trait, but by understanding its temperament.
Effects Upon Tissue States: There’s a moment in herbal medicine when you stop thinking about symptoms in isolation and begin to notice patterns. Cycles. Rhythms. Something comes and goes. A chill followed by a fever. Muscle tension followed by collapse. Hunger swings, mood shifts, sleep disturbances that arrive without warning. The body becomes a pendulum.
This is where Boneset lives.
According to the six tissue state model, Boneset finds its strongest footing in the wind/tension pattern—particularly when this tension targets the liver. But here’s the twist: it doesn’t act on the liver directly. Instead, it influences the blood vessels and the nerves. And that’s enough.
Tension in the vasculature reduces blood flow. And the liver, being the metabolic engine that it is, depends on a constant stream of fresh, oxygenated blood. Cut off the supply, and you don’t just get stagnation—you get dysfunction. The result? Fevers that come and go. Malaise that won’t lift. Night sweats. Headaches. Tenderness over the liver that isn’t quite pain, but isn't nothing either.
Matthew Wood captures it neatly: “The gall bladder is the flagship of the parasympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system.” If there’s trouble switching into the parasympathetic—into rest, digestion, healing—you’ll see it ripple through the gall bladder first. The pattern is changeable: chills and fever, constipation and diarrhea, no appetite one moment, ravenous hunger the next.
In these cases, the old herbalists didn’t reach for gentle tonics. They reached for nauseating bitters—plants like Boneset, Blue Vervain (Verbena hastata), Blessed Thistle (Cnicus benedictus). Bitters with bite. Bitters that could move the liver, ease the nerves, and coax the system out of its loop.
Traditional Chinese medicine has its own name for this pattern: liver wind. But here, "liver" doesn’t just mean an organ—it refers to a system. A distribution of energy, a coordination of rhythm. When liver wind arises, it doesn’t just cause dysfunction—it disrupts movement. Muscles tighten. Nerves fire. The whole system locks into a kind of restless tension. And that’s where Boneset comes in again—not just as a bitter, but as a relaxant.
It softens the wind.
Historically, this pattern was most visible in cases of malarial fever. The alternating chills and heat. The long-term digestive damage. The aching joints and weakened liver. All classic signs. And all, interestingly, responsive to herbs like Boneset, Blue Vervain, and Wormwood (Artemisia absinthium). Malarial fevers, in essence, create liver wind.
Now, here’s something curious: the immune and lymphatic systems aren’t innervated directly. Which means, technically, wind/tension shouldn't affect them. But long-standing congestion tells a different story. The lymph can seize. Dump. Purge itself in sudden spasms—what older texts once called “venting the spleen.” The body sweats, trembles, weakens. The blood shifts, and the nervous system jolts into a sympathetic overdrive. These aren’t just immune reactions. They’re emotional ones too.
The most familiar version of this state? Acute fever. The kind where the body is tense, cramping, sore, irritable. Muscles ache. Sleep is elusive. The whole person feels tightly wound. A “ball of nerves.” And behind it all, the prolonged shivering mechanism keeps feeding back into the same loop—sympathetic dominance, poor digestion, liver stagnation. Boneset, as a relaxant diaphoretic, was made for this.
Look at the clues: chills, shivering, tension, spasms, post-fever malaise, unresolved digestive issues, alternating diarrhea and constipation, symptoms that come and go. It’s the classic wind/tension portrait. And Boneset, consistently, brings relief.
Now, it’s worth noting: Boneset isn’t just for one state. It can be used in heat/excitation—the full-blown, red-faced fever. And it can be used in cold/depression—the dull, aching muscles, the underperforming liver, the slow, heavy digestion. But these are variations on a theme.
At its heart, Boneset is a remedy for alternation. For wind that moves between extremes. For systems that don’t know how to stop swinging.
And in a world where imbalance often shows up not as too much or too little, but as too much of both—that’s a powerful place to stand.
Specific Indications
The old herbalists didn’t speak in systems biology or biochemical cascades. They spoke in patterns. In temperaments. In qualities. And when it came to Boneset, their language told a very clear story.
In cases of coughs, bronchitis, or pneumonia—especially when the person is sluggish, constipated, and “bilious,” that archaic yet still useful descriptor of stagnation and irritability—the early recommendation was both direct and forceful. A strong enema to move the lower bowels, followed by a hot infusion of Boneset, taken orally, until vomiting occurred.
This might sound harsh. But in that era, this type of treatment wasn’t just accepted—it was a carefully tuned method. The logic was simple: clear the bowels, purge the stomach, and suddenly the whole system starts to move. Congested mucous membranes relax. Heat finds its exit routes. Circulation returns to the surface. And afterward, with smaller, sustained doses, Boneset could continue to tone the tissues that had just been emptied.
In eruptive fevers, more stimulation was needed. But in the sluggish, tense, aching kind—Boneset was already enough.
Then there’s its role in rheumatism.
Here, too, specificity mattered. Boneset was especially prized in gouty and bilious types of rheumatism. In the first case, it cleansed and toned the gastric membrane; in the second, it relieved tension in the liver and gallbladder. Again, the focus wasn’t just on symptom relief—it was on restoring flow. On helping the system reset.
In patients where the stomach and nervous system were both irritable—especially with accompanying constipation and bile congestion—small enemas of Boneset were considered a direct way to calm the nerves and benefit the gut. No matter how it was administered, one thing remained consistent: Boneset eased nervous irritation.
Dr. Locke offered one of the more methodical approaches. In cases of masked intermittent fever—where chills and heat came in irregular waves, where the patient was slow, tired, aching, but barely perspiring—he recommended a strong infusion. One ounce of the plant steeped in a quart of boiling water. Half a teacup every fifteen minutes until vomiting occurred. Then bedrest. Smaller doses continued until a good, solid sweat broke through, often lasting two or three hours. After that: tonics.
The method may sound antiquated. But the observation is precise: in these slippery fevers, where almost nothing else seems to shift the tide, Boneset takes precedence.
Even in persistent headaches linked to malaria—intermittent, focused behind the brow, accompanied by the ghosts of ague—Boneset was the herb of choice.
But not all of Boneset’s gifts are physical.
Matthew Wood observed something striking: the overall pattern of this herb suggests that it may play a role in normalizing excess androgen levels. These hormones heighten the immune response, stimulate bone growth, stress the liver, fuel anger, and often contribute to acne. In that light, Boneset’s historical affinity for bones, immunity, liver tension, and irritability begins to align in a new way.
This leads us directly to the psychological profile.
Anger. Tantrums. Mood swings that flip without warning. These are classic signs of liver congestion. But it’s not just moodiness—it’s a specific kind of emotional terrain: vital, but volatile. David Winston calls it “stagnant depression”—not the low-energy, flatline despair that most associate with the word, but something sharper. It’s depression that smolders. It holds anger underneath. Irritability that alternates with withdrawal. It moves back and forth—another expression of wind.
And this, too, is where Boneset belongs.
Clinical Patterns
1. Fevers: Of all the uses for Boneset, one stands apart.
Fever.
Not the low-grade, lingering kind. Not the occasional flush that fades with a nap. Boneset is for fevers that dig deep. Fevers that live in the bones, where aches radiate from the marrow outward. Fevers that don’t just burn—but pull, stretch, and wrench the muscles in the process.
There are a few theories as to why fever can feel this way. One points to the bone marrow. During an intense immune response, the body ramps up production of white blood cells—drawing heavily on the marrow’s reserves. Another view holds that the immune system begins breaking down muscle tissue for raw materials—stripping proteins and turning them into amino acids to build more immune cells. Either way, the sensation is the same: a profound ache in the bones and muscles. And that’s where Boneset earns its name.
Some say it was named for its use in “breakbone fever” (now known as dengue). Others suggest it was literally used to help set bones—but that’s a story for another section. For now, it’s enough to say that when someone describes their fever as “bone-deep,” Boneset is the first plant that comes to mind.
Its value lies in its action. Boneset is one of the best examples we have of a relaxant diaphoretic. It cools, it relaxes, and it opens the skin to release internal heat. This makes it ideal for high fevers where tension builds alongside temperature. The acrid bitterness points to this energetically—it draws down, softens, and moves.
Traditionally, it was also used for intermittent or malarial fever—those marked by alternating chills and heat, by symptoms that flicker back and forth but never resolve. That lingering chill that seems to settle in and never quite leaves? Boneset was made for that.
In Chinese medicine, intermittent fevers are often seen as liver-related. And the liver, of course, is paired with the gallbladder. Chronic fevers, especially the kind that create tension in the blood vessels and reduce circulation, can eventually lead to hepatic congestion—even jaundice. In a strange but elegant arc, pain that begins in the bones travels through fever and ends, ultimately, in the gallbladder.
Warm Infusion and Traditional Application:
As a warm infusion, Boneset produces slow, steady sweating—diaphoresis, as the old texts say. This makes it especially useful in bilious, bilious-remitting, and yellow fevers, where liver and bowel involvement is marked. The herb’s dual action—on both surface and organ—makes it doubly effective.
Some believed its free use could cut short a bilious fever entirely. Others were more cautious, noting that while it wasn’t especially stimulating, it remained reliable in “terminating agues”—another old name for intermittent fever. In very large doses, especially when taken warm and frequently, Boneset acts as an emetic. Though rarely used this way intentionally, it proved useful when vomiting was needed—especially in bilious fevers or acute liver inflammation.
In conditions like dengue—or breakbone fever—it was a trusted ally. The same for scarlatina (scarlet fever) and smallpox, where it served as a “depurating sudorific”—a herb that helped cleanse through sweat. In measles, though, it was less useful, as it had a tendency to relax the bowels too much.
And there’s more.
In the early stages of colds and rheumatism, when the limbs ache and the chill feels anchored in the joints, Boneset often brought relief. This, many believe, is the real reason for its name. It didn’t just support fevers. It soothed the aftermath.
Its effect on the nervous system, while moderate, was noticeable. Combined with a diffusive stimulant like Xanthoxylum, it could function as an antispasmodic. Used in enemas, it was even more pronounced—both as a relaxant for the lower bowels and as a nervine, calming the system more effectively than when taken by mouth.
Add in a bit of Zingiber (ginger), mix it with demulcents, and the combination helped push blood gently and evenly toward the surface. That last effect—an equable diffusion of blood—may sound technical. But for someone in the throes of fever, aching and restless, it meant one thing: relief.
2. Depressed Digestive Function: The digestive system is often the first to falter and the last to recover.
In the wake of a fever or flu, it’s not uncommon to feel drained—not just in energy, but in appetite, function, and rhythm. Food doesn’t sit right. Hunger comes and goes unpredictably. The gut feels slow, stagnant, heavy. And this is where Boneset steps back in—not with dramatic force, but with a steady, downward movement that brings things back online.
Its bitter taste is more than a flavor—it’s a signal. Bitterness has long been known to awaken the digestive tract, and Boneset does this thoroughly. It stimulates not only the primary organs—stomach, intestines—but also the supporting cast: liver, gallbladder, pancreas. The result is a quiet but widespread effect on digestion and assimilation.
The Eclectic physician Finley Ellingwood singled it out for its effect on the stomach, calling it a notable agent in that arena. Historically, as you know by now it was even used as an emetic—clearing the stomach at the onset of fever, a practice less common today, but once valued for its ability to “reset” the gut in acute illness.
But Boneset’s influence doesn't stop at the stomach. It continues downward, encouraging bile production in the liver and promoting its release from the gallbladder. This is where it becomes a cholagogue—an agent that helps move bile—and a choleretic, stimulating its creation.
And bile, of course, is the body’s own laxative.
Taken in larger doses, Boneset will often move the bowels swiftly. In smaller amounts, its astringent side becomes more apparent—gently toning the intestinal walls. This balance between stimulation and toning makes it especially well-suited for the sluggish, post-fever constitution: the kind marked by low appetite, heavy limbs, and a sense that digestion just hasn’t caught up yet.
This connection is no coincidence. In high fevers, the parasympathetic nervous system—responsible for digestion and rest—can become disoriented. Chills and heat alternate. Signals get crossed. Boneset helps re-establish the rhythm.
Still, it’s not for every digestive picture.
As T. J. Lyle, a Physiomedicalist, observed: Eupatorium is not best when the bowels are too free—except when such is the result of torpor of the liver. In other words, if loose stools are coming from heat or irritation, Boneset may not be appropriate. But if the looseness is paradoxically the result of stagnation—if the liver is sluggish and not doing its job—it might still be just the thing.
It has also been used in various liver complaints, especially general biliousness, habitual constipation, and even some forms of jaundice—both acute and chronic. In more stubborn cases, it was often paired with Syrup of Juglans cinerea (butternut bark), which helped move the bowels more reliably when needed.
Taken as a cold infusion, Boneset behaves less like an aggressive bitter and more like a quiet, steady tonic. In this form, it acts as an aperient—a mild agent to relieve constipation—and supports the digestive tract without pushing too hard. The effect is subtle but persistent, especially helpful when tissues are irritable, dry, or inflamed.
Used externally—as a fomentation (a warm compress made from tea), or more conveniently, as a sitz bath—Boneset’s influence becomes even more tactile. It helps relax the bowels from the outside in. Inflammation eases. Spasms settle. And what was once locked in tension begins to unwind.
William Cook, a careful observer from the Physiomedicalist tradition, offered a particularly vivid description of how Boneset works through the digestive and hepatic systems:
Given by cold infusion, or other cold preparation, it is a soothing and relaxing tonic, suitable to all irritable forms of dyspepsia; is gently relaxant to the hepatic apparatus, promoting both the secretion of bile and its ejection from the gall-ducts; and finally securing a mild laxative action on the bowels.
It reads like a checklist: calm the stomach, stimulate the liver, clear the gallbladder, and move the bowels—gently, but effectively. According to Cook, this makes Boneset valuable in bilious conditions, especially when tissues are sensitive or tight, and in habitual constipation where dryness and thirst are part of the picture.
It also finds a place in skin conditions stemming from liver dysfunction, and in recovery after fevers—particularly those that were intermittent or bilious in nature. The recovery phase, Cook suggests, is where Boneset quietly shines: it restores tone without overexerting the system.
But the same qualities that make it so useful also define its limits.
Boneset is not meant for cold and sluggish stomachs—that is, those lacking in fire or tone. It’s not appropriate for torpid livers paired with flaccid tissues. And it is definitely not helpful where the bowels are already moving too freely. In all of these cases, its cooling, relaxant nature may do more harm than good.
Which brings us back to the importance of nuance.
Boneset is not a blanket remedy. It excels in specific patterns—especially where there's tension, heat, or irritability to soften. And when used as a tonic, it often works best in combination. Cook recommends pairing it with more stimulating allies: Gentiana, Sabbatia, Hydrastis, Artemisia, or even a touch of Capsicum. Together, these combinations help maintain a gentle laxity in the bowels without tipping over into catharsis.
Its action may be slow. But it is, in Cook’s words, persistent and very reliable.
And in herbal medicine, sometimes those are the qualities that matter most.
3. Respiratory Conditions: Most people don’t associate bitterness with breathing. But Boneset, like many herbs in the old pharmacopoeias, refuses to stay in its assigned lane.
As a bitter tonic, its first impression is gastrointestinal. It stirs digestion, moves bile, tones the gut. But that same bitter edge—the sharpness that signals motion and release—also reaches the mucosa. And not just in the digestive tract, but in the respiratory system, especially its upper reaches.
The result is what herbalists would call a clearing action: Boneset helps move out stagnation. Specifically, mucous and phlegm that hang around too long in the sinuses, throat, or bronchi.
Stephen Buhner offers a precise protocol: in the acute phase of a viral or bacterial upper respiratory infection, take ten drops of tincture in hot water every half hour—up to six times a day. If that intense phase has passed but fatigue lingers, he shifts to four doses daily. The key insight is that Boneset doesn’t just address the spike of illness—it follows the arc through relapse and recovery.
Simon Mills frames it in energetic terms: Boneset “provides an excellent diffusion of heat out of the body,” especially when that heat comes from respiratory fevers, like influenza. It’s useful in catarrhal states generally—the damp, bogged-down conditions where mucous accumulates faster than it clears.
Harvey Felter, an Eclectic physician, adds specificity. He writes:
“In respiratory affections Boneset is efficient to relieve cough, acting best in that occurring in the aged and debilitated, where there is an abundance of secretion, but lack of power to expel it.”
That line captures something essential. Boneset isn’t for dry, hacking coughs. It’s for the heavy ones. The ones where phlegm is present but the body doesn’t have the strength to move it out. This is what herbalists call an atonic state: fluids stagnate not from excess, but from exhaustion.
Felter goes on. Boneset can relieve hoarseness, support cases of humid asthma, and help soothe the irritable cough that lingers after measles. He cautions not to push it too hard—its bitterness, after all, can tip into nausea. For children, he recommended administering it in a gently aromatized syrup.
In pneumonia, Boneset’s role is twofold: early on, it helps with chest pain and cough; later, it becomes useful again—this time to calm the residual irritation and support expectoration when the lungs begin producing more mucous than they can manage. And because it is both a tonic and a stomachic, it doesn’t just clear symptoms—it supports recovery, sharpening appetite and aiding digestion, both of which often collapse during lung infections.
William Cook adds another layer. He notes that Boneset has a clear influence on the lungs themselves. It helps in cases of dull, aching sensations in the chest, chronic coughs, and general lung weakness—especially when the tissue is slightly irritated or sluggish. Even in languid respiratory conditions, it works well, particularly when paired with a more stimulating herb. Its action is both soothing and toning, and, as Cook observes, “too often overlooked.”
That’s the paradox of Boneset. It doesn’t perform in grand gestures. It doesn’t create spectacular change overnight. But in cases of fatigue, lingering dampness, dull ache, and low respiratory tone—it quietly becomes essential.
Felter’s comment may say it best: “There is an abundance of secretion, but lack of power to expel it.”
And that’s exactly where Boneset does its best work.
4. Muscular and Nervous Tension: At first glance, it’s easy to place Boneset in the category of diaphoretics—herbs that help the body sweat, release heat, move fever along. But what’s often overlooked is that many of the best relaxant diaphoretics are also antispasmodics. They don’t just open pores. They ease tension—especially in the vasculature, the skin, and the nervous system.
And here’s the interesting part: that action is rooted in bitterness.
We often associate bitter herbs with the digestive system. But the body tells a broader story. Bitter receptors aren’t limited to the tongue and gut—they’re also found in the heart, the lungs, the vascular walls. And perhaps most importantly, bitter plants influence the parasympathetic nervous system—the system that governs rest, digestion, and regulation.
That’s the real secret behind Boneset’s action on tension. It’s not a hypnotic. It doesn’t sedate or dull. Instead, it supports the body in remembering how to relax. When the parasympathetic rhythm has been thrown off—especially by alternating chills and fever, or by the lingering sympathetic dominance that follows illness—Boneset helps restore the baseline. This is the reset switch. And from there, everything begins to shift: digestion, gallbladder function, circulation, muscle tone, mood.
Finley Ellingwood, one of the old Eclectic physicians, offers a compelling example. He describes a case of nervous digestive collapse—a woman with longstanding neurasthenia and emphysema, who could not keep food down. She wasn’t nauseated. She didn’t vomit. The food simply came back up. Doctors were stumped. But fifteen drops of Boneset, every two hours, changed the outcome. By day two, the regurgitation stopped. By day five, it didn’t return. And when it did relapse months later, the same simple treatment worked again.
Ellingwood also recounts another case—a man with intractable hiccough at the edge of death. Every remedy had failed. Then came Boneset, this time with a bit of capsicum, administered hourly. The hiccough vanished. Permanently.
These stories are reminders: sometimes the most subtle plant action—relaxation, rhythm, tone—is exactly what the system needs to right itself.
William Cook takes this idea further. Boneset, he says, is almost a pure relaxant. It doesn’t stimulate the way some herbs do. Its power is slow, persistent, and directed—especially toward what he calls the “more inward muscular structures.” The stomach, gall ducts, bowels, uterus. But that’s not the whole picture. It also reaches the skin and peripheral nerves. Cook saw clearly that Boneset’s effect wasn’t limited to one region. It radiated inward and outward.
That’s part of what makes Boneset so difficult—and so fascinating—to study. Sometimes it appears cooling. Other times, warming. It relaxes, but also tones. It drains, but strengthens. It acts deeply in the gut and gallbladder, yet also works at the surface of the skin. The remedy moves where it’s needed.
And that movement, as Cook suggests, can be shaped by form. A hot infusion will draw it toward the skin. A cold infusion moves it inward. A tincture lands somewhere in between.
The deeper you study Boneset, the clearer the pattern becomes: it doesn’t follow the usual rules. It follows the body.
5. Broken Bones: There’s a long-standing argument in herbalism—quiet, persistent, and surprisingly heated—about a single question:
Can an herb heal a broken bone?
When it comes to Boneset, the name alone seems to demand the question. But depending on who you ask, you’ll get very different answers.
Some insist that the name refers only to “breakbone fever,” a nickname for dengue, with its infamous bone-deep aches. They’ll tell you flatly: the plant doesn’t heal bones. It was never used that way. It can’t be used that way—at least not internally. Others take it further, saying the whole idea stems from a misunderstanding, a linguistic twist that herbalists have clung to without evidence.
And yet, if you step outside the bounds of nineteenth-century medical literature and into the oral traditions of North America, a very different story emerges.
Matthew Wood has explored this in depth—both through clinical experience and through conversations with traditional healers. He points out that the Anishinabe, along with other Indigenous and rural communities in the Great Lakes and Appalachian regions, have long considered Boneset a bone-healing medicine. One of the Anishinabe names for the plant translates directly as “bone… to repair.”
Keewaydinoquay, a well-known Anishinabe herbalist, learned about Boneset’s bone-healing properties from her mentor, Nomequay. She herself used it to treat a compound fracture while alone on an island in Lake Michigan.
And the stories keep coming.
In The Book of Herbal Wisdom, Wood shares several cases. In one, a woman shattered the ball of her humerus in a dirt bike accident. Doctors were pessimistic—talking about amputation. But she turned to Boneset, combined it with mullein, lobelia, energy work, and prayer. Two weeks later, during surgery, the surgeon found something he hadn’t expected: “It was as if I had opened up a different person.” The fragments had begun knitting back together. She later added comfrey, and six months on, she was fully recovered.
Another woman suffered a spiral fracture in her tibia. On the third day of sipping Boneset tea while her husband did Reiki, they heard a click. She looked up and said, “I just felt the bone go back into place.” The fracture healed without complication.
None of this shows up in conventional medical texts. No formal documentation. No peer-reviewed studies. But it does appear again and again in folk medicine. And it begs a deeper question: what if the name “Boneset” wasn’t a misunderstanding at all?
There’s even a theory as to how this might work.
When a bone breaks, it releases chemical signals—messenger compounds that guide the repair process, helping the two ends realign. One thought is that Boneset may amplify or assist these signaling mechanisms. It’s not a bone builder like Comfrey. It doesn’t flood the system with minerals or directly regenerate tissue. Instead, its gift may lie in alignment—in helping the pieces find their place before healing begins.
That subtle distinction is crucial.
Comfrey, they say, will knit bone back together—but if the bone is out of place, it will heal wrong. Boneset, by contrast, might help the structure realign. Then Comfrey can come in to strengthen and rebuild.
In other words, Boneset sets the stage. Comfrey seals the deal.
And once again, what seemed at first like an old herbal myth begins to look like something far more precise.
6. Weak Immunity: There’s a familiar rhythm to immune support in herbalism: a handful of herbs tend to dominate the conversation. Echinacea usually leads the list—reliable, well-studied, widely accepted. But sometimes, a lesser-known herb steps quietly out of the shadows, and the picture starts to change.
Boneset is one of those herbs.
German researchers, looking for compounds that could enhance the body’s own immune function, found something striking: Boneset appears to “boost” immunity even more strongly than Echinacea. That’s not to say it replaces it—but rather that it operates with a kind of intensity that makes it hard to ignore.
The mechanisms aren’t entirely clear. Some suggest it enhances both innate and specific immune responses. Others point to possible direct antimicrobial actions. But even without a full scientific explanation, the effects are being noticed in clinical settings.
Richard Whelan, a clinical herbalist based in New Zealand, puts it plainly:
“Boneset… conveys a singular kind of fast-acting intensity to an immune tonic that has no comparison.”
He uses it carefully—in moderate doses—but frequently, especially when something needs to shift quickly. Echinacea, he says, has broad and dependable effects. But paired with Boneset, the result can be transformative. The body moves from stagnation to momentum. It stops spinning its wheels.
And Boneset doesn’t stop at immunity.
It also has a cleansing effect, particularly in cases where there’s too much fluid hanging around. It’s the kind of plant that shines in patterns of damp congestion—not just physically, but energetically. Think: night sweats, bloating, chronic sinus issues, fluid retention, sluggish digestion. Boneset doesn’t just push the immune system—it clears space for it to work better.
In a way, its immune action mirrors its diaphoretic one. It doesn't force; it opens pathways. And sometimes, that’s exactly what the body needs to remember how to defend itself.
Pharmacological Data
When we talk about the power of plants, we’re often talking about experience—how they make us feel, how they shift a fever, clear a cough, or calm a gut. But beneath those effects lies chemistry. And in the case of Boneset, the chemistry is surprisingly complex.
Start with the sesquiterpene lactones—a group of compounds that sound obscure but act broadly. Boneset contains several: eupafolin, eupafolutin, eufoliatin, euperfolide, and helenalin, to name a few. These constituents are bitter—very bitter—and that bitterness is not just a taste, but a signal. It tells the body to pay attention. In the digestive tract, these compounds stimulate secretions and, in larger doses, may even help expel parasites—a less talked about, but historically relevant use for the herb.
They’re also highly immunostimulatory, with some evidence pointing toward anti-tumor effects and antiplasmodial actions. That’s a mouthful, but it essentially means these compounds may help the body resist both cancerous cells and malaria-causing parasites. It’s not just folklore—these molecules are busy.
Then there are the polysaccharides, specifically a group called 4-O-methylglucuroxylans. These long-chain sugars are noted for stimulating the immune system as well—but likely through different mechanisms than the lactones. Where the sesquiterpenes are sharp and aggressive, polysaccharides tend to be more supportive, more nourishing in how they engage immune response.
The flavonoids—quercetin, kaempferol, rutin, eupatorin—are more familiar. These are inflammation modulators. They move through the blood vessels, influencing capillary tone, reducing irritation, and often adding a degree of resilience to tissues. You’ve likely seen them cited in everything from elderberries to green tea.
Volatile oils, present in smaller amounts, offer yet another layer. These are warming and antiseptic, helping to stimulate circulation and clean up along the way. Not dramatic, but definitely supportive.
What all of this tells us is something simple, but easy to overlook:
Boneset doesn’t work because of one magic ingredient. It works because of many small conversations happening throughout the body. From the gut to the immune system, from the vasculature to the lungs, its compounds nudge and guide rather than overpower.
Which may be why it’s so widely useful—and so often misunderstood.
Safety
Every medicine, no matter how trusted, has its limits. Boneset is no exception.
For all its usefulness—for fevers, tension, sluggish digestion, respiratory congestion—this is not an herb to use casually, or for the long haul. It’s an acute remedy, not a daily tonic.
Let’s begin with the basics. Boneset belongs to the Asteraceae family, a group that includes ragweed, daisies, and sunflowers. People with allergies to this plant family may react to Boneset as well—typically through skin irritation or digestive upset. It’s a manageable concern, but worth noting.
Then there’s the question of form and preparation.
Some herbalists prefer the fresh tincture, believing it retains more potency. But Richard Whelan offers a crucial warning: fresh Boneset contains a toxic compound called tremetol, which is broken down during drying. His advice is simple and sound: don’t use the fresh plant. Dry it first, then prepare your tincture. That one step makes all the difference.
And while toxicity is rare, a newly discovered layer of caution has emerged: pyrrolizidine alkaloids, or PAs. These compounds are present only in trace amounts, and there’s no evidence they’ve caused harm in therapeutic doses. Still, prudence suggests we use Boneset only when needed, and for short durations. Thankfully, it’s a fast-acting herb. You don’t need large doses or prolonged use to see results. That’s part of what makes it so effective—and so safe when used wisely.
Larger doses may induce vomiting—a property that was once used intentionally, but today is more of a side effect to avoid. It’s also not recommended during pregnancy, another standard precaution with herbs that stimulate the bowels or circulation.
Then there’s the energetic profile.
Boneset is cooling and drying. That’s part of what makes it so effective in hot, damp conditions—fevers, flus, congested tissues. But in someone already cold and dry? The effects can be aggravating. Over time, it may worsen those constitutional patterns—especially if the bowels are already loose or the system relaxed. In such cases, Boneset doesn’t support. It depletes.
As for herb-drug interactions—there are no known conflicts. But like everything with Boneset, that doesn’t mean “use freely.” It means “use thoughtfully.”
Because this is not an everyday herb. It’s the kind you reach for when something’s wrong—and then set aside once the balance is restored.
Dosage and Preparation
There’s a certain paradox built into Boneset: it’s powerful, but doesn’t demand much.
A little goes a long way—especially when it’s prepared correctly. Its bitterness is intense, almost piercing, and for most people, a small amount is more than enough. That makes dosing both easier and more delicate. Push too far, and you cross into emetic territory. For some, that may be therapeutic. For others, it’s a surprise they weren’t ready for.
Boneset yields readily to both water and alcohol. This means it can be prepared in a variety of ways, depending on the need—and the constitution of the person using it.
Infusion
The most traditional method is the hot infusion. Just half a teaspoon to a full teaspoon of dried herb per cup of water. In the midst of a fever, this is taken hot—one cup every half hour—to encourage sweating and break the fever’s grip. But again, less is often more. Even half a teaspoon of Boneset tea is enough to generate a strong physiological response in most people.
Too much, and you risk vomiting. Which, to be fair, was once considered part of the treatment. Some herbalists still see it that way. But for those unfamiliar with the experience, the reaction can be alarming—so caution, and context, are key.
Tincture
For tinctures, the form of the herb matters.
Fresh Boneset contains tremetol, a toxic constituent that breaks down during drying. Because of this, the consensus is to use the dried herb when preparing a tincture. The flowers are most often used, sometimes with the leaves.
The standard ratio is 1:5 dried herb in 45% alcohol. David Hoffman recommends a dose of 2–4 ml, three times daily, though in practice, smaller doses are often sufficient to achieve the desired effect.
Fluid Extract
William Cook, always methodical, offers a recipe for a fluid extract—a more concentrated preparation:
“Macerate a pound of coarsely ground Boneset in a quart of fifty percent alcohol; transfer to a percolator, and treat till half a pint has passed... evaporate the rest... combine, filter, and dissolve the residue in diluted alcohol.”
The result is a strong, versatile extract—used not on its own, but in combination with other herbal preparations. Tonic syrups, cough remedies, alteratives for skin complaints. Boneset, in this context, serves as a catalyst—bringing movement, sharpness, and clarity to sluggish systems.
Cook’s recommended dose: 20 drops to half a fluid drachm, three or more times daily.
It’s a small dose. But with Boneset, that’s all you need.
Formulation
There’s a certain elegance in how herbs work together—like voices in harmony, each bringing something distinct to the blend. Boneset, despite its bitterness, finds its way into several such combinations. Not as a gentle background note, but as a firm, clear presence—one that guides the direction of the whole formula.

This trio does something very specific: it relaxes the neuro-vasculature. In other words, it helps the body let go of the internal clenching that keeps fever trapped inside. These are herbs that loosen blood vessels, ease muscular tension, and open the skin to promote sweating.
There’s a catch, of course. All three also carry emetic properties—they can provoke vomiting. So the key here is to start small. Let the body adjust. The action is therapeutic, but the reaction can be intense.
For a gentler version, you can balance this formula by adding Ginger (Zingiber officinale)—a warming stimulant diaphoretic. Or soften the edges with Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis) or Peppermint (Mentha piperita). Either way, let’s be honest: this formula will taste terrible. Which means thoughtful pairing isn’t just therapeutic—it’s survival.
