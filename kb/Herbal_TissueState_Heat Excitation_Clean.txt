The Heat/Excitation Tissue State
At first glance, it sounds like something we already know: inflammation.
Redness. Swelling. Heat. Tenderness. These are the hallmarks we’ve all been taught to recognize. And in many ways, the heat/excitation tissue state does echo that familiar pattern.
But if you stop there—if you simply equate heat with inflammation—you miss something important.
Because not all inflammation is driven by true heat. And not all heat is what it seems.
In fact, the appearance of heat can sometimes be a response—a secondary reaction to something else happening deeper in the system.
For example, take the damp/stagnation state. In Chinese Medicine, this is often referred to as damp-heat—an accumulation of fluid and waste that the body tries to push out by turning up the temperature. Heat, in this case, is a kind of mobilization effort. A strategy.
Or consider dry/atrophy. This pattern looks like heat because the fluids are gone—and without fluids to cool and cushion, tissues become irritated, hot, and dry. But the heat isn’t the root—it’s a consequence of depletion. In Chinese Medicine, they’d call this yin deficiency.
Then there’s the more counterintuitive case: heat from cold. Here, the tissues are so depressed, so inactive, that the vital force tries to spark movement any way it can. The result looks like heat—but it comes from a place of collapse, not excess.
As Matthew Wood puts it:
“There are several different kinds of heat. Only that associated with an acceleration in the rate of tissue function… is associated with the irritated tissue state.”
And this is where the term excitation becomes useful.
Because this tissue state isn’t just about heat. It’s about overactivity. It’s metabolic acceleration. Irritation. Oxidation. The tissues are doing too much, too fast. They’re breaking down under the strain.
Think of a child on Christmas Eve—bouncing off the walls, jittery with anticipation, every sense turned up to eleven. That’s excitation. It’s a kind of raw, unfocused energy—too much fire in too small a space.
Now imagine that same energy in the liver. Or the gut. Or the skin.
That’s what this tissue state looks like.
Joseph Thurston called it “an exaggeration of the normal function-rate.” And that’s precisely what it is. Normal processes—digestion, circulation, nerve signaling—start happening at a pace the tissue can’t sustain. The system overheats.
Modern science might call this oxidative stress. Hyper-reactivity. Metabolic overload. But long before those terms existed, herbalists recognized the same pattern—and learned to treat the heat not by suppressing it, but by understanding where it came from.
So what does heat look like?
More importantly—what does it feel like?
Start with the sensory details. A burning sensation. A rush of warmth. Skin that turns red and flushes easily. A pulse that feels rapid and close to the surface. In some cases, you might see a sharp tongue—red in body, tipped with yellow coating. Matthew Wood calls this the “flame-shaped tongue,” and once you’ve seen it, you won’t forget it.
These are the kinds of signals the body sends when it's running too hot.
The signs extend outward:
Nervous excitability—restlessness, insomnia, anxiety.
Pain that’s sharp and shooting, not dull or achy.
Hypersensitivities—autoimmunity, allergic flares, overreaction to stimuli.
A general intolerance to hot weather, spicy food, or even warm drinks.
The vital force in this state doesn’t stay hidden. It rises. It radiates. It behaves, in essence, like fire. You see it in red cheeks, a high superficial pulse, and symptoms that move upward—heat in the head, tension in the chest, buzzing in the mind.
And then there are the conditions—those modern names that carry a very old energetic story:
Stomach ulcers, acid reflux, GERD.
Hyperthyroidism, rapid metabolism, heat intolerance.
Burning urinary tract infections.
Fever.
Canker sores.
Sunburns.
High blood pressure—particularly when driven by stress or emotional intensity.
Allergies, where the immune system acts more like a trigger-happy alarm than a balanced gatekeeper.
In each of these, you’re not just dealing with inflammation. You’re seeing the tissues in a state of excitation. They’re overstimulated, hyper-responsive, metabolizing too fast. There’s too much signal, not enough rest.
Different traditions describe this in different ways. In Ayurveda, it’s associated with the pitta constitution—sharp, fiery, prone to intensity. In Chinese Medicine, it’s a state of excess Yang, a surplus of primal fire. And in medical astrology, this pattern often aligns with the influence of Mars—the planet of heat, speed, and agitation. The Sun can contribute too. And unsurprisingly, the Fire signs—Aries, Leo, Sagittarius—are more prone to this state.
But regardless of the system, the pattern is the same.
Too much movement. Too much fire. And a body that needs to cool, calm, and slow down.
In the next section, we’ll explore the kinds of plants that help shift this state—those with the power to soothe, cool, and restore balance to a system on overdrive.
Cooling the Flame: Herbal Categories for Heat/Excitation
When tissues overheat—when metabolism speeds up, when irritation sets in, when the whole system begins to feel inflamed and reactive—what kind of plant do you reach for?
You reach for coolness.
That much is intuitive. But the way cooling shows up in herbs is more nuanced than it first appears. There are different strategies for cooling the body, and different plants that approach it from different angles—through moisture, flavor, tone, or metabolic shift.
Let’s look at a few of them.
Start with sour.
Now, in Ayurveda, sour is often flagged as aggravating to pitta, the fire-based constitution. But that’s largely due to the use of vinegar—an acidic, warming substance—as the archetypal sour. But in Western herbalism, many of our truly sour plants behave very differently.
Think of berries in summer. Lemonade on a hot day. The tartness doesn’t stoke the fire—it cools it.
These herbs—often fruits—carry a light, refreshing quality. They lower heat by supporting the tissues and blood vessels, toning the cardiovascular system, and restoring hydration. Examples include:
Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna)
Elderberry (Sambucus spp.)
Hibiscus (Hibiscus spp.)
Rose hips (Rosa spp.)
Then there are the bitters.
These are the great metabolic moderators. Almost universally cooling, but only when they are true bitters—plants that don’t also carry stimulating aromatic compounds like essential oils. (Think gentian over peppermint.)
Among the most helpful for heat are the bitter nervines—plants that not only cool the digestive fire but also calm nervous excitation.
Some key allies:
Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora)
Motherwort (Leonurus cardiaca)
Bugleweed (Lycopus virginiana)
Blue Vervain (Verbena hastata)
Hops (Humulus lupulus)
These herbs aren’t just bitter—they soothe. They slow things down. They help the overactive system pause.
But cooling isn’t always about temperature. Sometimes it’s about moisture.
Heat often arises in part because something else is missing—namely, fluids. In Chinese medicine, this is called yin deficiency. In Western terms, you could say the tissues are parched—literally burned out.
Here, we turn to demulcents. Their cooling action comes not from chemistry, but from consistency. They soothe dryness, rehydrate mucosal tissue, and coat irritated linings.
Try:
Marshmallow (Althaea officinalis)
Slippery Elm (Ulmus rubra)
Chickweed (Stellaria media)
Solomon’s Seal (Polygonatum spp.)
Plantain (Plantago major)
Linden (Tilia europea)
We’ll revisit these under the dry/atrophy state—but here, they already begin to show their value.
Now, let’s return to a broader truth: heat, left unchecked, doesn’t stay in one place. When chronic, it begins to affect the cardiovascular system. It oxidizes tissues, weakens capillaries, stresses the heart, and contributes to systemic inflammation.
This is where cardiac tonics come in—plants that aren’t just cooling, but specifically protective of the heart and vessels.
Among them:
Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)
Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.)
Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis)
Motherwort (Leonurus cardiaca)
Linden (Tilia europea)
They regulate circulation. They reduce reactivity. They help the heart hold steady in the face of internal fire.

Patterns and Universals
Of course, these herbal lists aren’t exhaustive.
They’re not meant to be. What they offer is something more valuable than completeness: pattern recognition.
Because once you begin to see how the heat/excitation tissue state expresses itself across different organ systems, you also start to notice which plants return again and again—regardless of where the heat shows up.
These are the universals—the herbs that seem to speak a language the whole body understands. They’re versatile, consistent, and reliably cooling across systems.
You’ll see them often:
Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)
Wild Cherry (Prunus serotina)
Rose hips (Rosa spp.)
Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.)
They don’t just treat one kind of heat. They help shift the pattern itself—wherever it lives.
And as you get deeper into this work, you’ll begin to build your own inner map of these relationships. Organ affinities, energetic actions, constitutional patterns—it all becomes clearer, not because you memorize it, but because you recognize it.
