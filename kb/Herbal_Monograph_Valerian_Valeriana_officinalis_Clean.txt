Valerian (Valeriana spp.)

The name Valerian comes from the Latin valere—to be strong, to be brave, to be well. It's a word that suggests fortitude. And for a plant that’s spent centuries calming anxious minds and restless bodies, it’s a curious contradiction. Why name a sedative after strength? Perhaps because sometimes, being calm is the bravest thing we can do.

In North America and the UK, it’s simply Valerian. In France, Valériane. In Germany, Baldrian. The Italians call it Amantilla. Across languages, the name changes—but the root remains the same, quite literally. It is the root of the plant that herbalists use, and it is the root that carries its defining power.

Valerian belongs to a family once known as Valerianaceae, though more recently it’s been shuffled into the Caprifoliaceae family—a reminder that even taxonomy can’t sit still for too long. And there’s more than one Valerian to know. Valeriana officinalis is the one most are familiar with, but it shares its lineage with V. edulis, V. jatamansi, V. sitchensis, and Valeriana wallichii—each shaped by their region, their climate, their ecosystem.

It’s native to Europe and western Asia, but like many herbs of deep utility, Valerian didn’t stay put. It took root in North America, particularly in the Pacific Northwest, where it flourishes. You’ll find it not only along the forest floor but higher up—in sub-alpine and alpine zones, where plants often have to work harder to survive.

There’s a moment that stands out: seeing Valeriana sitchensis growing unexpectedly at sea level—just 200 meters—in the understory of old Douglas Fir, Cedar, Hemlock, and Maple. That shouldn’t happen. But it did. And maybe that tells us something else about Valerian: it finds a way.

If you choose to grow it, know this—it’s easy enough to start. But Valerian doesn’t rush. As a perennial, it needs time. A year or two won’t cut it. You’ll want to wait four, maybe five years, to harvest a root of real substance. Because here’s the thing: roots, like character, take time to develop. The deeper the root, the more potent the plant. And if you have the chance to use it fresh—do. The fresh root is more reliable, more effective, and, perhaps most surprising of all, better tasting than its dried counterpart.

So what begins as a quiet, humble herb—tucked in forests, sprouting in high places, maturing slowly underground—turns out to be a teacher in disguise. Not just of sleep or nervous system relief, but of strength, patience, and place.

And maybe that’s what valere really means.

The 5 Pillars of Valerian

Tastes

Try asking a group of herbalists to describe the smell of Valerian. You won’t get silence. In fact, it’s quite the opposite—everyone seems to have something to say. And often, they don’t hold back.

The comparisons are colorful: dirty socks. Old gym bags. Something left too long in the back of the closet. The aroma is... distinctive. No one forgets their first encounter with Valerian’s scent. It is, in the language of herbalism, “characteristic.”

And yet, not everyone finds it unpleasant. Some even come to love it. That’s the curious part. I happen to be one of those people. To me, the smell is aromatic, sweet even—though I should confess I tend to work with the fresh root, not the dried one. That makes a difference. When Valerian is dried, a particular compound—isovaleric acid—takes center stage. And isovaleric acid, chemically speaking, has a certain reputation. It’s what gives the plant that infamous, fetid edge.

Taste-wise, Valerian is equally complex. It’s bitter, pungent, and acrid. And if you’ve never tasted something acrid, you might assume it’s just another shade of bitterness or sourness. But it’s not. Acrid has a way of hitting the back of the throat—a kind of medical edge, unmistakably “herbal,” unmistakably medicinal.

Matthew Wood once described the acrid taste as being like “bile in the back of the throat.” And while that’s hardly flattering, it’s accurate. Many of our strongest antispasmodic herbs are acrid: Lobelia, Black Cohosh, Kava, Hops, Skunk Cabbage—each of them carrying that same bold, unmistakable flavor profile. They don’t whisper their intentions. They announce them.

What’s interesting about Valerian is that it contains valproic acid, a kind of butyric acid—the same class of compounds responsible for strong, aromatic smells. In perfumery, a butyric note can give depth. In herbalism, it tends to be... memorable.

Nicolas Culpeper, writing centuries ago, summed it up this way:
“The roots of Valerian have a strong, and to most people a most disagreeable smell; to the taste they are warm, bitter, and a little acrid.”

And that’s the thing about Valerian. It doesn’t hide. It isn’t subtle. But in a world full of soft edges and mild impressions, there’s something admirable about that kind of honesty.

Primary Actions

Valerian is, above all else, a nervine hypnotic. That term—hypnotic—might sound a little dramatic. But in herbalism, it means something quite specific: these are the herbs that relax the nervous system so deeply, so profoundly, that they invite the body into sleep. They don’t merely take the edge off. They quiet the entire system.

It’s why Valerian is often reserved for evening use. For many, its sedative effect is simply too strong for daytime hours. And yet, for someone living with anxiety or prone to panic attacks, that depth of calm might be exactly what’s needed, even at noon. The key is in the dosage. Too much and you may find yourself drowsy and detached. Too little and it may not touch the tension at all. The sweet spot is where relief and functionality meet—and that line is different for everyone.

From that core hypnotic action, all of Valerian’s other effects unfold. But here’s where things get interesting.

For some people—around four in ten, in fact—Valerian doesn’t sedate at all. It stimulates.

Yes, it’s confusing. A plant famous for sleep also has the power to keep people awake. For those individuals, it’s not a lullaby—it’s a jolt. One teacher I studied with eventually stopped recommending it altogether, simply because too many of his clients had this paradoxical response. For them, Valerian didn’t soothe. It agitated. It sharpened rather than softened. It brought energy instead of rest.

Cats respond this way too, interestingly enough. Valerian, like Catnip, can make them wild. And perhaps there’s something to that. The Eclectic physician Finley Ellingwood once called Valerian a “cerebral stimulant.” In higher doses, he warned, it increases circulation to the brain, especially toward the back of the head. In some people, that results in headaches, restlessness, even nausea. He writes:

“Valerian in large doses stimulates the brain, causing headaches, giddiness, perverted vision, restlessness, agitation, nausea… It lessens sensibility, motility and reflex excitability, and, if the dose be large enough, causes central paralysis. The first effect is stimulation, followed by depression of the nerve centers.”
(Ellingwood, p.125)

In other words, the line between stimulation and sedation may not be as clear as we think.

This effect might come down to Valerian’s energetics. Unlike most hypnotics, which are cooling, Valerian is distinctly warming. That warmth increases circulation and stimulates movement in the body. For people with hot constitutions—those who are already running warm—it can push them further in that direction, tipping into overstimulation. But for those who are cold, pale, sluggish? Valerian works like a light turning on. Ellingwood reinforces this point when he notes that its effects are best observed when the nervous system is “inactive and feeble,” especially when the face is pale and the skin is cool.

This pattern—cool body, sluggish circulation, nervous agitation—is where Valerian shines.

It’s also why Valerian is an effective spasmolytic, or anti-spasmodic. It works by easing overactive nerve impulses, the kind that send muscles—smooth or skeletal—into painful contractions. It’s especially helpful in the digestive system, female reproductive tract, and anywhere tension restricts flow. Again, it’s that combination of nerve relaxation and warmth that makes Valerian so uniquely suited to untangle knots in the body.

Add to that its role as a carminative, and you begin to see the full picture. Carminatives are herbs that contain volatile oils—aromatic, warming compounds that move energy and ease cramping in the gut. Valerian fits this profile. It doesn’t just settle the nerves; it moves the body. It gets things flowing again.

And finally, Valerian acts as an anodyne, a plant that helps with pain. Not in the same way as aspirin or morphine, but in a subtler, systemic way. It reduces tension. It calms spasm. It distances the mind from the sensation of discomfort. It doesn’t erase pain. It reframes it.

So we arrive at this paradox: Valerian is a sedative, yes. But it is also a stimulant. It is both warm and relaxing, active and grounding. It soothes the nervous system while also stoking the inner fire. And depending on who you are—depending on your constitution, your symptoms, your state of health—that contradiction might be the very thing you need.

Affinities

Every herb has its favorite places in the body—systems, tissues, organs it returns to again and again, as if drawn by instinct. For Valerian, that pull is strongest in the nervous system. That’s its home base. But as with all complex remedies, its reach doesn’t end there.

Let’s start where the action is clearest.

Valerian works on both the central and peripheral nervous systems. It helps the body shift from fight-or-flight—what’s known as the sympathetic state—into rest-and-digest, or parasympathetic dominance. This alone makes it a profoundly useful plant in our high-speed, overcaffeinated world. And it doesn’t just calm you down. It moves blood. It increases circulation to the brain, particularly to the occipital region at the back of the head, where stress and overthinking often manifest. Ellingwood, always a careful observer, noted that its influence reaches deep into the spinal nerve centers, reinforcing that Valerian’s action is not just general—it’s targeted.

But the nervous system is not a closed loop. When it calms, the rest of the body follows.

That’s where the musculoskeletal system comes in. Valerian’s antispasmodic effect—its ability to quiet nerves and loosen muscle tension—gives it a clear affinity for both smooth and skeletal muscles. Whether it’s the gripping pain of menstrual cramps, the tension of a spastic cough, or the sharp discomfort of urinary stones, Valerian responds the same way: it softens the signal from the nerves and allows the muscles to let go.

And then there’s the digestive system.

It’s not hard to see how these systems connect. When the nervous system is on high alert, digestion suffers. Blood is pulled away from the gut. The body prioritizes survival over assimilation. But when Valerian steps in—relaxing nerves, easing muscular tension—it sends a new message: you’re safe. And digestion responds.

Valerian’s aromatic, slightly warming nature adds another layer here. As a carminative, it encourages movement in the GI tract—not the frenetic churn of stress, but a rhythmic, grounded flow. It draws blood back to the belly, slows the system, and invites a deeper kind of nourishment.

So while the nervous system is where Valerian makes its first and strongest impression, it’s the ripple effect—into the muscles, into the gut, into the body’s very rhythm—that shows us just how deeply this plant knows its way around.

Energetics

Valerian isn’t just chemically complex—it’s energetically distinct. In traditional energetic terms, it is a warming stimulant, thanks to its volatile oils and resins. And warmth, in this context, almost always comes with a degree of dryness.

Avicenna—the Persian physician whose influence stretched across centuries—described Valerian as “warming, hot and dry in the third degree”. But he was careful to note that its heat exceeded its dryness. The dryness isn’t about draining fluids the way a diuretic might. Instead, it’s a byproduct of its warming nature, a heat that subtly shifts the body’s internal climate.

In terms of tone, Valerian is notably relaxant. Its influence softens tissue tension rather than tightening or bracing it. And that’s important, because it tells us where in the spectrum of tissue states Valerian finds its home.

The match is clearest with wind/tension states, particularly when cold signs are present. This is the state of sympathetic overdrive—when the fight-or-flight response dominates, keeping the parasympathetic “rest and digest” mode sidelined. In this state, the nervous system drives contraction. The body pulls inward. We become, as the saying goes, “a ball of nerves.”

Valerian addresses this from multiple angles. It sedates the nerves. It relaxes the muscles. It supports digestion. And as a carminative, it moves stagnant wind through the digestive tract, easing the cramps and spasms that tension so often brings along for the ride.

But wind/tension doesn’t exist in isolation. It can be both cause and consequence of other states. Heat and dryness, for example, can set the stage for wind/tension by depriving tissues of moisture, nutrition, and oxygen. That dryness can in turn intensify heat. Heat then worsens the dryness. And both can fan the flames of wind. These states—wind, dryness, heat—often loop into each other, forming a self-perpetuating cycle.

Wind, being the state of movement, rarely stays still. It’s unpredictable. It shows up as alternating conditions: diarrhea followed by constipation, chills followed by fever, muscles that tense and release without warning. It’s the constant shift, the changeability, that defines it. In Ayurvedic terms, this is the nature of vata—mobile, variable, sometimes sudden in onset, often sudden in disappearance.

Valerian meets this movement with stillness. It doesn’t just calm the surface. It works from the roots—where the nerves begin, where the contraction starts—to unwind the system and make space for rest.

Special Indications

There’s a moment when you meet someone—pale hands, cold fingers, a nervous smile that doesn’t quite land—and you can almost feel it in your own body. The tension. The tightness. The sense that their nervous system is always just one step away from unraveling.

This is the person Valerian speaks to most clearly. Muscular tension, nervousness, insomnia, anxiety—especially when paired with cold extremities and pale skin—these are the signals. And not just physical signs, but energetic ones.

Because the truth is, Valerian is heavy. Not in a burdensome way, but in the way gravity is heavy. It pulls you down—out of your head and back into your body.

The people who need it most often feel the opposite: ungrounded, ethereal, scattered. Their stress rides high in the shoulders, lives in the belly, hums beneath the surface of their thoughts. They're thinkers, overthinkers, people whose minds never fully turn off.

Valerian interrupts that. It helps the body let go first, and from there, the mind follows.

Herbalist David Osborn put it this way:

“Valerian closes off the aura so that these subtle spiritual entities and perceptions can no longer agitate and distract the individual. They are able to get back to the basics of life and regain their focus and composure.”

It’s a poetic description, but one that captures something real: the way certain people seem overwhelmed not just by daily stress, but by the sheer volume of sensation—internal, external, emotional, invisible.

Valerian quiets that interference.

Matthew Wood brings this into sharper emotional focus. He writes that Valerian is specific for people who “hold anger inside, causing a knotted feeling in the stomach or low back.” People who carry tension not just in their body, but in their story. And for the tightly-wound, over-stimulated types, even a small dose—just 1 to 3 drops—can be enough to spark a shift:

“Lack of concentration and attentiveness, in tense people,” he adds.

So Valerian isn’t just about sleep or spasm. It’s about returning to self—calming the nerves, softening the body, and dimming the mental noise just enough to hear something quieter underneath.

Clinical Patterns

1. Nervous System Disorders: There’s a certain kind of insomnia that Valerian was made for. The kind where you lie awake, staring at the ceiling. Minutes tick by. Then hours. Your body’s tired, but your nerves haven’t gotten the message. This is where Valerian comes in—not just as a sleep aid, but as a bridge. It helps the act of falling asleep actually happen.

And when it works, it works. Quickly. Maybe too quickly. Some people wake up the next morning feeling like they’ve been hit with a heavy, soft hammer. Groggy. Foggy. Sluggish. I’ve felt that. In fact, I generally avoid Valerian for that very reason. But when sleep won’t come, I know I can count on it to work. It’s a powerful tool—but like any powerful tool, it needs precision. A lower dose can often make all the difference. Find the sweet spot, and you get the benefit without the morning-after fog.

Valerian’s reach goes well beyond sleep. Its effects on the cerebrospinal system make it a candidate for a wide range of nervous complaints—anxiety, stress, nervousness, irritability, insomnia, even depression. But here’s where things get more complicated.

With depression, caution is necessary. Valerian is deeply sedating. And for someone already weighed down by emotional heaviness, that sedative effect might push them further into the fog rather than helping lift it. But depression is rarely just one thing. When nervousness and anxiety are part of the picture, Valerian can actually help by calming the system without dulling it entirely. Ellingwood put it this way:

“It is applicable in the nervousness of depression because of its gentle stimulating influence, and in these cases its influence is heightened by combining it with stimulants.”

So, rather than giving Valerian on its own, pairing it with herbs like Rosemary or Ginkgo—circulatory stimulants—can balance out its heavier qualities. Especially for someone who’s pale, cold, and low in vitality. In this context, Valerian becomes not a tranquilizer, but a grounding force.

And science seems to back this up. In one German study from 1985, Valerian was shown to be as effective as diazepam—a well-known benzodiazepine—at treating anxiety, and it outperformed diazepam when researchers considered the risk–benefit ratio (Panijel, 1985). Another trial in 1993 (Leuschner et al.) found Valerian performed similarly to diazepam and chlorpromazine, another major anxiolytic. In both cases, the plant held its own alongside some of the most powerful pharmaceutical sedatives available.

But Valerian doesn’t stop at mild nervous conditions.

Its affinity for the cerebrospinal system also makes it relevant for more severe neurological disorders, including epilepsy. Greives notes that Valerian was first introduced as a specific remedy for epilepsy as far back as 1592, when Fabius Calumna reportedly used it to cure the disease. Ellingwood confirms its value:

“It is directly indicated in hysterical conditions of whatever character with feebleness; with nervous excitement, and morbid vigilance, in hysterical epilepsy, and in nervous headaches with some pallor. It is excellent in the hysteria and nervous disturbances incident to menopause.”

That phrase—"morbid vigilance"—says a lot. The kind of nervous system overdrive where sleep, rest, even mental quiet, feels impossible.

Valerian has also shown benefit in chorea, a spasmodic disorder characterized by involuntary jerking movements. Ellingwood called it “disordered motility,” and in this context, Valerian’s antispasmodic effects on the nervous system become central. When nerves fire unpredictably, when the body jerks, twitches, or shudders without cause, Valerian helps to steady the storm.

But that steadiness comes with a caveat.

In large doses, Valerian can flip. Ellingwood warns:

“It stimulates the brain, causing headache, giddiness, perverted vision, restlessness, agitation, nausea… Large doses cause increase of urine with slow pulse and drowsiness, ending in deep sleep… If the dose be large enough, it causes central paralysis. The first effect is stimulation, followed by depression of the nerve centers.”

It’s not a gentle arc—it’s a sharp curve. From stimulation to sedation to paralysis. Which is why Valerian demands respect.

Matthew Wood offers another perspective. He calls Valerian a “palliative sedative with a generalized, nonspecific range of activity.” His teacher, William LeSassier, went further:

“It’s a knocker-outer. Makes one numb. It is generally overused and overrated.”

That might sound harsh. But in the context of modern herbalism, it’s a necessary caution. Valerian is excellent for some. For others, it’s too much. And even when it works, it may not get to the root of the problem. It provides relief—but not necessarily resolution.

That’s why many practitioners reserve Valerian for short-term use, and with careful attention to both constitution and dose. For the right person, in the right moment, Valerian can be the plant that brings everything back down to earth. But used too broadly, too often, or too heavily—and it risks becoming the very thing it was meant to relieve: a weight too hard to shake.

2. Musculoskeletal Pain and Spasm: There’s a particular kind of pain that doesn’t come from injury or inflammation, but from something subtler—spasm. The nerves misfire. The muscles tighten. And they don’t let go.

Valerian meets this kind of pain at its source.

As a strong antispasmodic, Valerian works across the spectrum of smooth and skeletal muscle spasms—from minor tics and twitches to deeper, more persistent patterns of tension. It not only calms the nerves that send errant signals to the muscles, but also warms and circulates, helping to move blood into areas that are cold, stagnant, or gripped by contraction.

That warming, moving quality makes it especially useful in menstrual pain, where the blood is often described as thick or coagulated, and where circulation to the uterus is poor—what some call a “cold uterus.” In this context, Valerian does more than relieve cramping. It acts as a female tonic, helping to regulate menstrual flow, and offering emotional support for the nervousness, mood swings, and irritability that can appear premenstrually.

It also has a place in menopause, particularly when anxiety or restlessness dominate. But there’s a nuance here. Because of its warming energetics, Valerian may be contraindicated in cases of excessive hot flashes. Again, the match depends on the individual’s constitution. Too much heat, and Valerian might add fuel to the fire.

But perhaps the most fascinating part of Valerian’s action is where it works: at the neuromuscular junction, the precise space where nerves and muscle fibers meet. This is the conversation point—the place where intention becomes movement. And it’s here that Valerian softens the signal, relaxes the contraction, and quiets the system down.

That makes it relevant for a wide range of conditions:
• Lumbago
• Sciatica
• Bronchial pain

These aren’t just “pain” conditions. They’re pain bound up in the nervous system—tight, reactive, patterned. And Valerian has something to say in each case.

It also stretches its usefulness to the urinary tract, especially in urinary calculi—kidney stones—where pain stems from muscular spasm and pressure. In larger doses, its diuretic properties help move fluid, ease tension, and relieve the gripping discomfort that comes when stone meets nerve.

So what we see, again and again, is that Valerian doesn’t act in isolation. It’s not just a painkiller. It doesn’t numb or override. It works with the body’s systems—where nerve meets muscle, where tension holds, where warmth is needed—and helps them let go.

3. Digestive Upset: It’s easy to forget that digestion isn’t just mechanical. It’s emotional. Psychological. Somatic. There’s a reason we talk about “gut feelings,” or feeling “sick to our stomachs.” The nervous system lives in the belly just as much as it lives in the brain.

Valerian knows this.

It’s not typically thought of as a digestive herb. And yet, in the right situation—nervous digestion, stress-related gut tension, psychosomatic upset—it may be one of the most useful plants you could reach for.

Valerian’s contribution here is multifold. It’s a bitter tonic, a warming carminative, a sedative, and an antispasmodic—all in one. That combination makes it especially well-suited to cramping, gas, colic, dyspepsia, and belching. But more specifically, it shines when those symptoms are rooted not in food, but in feeling—when digestion goes off course because the mind can’t let go.

That’s what we mean when we talk about nervous digestion. The kind where meals sit heavy in the stomach. Where the gut feels tight, contracted, slow. Where stress expresses itself as bloating, tension, irregularity.

Valerian steps in to warm, stimulate, and relax. It enhances gastrointestinal secretions through its warming nature, but at the same time, it soothes. It doesn’t push the system. It invites it back into balance.

One of its key mechanisms is its antispasmodic action. It relaxes the smooth muscles of the gastrointestinal tract, which helps “stuck wind”—that common and uncomfortable symptom of tension in the gut—to move again. In this way, Valerian doesn’t just act on the stomach. It acts on the body’s relationship to stress.

Because here’s what often happens: we get stressed, the sympathetic nervous system (fight or flight) takes over, and digestion slows down. Blood leaves the gut. Tension rises. And everything becomes harder to process—both food and emotion.

Valerian helps to switch the system back. As a parasympathomimetic, it encourages a return to parasympathetic dominance—that “rest and digest” state where the gut can finally do its job. In doing so, it helps us digest not only our meals, but also our experience.

So while Valerian may be known for sleep and spasm, it may be in the gut—quietly, subtly, gently—where some of its most profound healing takes place.

4. Stress Related Heart Conditions: When we talk about stress, we usually speak in emotional terms—tension, worry, restlessness. But stress is also structural. It shows up in the tissues, in the vessels, in the constriction of the very systems meant to carry life forward.

Valerian understands that.

It’s not just a sedative or a sleep aid. It’s a vascular relaxant—a plant that physically softens the body in places where chronic tension has made everything tight and brittle.

Take high blood pressure, for example. In the context of stress and anxiety, hypertension often begins with constriction. The vessels tighten. The tissues lose pliability. The blood still flows, but now it’s pushing through narrowed channels. Pressure rises, circulation suffers, and symptoms—like cold extremities or pale skin—begin to appear. The blood never quite makes it to the edges.

Valerian’s role here is subtle but important. It works by relaxing vascular tension. It opens the path. It gives the blood more space to move. And that simple act—more space—has profound consequences: improved peripheral circulation, warmth in the hands and feet, better blood flow to the brain, and even clearer thinking.

This effect comes not just from its sedative nature, but from its warming stimulant action. In this sense, Valerian behaves a little like a mild version of Lobelia or Cayenne—stimulating circulation, softening constriction—but without the intensity those two herbs often bring. It's movement without agitation. Warmth without heat.

So, when we think of the “Valerian person”—tense, tight, pale, cold—we can see the pattern emerging. Their nervous system is holding too much. Their vessels are holding too tight. And their blood doesn’t quite reach where it’s meant to go. Valerian releases the grip, and the system begins to move again.

Because of this, heart conditions rooted in nervous energy—palpitations, stress-induced hypertension, anxious hearts that race without cause—are often helped by Valerian. This is why, in British herbalism, Valerian appears so frequently in cardiovascular formulas, especially paired with Hawthorn and Yarrow. It doesn’t treat the heart in isolation. It treats the nervous system as it relates to the heart.

And then there’s this curious note from Grieve, who quotes Ettmuller on a more delicate application:

“Valerian strengthens the eyesight, especially when this is weakened by want of energy in the optic nerve.”

It may seem unrelated—vision, blood pressure, anxiety—but it’s not. The optic nerve is fed by some of the smallest capillary beds in the body. If blood can’t move freely, neither can nutrients. Waste can’t clear. Vision becomes strained—not because of overuse, but because of lack.

By increasing cerebral circulation and relaxing vascular tone, Valerian may support not only the heart and the nerves, but also the tiny, easily-forgotten systems that depend on smooth, gentle flow.

So in the end, Valerian isn’t just calming. It’s expanding. It makes room—physically, emotionally, neurologically—for the system to do what it already knows how to do: regulate itself.

Pharmacological Data

Behind every plant’s action lies a quiet architecture of chemistry. In Valerian, that architecture is both intricate and powerful—a set of compounds that explain, piece by piece, how this unassuming root can slow the mind, soften the body, and send us into sleep.

Let’s begin with what we can smell.

Volatile oils are among Valerian’s most noticeable constituents—bornyl acetate, beta-caryophyllene, valerone, and valerianic acid. They’re what give the root its infamous aroma, often compared to dirty socks or wet earth. But their purpose goes far beyond the nose. These volatile oils are also what give Valerian its warming, stimulant effect, helping to move blood, open vessels, and engage the body’s circulatory rhythm.

The next group—less immediately sensed but deeply active—are the iridoid glycosides, especially the valepotriates like valtrate and isovaltrate. These are considered some of Valerian’s primary active constituents, known for their antispasmodic and nervine hypnotic effects. But there’s a twist: these compounds are delicate. In dried extracts, they often degrade into what are called baldrinals—a transformation that alters their activity. So the form in which Valerian is prepared matters. A fresh root tincture is not the same as a shelf-stable capsule.

And then there’s GABA—Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid. It’s not just present in Valerian. It defines how Valerian works.

GABA is the brain’s primary inhibitory neurotransmitter—the chemical messenger responsible for calming the central nervous system and regulating muscle tone. In simple terms: GABA slows things down. It tells the nerves, not now. It says, rest.

Valerian enhances that message.

According to a study by Andreatini & Leite (1994), Valerian’s sedative effect may stem from changes in cortical membrane receptor activity, specifically through a process called hyperpolarization—where chloride channels open, and neurons become less likely to fire.

That’s the technical side. But here’s what it means in plain terms:

Valerian doesn’t just contain GABA. It supports its entire ecosystem. It may:

Block the reuptake of GABA, keeping it active in the synapse

Inhibit GABA’s breakdown, preserving its effect longer

Act as a precursor, possibly increasing GABA synthesis itself

The result? As GABA activity rises, the brain’s natural brakes engage. The system slows. Muscles relax. Sleep becomes more accessible. This is sedation from within, not imposed from outside.

Spinella (2001) sums it up this way:

“As GABA activity increases, sedation becomes apparent and sleep onset is promoted.”

The picture that emerges is one of synergy. Volatile oils stimulate. Iridoids relax. GABA modulation calms. Together, they form a three-part harmony—one that works not just on one receptor or one symptom, but across systems.

Valerian doesn’t force the body to rest. It builds the conditions for rest to arrive.

Safety

Valerian, for all its strengths, is not without its complications. That’s often the case with plants that act deeply on the nervous system. They calm—but only when they’re well-matched to the person. Otherwise, they can cloud, or even aggravate.

Start with the basics.

Long-term use of dried Valerian preparations is not recommended, especially in those with dry constitutions. The plant itself isn’t inherently drying, but its preparation can shift its energetics. The fresh root behaves differently than the dried one. As Michael Moore notes in Medicinal Plants of the Mountain West, once dried, isovaleric acid emerges—what he calls the “dirty sock factor.” The sedative action remains, but chronic ingestion of the dried root in high doses can, he warns, lead to melancholy and depression.

That matters, especially for people already prone to low mood. It also helps explain why some herbalists view Valerian as a palliative, not curative. It can relieve, yes. But it may not resolve. In those cases, rebuilding herbs like Milky Oats, paired with nutritional and lifestyle changes, may serve better for long-term nervous system health.

There’s also the matter of energetics. Valerian is warming, and for hot, choleric constitutions, this can backfire. People who already run hot—flushed skin, red tongue, rapid pulse—may find that Valerian stirs up more agitation than it calms. In Valeriana wallichii, the Indian species, that heat is even more pronounced. For these individuals, balancing Valerian with cooling herbs is essential, or avoiding it altogether.

And then there’s the paradox.

Somewhere between 30–40% of people—almost 4 in 10—experience the opposite of what Valerian is supposed to do. Instead of calming, it stimulates. Instead of sleep, it brings restlessness and anxiety. That’s why it’s wise to try Valerian for the first time during the day, not at bedtime. Especially for children, where the wrong response doesn’t just affect the child—it affects the whole household.

Ellingwood cautions, in no uncertain terms:

“In conditions where the nervousness is induced by hyperactivity—actual increased nerve force—or where there is organic disease, it is not the remedy.”

In other words, Valerian calms what is overstimulated, but it does not suppress true excess. It soothes weakness, not power. And when misapplied—especially in large doses—the consequences are predictable:

“Headache, giddiness, perverted vision, restlessness, agitation, nausea... increase of urine, slow pulse, deep sleep... central paralysis. The first effect is stimulation, followed by depression of the nerve centers.”

The progression is important: stimulation, then depression. A dose too high doesn’t just sedate. It destabilizes.

Valerian’s interaction with medications also deserves attention. It may interfere with iron absorption, so iron supplements should be taken at a different time of day. And because Valerian’s sedative effect resembles that of benzodiazepines, it’s occasionally used to help people taper off these drugs. But this should never be done casually. Benzodiazepine withdrawal can be dangerous, even fatal. Any transition from pharmaceuticals to herbs requires medical supervision.

Finally, because of its synergistic effect, Valerian amplifies other sedatives, narcotics, and anxiolytics. That can be helpful. But it can also be risky. What starts as a gentle sleep aid could become, in the wrong context, too much.

So, like many powerful herbs, Valerian lives on a spectrum. For some, it brings peace. For others, it brings fog—or even fire. The key is matching it wisely: to the person, the pattern, the dose, the preparation.

When it fits, it’s remarkable. But when it doesn’t, you feel it.

Dosage and Preparation

With Valerian, dose is everything.

Take a small dose, and the effect can be light, dispersing, even gently stimulating. It loosens tension in the neuromuscular system, supports subtle shifts in organ function, and nudges the body toward balance without dragging it down.

Take a larger dose, and the whole nervous system begins to slow. For some, this is welcome—a sense of calm that leads directly to sleep. But for others, it can become too much: a heavy, dull mental fog, a sense of lethargy that lingers into the next day.

This is where Valerian becomes both art and science.

Because while general patterns exist, individual response varies wildly. One person’s perfect dose is another person’s overwhelm. That’s why experimentation—gentle, gradual, and attentive—is key. Try different amounts. Vary the frequency. And notice: not just if it works, but how it works. What’s its tone? Its rhythm? Does it clear your mind—or cloud it?

Michael Moore, as we’ve seen, preferred the fresh plant. He believed it carried less of the melancholic, depressive effect often seen with the dried root—an observation that dovetails with what we now understand about valepotriates, the active iridoid glycosides in Valerian.

These valepotriates are responsible for much of the stimulating, dispersive quality of the plant. But they are heat-sensitive. In dried or decocted preparations, they begin to degrade, giving way to a more deeply sedative effect. This is why, for those who want a more balanced Valerian—one that loosens but doesn’t flatten—a slightly warm or cold infusion, or a fresh tincture, is often preferred over a decoction.

Recommended Preparations:

Fresh Tincture: 1:2 in 70% alcohol, 30–90 drops, up to 3x daily

Capsules: 2–3 size #00 capsules daily

Cold infusion (powder or whole root): 60-120g, taken as needed

Again, these are starting points—not prescriptions. What matters most is how the body responds. The difference between relief and dullness, warmth and weight, is often just a matter of drops.

So with Valerian, as with so much in herbal medicine, it’s less about finding the “correct” dose and more about learning which version of the plant matches the moment—and the person—you're working with.

Formulation

In herbal medicine, synergy is everything. Rarely does one herb do all the work. The most effective formulas bring together multiple plants, each contributing a specific energetic or functional quality—much like a well-balanced team. And Valerian (Valeriana officinalis), with its complex profile of warmth, sedation, and antispasmodic power, fits into several roles depending on its companions.

Super Sleep Formula
Ingredients:

25% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

25% Hops (Humulus lupulus)

25% Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)

15% Pedicularis (Pedicularis spp.)

10% Wild Lettuce (Lactuca virosa)

This is a hypnotic blend—not just mild encouragement for rest, but a strong, full-bodied invitation to sleep. The lead triplet—Valerian, Hops, and Passionflower—is a well-established combination in Western herbalism, chosen for a reason. Energetically, it’s elegant: Valerian is warming, Hops is cooling, and Passionflower is neutral.

Pedicularis deepens muscular relaxation. Wild Lettuce handles pain that disrupts sleep. Together, this formula also functions as a gentle anodyne.

Dosage:

1 tsp 30 minutes before bed, and again at bedtime

Take during the night if needed

Use lower doses during the day for hysteria, anxiety, panic

Reduce if morning drowsiness occurs

Menstrual Cramp Relief
Ingredients:

20% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

20% Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa)

20% Wild Yam (Dioscorea villosa)

20% Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)

20% Kava-Kava (Piper methysticum)

Here, Valerian supports a blend targeting uterine cramping and muscle spasm. Two groups of herbs work in tandem: direct spasmolytics (Black Cohosh and Wild Yam) and nervine relaxants (Valerian, Passionflower, Kava-Kava). The net result is deeply sedative and antispasmodic.

Note:

This is a warming formula

Use cooling substitutions for hot-type cramping (e.g. avoid Kava)

Use lower doses during the day for alertness

Nervous System Tonic
Ingredients:

25% Milky Oats (Avena sativa)

20% St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum)

20% Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora)

20% Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)

15% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

A restorative formula for nervous system depletion—what used to be called “neurasthenia.” This isn’t about sedation. It’s about repair.

The lead trio—Milky Oats, St. John’s Wort, and Skullcap—forms the foundation for rebuilding and stabilizing the nervous system. Ashwagandha and Valerian round it out with trophorestorative and relaxant actions.

Cerebral Circulation Formula
Ingredients:

30% Eleuthero (Eleutherococcus senticosus)

30% Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba)

20% Bacopa (Bacopa monnieri)

10% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

5% Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis)

This is a warming, stimulating formula for brain fog, mental fatigue, and poor circulation. The core herbs push blood to the periphery and the head, waking up dull senses and cold hands.

Valerian’s inclusion here is minimal but purposeful—just enough to relax vascular tone without sedating the mind. Rosemary, though the smallest dose, adds spark.

Carminative Blend
This is a warming digestive formula—ideal for colic, gas, distention, bloating, and what many refer to as a “nervous stomach.” Each herb here brings a note of aromatic warmth, thanks to its volatile oils. Together, they create motion, ease spasm, and help the digestive system let go.

Ingredients:

25% Orange peel (Citrus aurantium)

15% Cardamom (Elletaria cardamomum)

15% Cinnamon (Cinnamomum verum)

15% Anise (Pimpinella anisum)

15% Fennel (Foeniculum vulgare)

10% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

5% Ginger (Zingiber officinale)

Valerian stands out here not for its usual sedative power, but for its ability to relax the gut wall. It doesn’t dominate the blend—it rounds it out. This formula can be prepared as a tea, syrup, or tincture, taken during or after meals. If deeper nervous support is needed, you could easily add Lemon Balm or Catnip—gentle, aromatic nervines that align with Valerian’s effects.

Heart Bitters Blend
At first glance, this formula breaks tradition. Hawthorn in a bitter blend isn’t common. But from a Chinese medicine perspective, Hawthorn supports stagnant digestion, making it a valuable bridge between the cardiovascular and digestive systems.

Ingredients:

25% Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna)

25% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

20% Motherwort (Leonurus cardiaca)

20% Gentian (Gentiana lutea)

10% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

Gentian drives digestion, stimulating bile and liver activity. Motherwort and Valerian work in tandem to relax the body and clear the mind. Valerian, in particular, acts as a vascular relaxant, opening circulation and enhancing the formula’s reach.

To amplify that effect, one might consider adding a bit of Ginger or Cayenne. The result is a bitters blend that supports both emotional digestion and physical digestion—ideal before meals with family or friends, when the body needs to relax as much as the gut needs to function.

Dosage: A few drops to ½ tsp, about 10 minutes before meals

Pain Formula
This is a broad-spectrum formula for pain, particularly pain rooted in spasm, tension, or injury. The structure is deliberate: a trio of anodynes, a quartet of antispasmodics, and two inflammation-modulating herbs round out the design.

Ingredients:

20% Jamaican Dogwood (Piscidia piscipula)

15% Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

15% Wild Lettuce (Lactuca virosa)

15% Pedicularis (Pedicularis spp.)

15% Crampbark (Viburnum opulus)

10% Turmeric (Curcuma longa)

10% Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra)

Valerian’s role here is unmistakable—it’s one of several herbs working at the neuromuscular junction, helping relax tight, twitching, or spasmodic tissue. But unlike the sharper effects of Jamaican Dogwood or Pedicularis, Valerian has a kind of steady rhythm to it. It eases pain by unwinding tension from within.

Add Turmeric and Licorice, and you get a touch of systemic anti-inflammatory support—quietly addressing the root causes of pain, not just the sensation.

This formula is best suited for musculoskeletal discomfort, nerve pain, or post-injury recovery, especially when tension is a dominant theme.
