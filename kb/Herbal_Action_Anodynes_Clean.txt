Rethinking Pain Relief
Let’s talk about pain.
More specifically, let’s talk about what we believe can and can’t help it.
Most people—understandably—don’t think of herbs as powerful when it comes to pain. They imagine chamomile tea, maybe a warm compress, something gentle, maybe even quaint. Certainly not a match for a throbbing back or nerve pain that keeps you awake at night.
But here’s the twist: I think we’ve been looking at this the wrong way.
Because when it comes to pain, herbalists aren’t at a disadvantage. In fact, I’d argue we’re holding the better hand.
Let’s consider the conventional approach: If you’re in pain, your options are usually limited to two categories. Over-the-counter NSAIDs—ibuprofen, aspirin, maybe acetaminophen—or something stronger: a prescription opioid. And yes, these drugs are effective. They work. They shut things down. They dull the senses. They suppress the body’s inflammatory response.
But that effectiveness comes at a cost.
NSAIDs, for all their accessibility, can lead to ulcers, liver congestion, and gut damage. Opiates—well, we know where that path can lead. In the U.S. alone, they’ve witnessed the full scope of that fallout: dependency, withdrawal, and lives derailed by addiction.
Now shift the lens.
Herbs may not offer the same blunt force power, but they bring something else entirely: range.
Instead of hammering the system into silence, herbal approaches work more subtly. They relax the nervous system. They calm spasms. They reduce inflammation without overriding the body’s intelligence. In many cases, they support healing even as they ease pain.
That’s the key.
Because inflammation isn’t the enemy. It’s a response. A process. A sign that the body is working to repair something. So the real question—the one we don’t ask often enough—isn’t “How do I turn off the inflammation?” It’s: What caused it in the first place?
We’ll dive deeper into that in a future lesson on inflammation modulators. But for now, here’s what I want to say, plainly:
Pain is the #1 reason people go to the doctor.
And yet many herbalists—especially those just starting out—hesitate when a chronic pain friend walks in. They doubt whether their remedies will be strong enough. Whether their toolkit can measure up to modern pharmacology.
But here’s the reality: in many cases, we can do more. Not louder. Not harder. But smarter. We can target multiple layers of the pain response. And we can do it without the fallout.
I know that sounds bold. Maybe even heretical.
But I’ve seen it. Over and over again.
And I believe it’s time we said it out loud.
When I first started studying anodynes—the class of herbs typically used for pain—what I found was, frankly, confusing.
Every book had the same thing to say: these are herbs that relieve pain. That’s it. End of definition.
Which left me wondering: Okay… but how? What kind of pain? What part of the body? What mechanism are we talking about here?
And then I’d see the list. Turmeric. Cayenne. Wild Lettuce. Three plants that couldn’t be more different in how they operate. Different energetics. Different systems. Different outcomes.
It didn’t make sense. Not until I realized something important: “Anodyne” isn’t really a specific action at all. It’s a use case. A label for effect, not process.
That’s why, when we talk about real herbal actions, we’re talking about how a plant changes physiological function—not just what it’s used for.
So I started digging. Asking a different kind of question. Instead of “Which herbs help with pain?” I asked: How do these herbs help with pain?
And that’s when the pattern started to emerge.
It wasn’t a straight line. It was a web. A network of sub-actions that pointed toward different types of pain, different systems involved, different modes of relief.
Some herbs work as antispasmodics—unwinding tension in the muscles and viscera. Others act as inflammation modulators, dampening heat and irritation. Some are nervines, calming frayed pathways in the nervous system. A few are warming stimulants that bring blood to the surface, while others draw it away.
And suddenly, the big messy list started to make sense.
Because a client with nerve pain isn’t the same as someone with arthritis. A throbbing headache is a different animal from a menstrual cramp. And you can’t just throw the word “anodyne” at both and expect the same results.
That’s where the precision comes in.
Understanding the sub-actions lets you match the plant to the person—and to the pain. It brings herbalism back to what it has always been: a practice rooted in observation, in nuance, and in treating people, not just symptoms.
So in the next section, we’ll break this down further. You’ll get to see how different types of anodynes work, when to use them, and—most importantly—why.
Because this is the kind of knowledge that doesn’t just help you manage pain.
It helps you understand it.
When the Body Hurts—and the Mind Floats
There’s a reason many of the herbs listed under “anodynes” tend to come from a single category. Not just because they dull pain—but because they shift something deeper: awareness.
These are the hypnotic nervines. Plants that don’t just reduce discomfort in the body, but create a gentle space between the mind and the physical sensation itself.
It’s subtle at first. A softening. A sense that you’re slightly less present in your body than you were a few moments ago. As if your awareness has drifted half a step backward, or sideways, or away.
But behind that soft drift lies a powerful action. Because most hypnotic nervines don’t just sedate the nervous system—they also relax it. Many are highly effective antispasmodics, especially suited to pain that’s rooted in tension: cramping, tightness, or spasmodic episodes that tug the body out of alignment.
And then, there’s a deeper tier.
When a hypnotic nervine becomes strong enough to suppress nervous system activity rather than just soften it, it enters the realm of narcotics. These are not casual herbs. They alter the central nervous system in profound ways—slowing perception, dulling reflexes, dimming the senses. The result isn’t psychological depression in the emotional sense—it’s physiological depression. Everything slows down.
This is why narcotic plants have always held a central place in the world of pain management. And why one plant in particular has shaped our entire modern understanding of pain relief.
Papaver somniferum. The opium poppy.
From this one plant, we’ve built an entire pharmaceutical industry—morphine, codeine, oxycodone, heroin. Every modern opiate can trace its roots back to the milky white latex that leaks from the scored pods of this striking flower.
And here’s where things get complicated.
Because the opium poppy is legal to grow. You can buy the seeds. You can grow them in your garden. But the moment you scratch the seed pod to release its latex—or prepare any kind of medicine from it—you’ve crossed a legal line.
Which means that while this plant is a powerful remedy, it’s also a forbidden one. If you were to work with it—and that’s a big if—it would require the utmost care. You’d need to know the plant, its effects, its risks. You’d need to understand how to prepare it (high-proof alcohol, 70–80%), how to dose it (slowly, cautiously), and, perhaps most importantly, when not to use it.
This is not a remedy for everyday pain. It’s a plant for emergencies. For trauma. For moments when no other medicine will reach.
And even then, its use comes with a price.
If the opium poppy is the cornerstone of narcotic pain relief, then Cannabis is its controversial sibling. Not quite as ancient in its medicinal prominence, but just as culturally loaded. Depending on where you live, it’s either a crime, a cure, or a casual part of daily life.
Cannabis—Cannabis sativa or indica—has long been used to treat pain. And yet, the very thing that makes it effective—its ability to shift consciousness—is what gives many people pause. Some feel that shift as relief. Others feel it as disorientation.
But that’s not the end of the story.
In recent years, new forms of Cannabis-based medicines have emerged. Products that preserve the plant’s pain-relieving properties while minimizing its psychoactive effects. In short: treatments without the high. For many, these have become not just viable, but preferred options—precise, gentle, and powerful.
Still, Cannabis isn’t alone in this category.
There’s an entire group of plants that, while not technically narcotics, begin to blur that line when taken in the right dose. These are the stronger hypnotic nervines. And if you’ve ever worked with them, you’ll know: they don’t just dull pain—they soften the experience of being in pain.
Taken at the appropriate dose (and that’s always a moving target), these herbs can quiet the nerves, relax skeletal and smooth muscle, and bring on a deep sense of calm. They don’t deaden sensation so much as shift your relationship to it.
But here’s where things get interesting.
Because the very strength that makes these plants effective can also be their limitation. Go too high, and the person gets sleepy. Too low, and the pain persists. It becomes a dance—one of sensitivity, of dosage, of individual constitution.
So what are we really talking about when we talk about herbal pain relief? Not blunt force. Not numbing. But modulation. Support. Relief that works with the nervous system, not against it.
Here are some of the herbs I find most useful when walking that line:

Kava-Kava – again, especially where tension is involved

St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum)
These aren’t just “natural alternatives” to pharmaceutical painkillers. They represent a different philosophy of medicine—one that sees pain not simply as a problem to be silenced, but as a message to be interpreted.
And the herbs? They don’t shout down the message. They soften it. They make space for the body to recalibrate. And in that space, healing begins.
Relaxing the Body, One Muscle at a Time
We often think of pain as something that needs to be numbed. But what if, instead, the answer is to unclench?
Spasmolytics—also known more commonly as anti-spasmodics—are a class of herbs that don’t dull sensation or cloud the mind. What they do is simple. They ease tension. They smooth out the rough edges of pain that comes from cramping, tightness, or spasm—whether in the large, voluntary muscles of the body, or in the more hidden, involuntary ones that surround our organs and vessels.
If pain is a clenched fist, these herbs are what allow the fingers to slowly unfurl.
Now here’s where it gets interesting: many of the hypnotic nervines we just discussed also happen to be strong anti-spasmodics. They relax the nervous system and the muscles it controls. But the reverse isn’t always true. Not all anti-spasmodics are sedating. And that difference matters.
Because when you’re formulating for someone in pain, you’re often doing a kind of balancing act. You may need to relax their muscles—but you might not want to put them to sleep. Or maybe you’re crafting a daytime blend that eases discomfort without fogging the brain, and a night-time formula that invites deeper rest.
Understanding which herbs do what—and when—gives you more leverage. It helps you customize with nuance instead of relying on broad categories.
Also worth noting: the type of muscle involved matters.
Spasm isn’t a one-size-fits-all event. Skeletal muscles (the ones that let you move your limbs) behave very differently from smooth muscles (which line your organs and blood vessels). Some herbs show a clear preference. Others are generalists.
This is what makes the spasmolytic category both rich and subtle. Not just a blunt tool for pain, but a precise key that can unlock specific kinds of tension.
Here are a few standouts in the spasmolytic—but not strongly hypnotic—category:
Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa)
Black Haw (Viburnum prunifolium)
Crampbark (Viburnum opulus)
Wild Yam (Dioscorea villosa)
Lobelia (Lobelia inflata)
Pulsatilla (Anemone pulsatilla)
Skunk Cabbage (Symplocarpus foetidus)
Carminatives (especially for gut-specific cramping)
These aren’t just herbs that “help with cramps.” They’re tools for restoring flow. They loosen what’s stuck, quiet what’s overactive, and bring relief—not by overpowering the system, but by working with its natural rhythms.
And in the world of chronic pain and tension, sometimes that kind of gentle precision is exactly what’s needed.
A Word About Inflammation
I need to be careful here—because this is a subject I could talk about for hours.
Let’s start with something simple: pain and inflammation are often two sides of the same coin. You see one, and the other is rarely far behind. Which means that if we can learn to modulate inflammation—not shut it down, but guide it, shape it—then we can do a great deal to relieve pain.
But here's the thing. Inflammation is not just a problem. It's also a process. A vital, intelligent one. One that the body uses to protect, heal, and signal that something needs attention. So when we reach for an “anti-inflammatory,” we’re not trying to silence the body—we're trying to help it speak more clearly.
I’ll get into the full story elsewhere. For now, just know this: there are herbs that don’t just suppress inflammation, but modulate it. And those are the ones I reach for when pain and heat are at the center of my experience.
Here are a few that I’ve worked with directly—plants that consistently show up when inflammation is driving pain:
Cat’s Claw (Uncaria tomentosa)

There are others, of course. But these are the ones that seem to consistently move the needle when inflammation and pain are locked together. Each one works a little differently. Some cool. Some soothe. Some even rebuild. But they all speak to the same root issue—and they speak it fluently.

A Surprising Ally in Pain Relief
You might raise an eyebrow when you see diuretics listed among remedies for pain. After all, diuretics aren’t the first thing that come to mind when someone’s back is throbbing or their knees are aching.
But here’s the thing: if you want to relieve pain—really relieve pain—you have to ask a more fundamental question. Not just what hurts, but why does it hurt?
Is there inflammation? Spasm? Congestion in the tissues?
Because behind every ache is a pattern. And sometimes that pattern involves water—too much of it, stuck in the wrong places.
When the body experiences injury or inflammation, it doesn’t just heat up. It swells. And when tissues swell, fluid presses into surrounding areas. Including the nerves. And nerve pressure, as it turns out, can feel a lot like pain.
Now imagine you’re trying to treat that pain with antispasmodics or hypnotics. You might get some relief—but if the underlying issue is fluid buildup, those remedies are only scratching the surface.
That’s where diuretics come in. They don’t sedate. They don’t numb. They drain. They help the body move fluid out of the tissues, reducing swelling, and along with it, the pressure—and the pain.
You’ll see this kind of fluid-driven discomfort in swollen, puffy joints that get worse in damp, cold weather. Or in the acute, throbbing aftermath of a sprain or injury. In both cases, pain relief starts with moving the water.
These are a few herbs that have stood the test of time in this regard:
Celery seed (Apium graveolens)
Gravelroot (Eupatorium perfoliatum)
Nettle (Urtica dioica)
Pipsissewa (Chimaphilla umbellata)
Each one works a little differently, but they all speak to that same underlying principle: reduce the swelling, and you shift the experience of pain itself. It’s not the obvious path. But it’s an effective one.
Blood Flow and the Roots of Pain
There’s a particular kind of pain that’s often overlooked. It’s not the stabbing pain of injury, or the dull ache of inflammation. It’s the slow, persistent discomfort that comes from something more subtle: poor circulation.
When an area of the body isn’t getting enough blood, two things happen. First, the oxygen and nutrients that normally nourish the tissue stop arriving. Second, the waste products that those tissues generate—metabolic byproducts, inflammatory compounds, cellular debris—get stuck.
You don’t need a medical degree to predict the result: discomfort, fatigue, and yes—pain.
This is where circulatory stimulants come in. These are herbs that increase blood flow, bringing fresh, oxygen-rich blood into the tissues and carrying out the trash, so to speak. And they do it in a very particular way—through heat.
Circulatory stimulants tend to be warming and pungent. Think ginger. Think cayenne. They light a small internal fire, warming the digestion and invigorating what Ayurveda calls Agni, the digestive flame. But the real magic? They push blood from the core out to the periphery—exactly where it’s needed.
Sometimes these herbs are taken internally. Other times, they’re applied right to the skin. In that case, we call them rubefacients—from the Latin ruber, meaning red. Why red? Because they dilate the capillaries at the surface of the skin, flooding the area with blood. And often, that flood comes with a bit of burn.
Take cayenne, for example. At first, it stings. But that sting is doing something very specific: it depletes substance P, one of the primary messengers of pain in the nervous system. Once it’s cleared, the pain drops off sharply. The discomfort at the beginning is the body’s way of resetting its pain threshold.
You’ll often see these remedies prepared as salves, liniments, or oils. In some cases, like fresh nettle leaf, you can just rub the plant directly on the skin. (Yes, it stings. That’s the point.)
Internally used circulatory stimulants:
Cayenne (Capsicum annuum)
Ginger (Zingiber officinale)
Poplar buds (Populus trichocarpa)
Prickly Ash (Zanthoxylum clava-herculis)
Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis)
Turmeric (Curcuma longa)
Topical rubefacients:
Cayenne (Capsicum annuum)
Ginger (Zingiber officinale)
Nettle leaf (Urtica dioica) — fresh and direct
Peppermint (Mentha piperita)
Poplar buds (Populus trichocarpa)
In the end, it's not just about masking pain. It's about restoring flow. Blood is what brings life to tissue—and when you return that flow, pain has a way of receding on its own.
The Root of the Pain
Let’s take a step back.
When someone comes to you in pain, the first instinct might be to find the herb that makes the pain go away. But what if we thought differently? What if the real question isn’t “What stops the pain?” but “Where is the pain coming from?”
Pain doesn’t appear out of nowhere. It’s an expression of something deeper—a tension in the muscles, a build-up of dampness, a coldness in the blood, an inflammatory fire that won’t die down. These are the patterns. The energetic signatures. And if you learn to recognize them, you start to see that pain is just the surface.
Which brings us to something subtle but incredibly powerful: matching the type of pain to the right category of herbs.
If the pain is sharp, erratic, and tense? That’s wind. You’ll want antispasmodics.
If it’s heavy, achy, and slow? Think dampness—bring in the diuretics and the bitters.
If it burns? That’s heat. Inflammation modulators will be your allies.
If it’s cold, dull, and slow to move? Circulatory stimulants bring the warmth and motion.
Pain isn’t one thing. And that’s exactly why plants are so uniquely suited to help.
Because here’s the secret: plants don’t just block pain. They listen to it. They work with it. They trace it back to its origin and begin the slow, intelligent process of repair. That’s something no pharmaceutical can replicate. Drugs tend to be binary—on/off, pain/no pain. Plants? They’re multidimensional.
Think about what’s possible.
You could create a formula with herbs that gently sedate the nervous system, ease muscle spasms, lower inflammation, improve circulation, and drain excess fluids. All at once. You’d be addressing five different mechanisms that contribute to pain—simultaneously. And you’d be doing it without numbing the person’s body or hijacking their awareness. Just guiding it back into balance.
That’s not just treatment. That’s transformation.
Of course, there’s a place for pharmaceuticals. There are moments—especially acute, traumatic ones—where nothing else will do. But when it comes to chronic pain, the kind that steals quality of life little by little, day after day, the right plant in the right dose at the right time can do something extraordinary.
It can give someone their life back.
So as herbalists, let’s remember this: we are not just soothing symptoms. We are restoring balance. We are helping people reconnect with their own vitality. And in a world where pain is the number one reason people walk into a doctor’s office, that might just be the most important work we can do.
Just think about it: look what you can do with plants.
