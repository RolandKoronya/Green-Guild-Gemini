Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

At first glance, Yarrow doesn’t seem like a plant with a story. It grows by the roadside, in the shadow of mountains, across meadows and open clearings. It’s not flashy. Not rare. But once you start paying attention, you begin to notice that it’s everywhere—and has been, for a very long time.

In fact, archaeologists discovered traces of Yarrow buried with a Neanderthal in Iran. That’s not folklore, it’s science—evidence dating back some 65,000 years. Even more remarkably, the plant showed up again on the teeth of Neanderthal remains in Spain, clocking in at around 40,000 to 60,000 years ago. It seems that Yarrow didn’t just grow near our ancestors—it was used by them. For healing. For survival.

Fast forward a few thousand years and the plant gets its Latin name: Achillea millefolium, bestowed by Linnaeus in 1753. The name draws from Homeric legend—Achilles, the warrior hero of the Iliad, who, as the story goes, learned the secrets of herbal medicine from the centaur Chiron. Achilles is said to have used Yarrow to tend wounds on the battlefield. That’s a heavy legacy for such a feathery plant.

Yarrow’s common names tell their own tale. Milfoil. Old man’s pepper. Bloodwort. Staunchweed. Devil’s Nettle. Herba militaris—soldier’s herb. Each one is a clue, a breadcrumb leading back through centuries of herbal use. This isn’t just a plant; it’s a living medical archive.

Botanically speaking, Achillea millefolium belongs to the Asteraceae family, and just about the whole plant is usable. The leaves are more bitter, the flowers more medicinal. It’s an herb that adapts to its surroundings—not by changing species, but by shifting subtly in size, flowering time, and stature depending on where it grows.

Which leads us to a puzzle: where is Yarrow from? The answer, it turns out, isn’t simple. Botanists agree it’s native to Europe and Asia, but it’s also found throughout North America. According to botanist Kathleen Keeler, what often grows in the U.S. and Canada is a hybrid—a blend of native and European lineages. In other words, most Yarrow in North America is a botanical fusion. The borders, in plant life as in culture, blur.

There are between 110 and 140 species of Yarrow in the world, mostly scattered across Eurasia.

Despite its reach, Yarrow isn’t a climber or a competitor. It lives where there’s space—on plains, in meadows, along roadsides, and into the edges of forests.

That’s the thing about Yarrow. You could pass it by for years without noticing. But once you tune in, it’s impossible not to see it. In ancient burials, in battlefield legends, in meadows and manuals and myths—it’s been quietly waiting to be rediscovered.

The 5 Pillars of Yarrow

Taste

If you’ve ever sipped a cup of Yarrow tea, you’ll understand something right away: this is not an herb that aims to please the palate.

The first thing you notice is the bitterness. It’s sharp, immediate, and unmistakable. The kind of bitterness that makes you pause and reconsider whether you really needed a whole cup. It’s the kind of taste that herbalists respect—but don’t necessarily enjoy. And it’s why Yarrow isn’t exactly the star of cozy evening infusions.

But then, something else happens. As the bitterness fades, a pungency rises. This second layer of flavor reveals something more volatile—literally. It’s the plant’s essential oils making themselves known, drifting into the sinuses, prickling at the edges of your awareness. It’s not quite spice. Not quite heat. But definitely not bland.

And just when you think you’ve figured it out, Yarrow throws in another surprise: astringency. The mouth begins to pucker. The membranes tighten. It’s a physical experience—your body responding in real time to the chemistry of the plant.

Bitterness. Pungency. Astringency.

Each one of these tastes tells a story about what Yarrow does. And when you put them together, you begin to see a deeper pattern. The bitterness points toward its effect on digestion and liver function. The pungency hints at stimulation, movement, circulation. And the astringency? That’s the tissue tone—the tightening, the sealing, the stopping of leaks.

It’s not a pleasant taste, necessarily. But it’s revealing. Because in plants, as in people, complexity often hides just beneath the surface.

Primary Actions

It’s tempting to look at Yarrow and see just another wildflower. But beneath that soft spray of feathery leaves lies a plant whose actions are anything but simple.

Let’s start with one of the most fundamental: astringency. Yarrow has the remarkable ability to tighten tissues. Not in a metaphorical sense, but in a very literal one. It tones and firms up what’s become lax, loose, or flabby. That “puckering” effect you taste in the mouth? That’s Yarrow doing its job on the mucosal membrane. Internally, this makes it useful for things like diarrhea or internal bleeding. Externally, it becomes what herbalists call a vulnerary—a wound healer. In fact, most vulneraries are astringent. Yarrow just happens to be a particularly effective one.

But Yarrow isn’t just astringent. It’s also bitter. Very bitter. And that bitterness isn’t just flavor—it’s function. Bitterness stimulates digestion. It wakes up the gut. It encourages appetite, bile flow, secretion. It cools, dries, and grounds the system. It even has a subtle downward pull—helping to move stagnation out of the body. Yarrow has a special affinity for the liver, gallbladder, and intestines. And thanks to its aromatic oils, it doesn’t just stimulate—it soothes too, making it a carminative. A digestive double threat.

Then there’s the pungency. The volatile oils. This is where Yarrow becomes a circulatory stimulant, especially for the venous side—the slower, more passive part of our bloodstream. It gets things moving. It’s no surprise then that herbalists reach for Yarrow in cases like varicose veins or hemorrhoids, where both stagnation and laxity are at play.

But the way Yarrow moves blood is what makes it really interesting.

It’s a diaphoretic, and not just any kind. A stimulant and relaxant diaphoretic. That’s rare. On one hand, it relaxes tension in the tiny blood vessels near the surface of the skin. On the other, it stimulates blood flow through the action of its essential oils. The result? It opens the pores, brings blood to the surface, and promotes sweating—especially during a fever. This is not just theoretical. This is why Yarrow has long been a go-to herb for flu, colds, and fevers that need to break.

Yarrow also acts as an emmenagogue—a mover of menstrual blood. It helps clear stagnation in the womb, promotes flow, and gets things moving. But here’s the twist: it also stops bleeding. It can bring on a delayed period and calm excessive flow. It does both, and the reason lies in its dual nature—bitter and pungent, drying and moving, toning and stimulating. There’s a paradox at the heart of Yarrow, and that’s what makes it so valuable.

Its influence on blood flow doesn’t stop there. Yarrow is also hypotensive. It relaxes blood vessels, tones their walls, and relieves the pressure building inside them. It won’t drop your blood pressure dramatically—but it will take the edge off.

Depending on how it’s prepared, Yarrow shifts again. A hot infusion tends to rise through the system, encouraging sweat. A cold extraction sends its action downward, through the kidneys, as a diuretic. This makes it especially helpful for urinary tract issues, particularly when paired with its anti-inflammatory and antiseptic powers.

Taken together, all these actions make Yarrow a classic alterative—a plant that gently nudges the body toward better balance by supporting elimination and fluid movement across multiple systems: liver, kidneys, skin, digestion.

And through it all, Yarrow remains an inflammation modulator. It cools and soothes. Burns. Wounds. Mucous membranes. Even joint inflammation from arthritis or autoimmune conditions. Herbalist Michael Moore put it plainly: Yarrow works for “low-level autoimmune or allergic conditions that settle in the joints.”

Finally, there’s the antiseptic action. The same volatile oils that make it pungent and stimulate circulation also combat microbes. Internally, externally, in wounds or infections—Yarrow disinfects.

If you look into herbal texts, you’ll find long, sprawling lists of Yarrow’s properties—sometimes overwhelming in their detail. But boil it down, and a few key roles emerge: bitter tonic, circulatory stimulant, astringent, and antiseptic. Those four give you the shape of Yarrow. Everything else is variation on a theme.

Affinities

If you want to understand a plant, look at where it goes in the body—what organs it gravitates toward, what tissues it speaks to, what systems it subtly shapes over time. With Yarrow, there’s a pattern that becomes impossible to ignore.

Let’s begin with the most obvious: blood.

Matthew Wood calls Yarrow the “master of the blood.” And that phrase lingers—because it’s true. Yarrow doesn’t just affect circulation. It orchestrates it. It can get the blood moving in cases of stagnation. But it can also do the opposite—staunch blood when it’s flowing too freely. In other words, it modulates. It chooses. It knows.

From there, it’s no surprise that Yarrow has a deep affinity for the cardiovascular system. This is a plant that tones vessels, opens capillaries, cools inflammation, and stimulates sluggish circulation. It works as a styptic and a vasodilator, as a tissue tonic and a circulatory stimulant. You’ll find it in many cardiovascular formulas—not because it does just one thing, but because it does so many.

Then there’s the digestive system. Yarrow has a knack for the liver and spleen, for the stomach, and particularly for issues tied to the hepatic portal vein—that quiet but critical highway between digestion and detox. Its bitterness wakes up the liver. Its aromatics soothe the gut. It moves stagnation and lifts congestion in a way that feels almost architectural—restoring flow and form to organs that have become bogged down.

The urogenital system offers another layer of complexity. Yarrow doesn’t take sides—it brings balance. It can stimulate menstruation. It can stop excessive flow. It tones tissues and kills bacteria. That dual ability—to cleanse and contain—is rare, and makes it a reliable ally in menstrual and urinary complaints alike.

Much of this, interestingly, comes back to its influence on the mucosa. Whether it’s in the gut, the womb, the urinary tract, or the skin, Yarrow has the same conversation with tissue: secrete when needed, tone when necessary. Its bitterness stimulates. Its astringency seals. The balance is everything.

And finally, there’s immunity. Yarrow doesn’t work like Echinacea or Elderberry—herbs that shout their immunostimulant roles from the rooftops. Yarrow is subtler. It supports immunity indirectly: by increasing circulation, calming inflammation, fighting microbes, and clearing out stagnation. It’s not a fire alarm. It’s the quiet repair crew behind the scenes.

So where does Yarrow go?

Blood. Vessels. Gut. Kidneys. Womb. Skin. All the messy, fluid places where things can either get stuck—or spill over. And Yarrow, time and again, finds the middle.

Energetics

Every plant tells a story, not just through what it treats, but how it treats. And with Yarrow, that story is one of paradox.

Start with the basics: temperature, moisture, tone. Yarrow is, unmistakably, cooling. That’s expected—it’s bitter, after all, and bitterness cools. But here’s where things get interesting. Yarrow also contains essential oils, the kind you’d normally associate with warming plants like thyme or oregano. Yet in Yarrow, those oils don’t heat—they cool. It’s one of the few aromatic plants that does this, and already, the contradictions begin to emerge.

Yarrow is also drying. It moves fluids out—through sweat, urine, and digestion. The astringency tightens tissues, seals leaky membranes, and reduces excess moisture. And yet, at the same time, Yarrow is also a relaxant. It softens tension in blood vessels, helping lower blood pressure and open up flow. So, which is it? Tonic or relaxant? The answer is both—but not in the same place. The tonic action targets the mucosa and skin. The relaxant action? That shows up in the vascular system.

This is the brilliance of Yarrow. Its energy profile—cooling, drying, astringent, relaxant, and stimulant—would seem self-contradictory. But inside the body, it unfolds in a coordinated way. A tightening here. A softening there. A nudge in one direction, a release in another.

And nowhere does this balancing act show up more clearly than in its effect on tissue states.

Let’s start with heat/excitation. This is what most people think of when they think of inflammation: red skin, rapid pulse, restlessness, high blood pressure. Heat in the body moves up and out, flaring like a fever. And Yarrow meets it head-on. It opens the pores, cools the surface, lowers tension, and brings the whole system back to center. You can feel this in the way it modulates blood pressure. It doesn’t sedate—it regulates.

But then there’s cold/depression. And this is where you’d expect a cooling plant like Yarrow to make things worse. Except—it doesn’t. Because Yarrow is also a stimulant. And cold/depression is, at its core, a state of inertia: tissues sluggish, waste trapped, circulation slow, vitality fading. In this state, Yarrow doesn’t cool—it moves. It pushes. It restores.

Even when dealing with infection, where cold and stagnation have let bacteria settle in, Yarrow’s ability to stimulate circulation and kill pathogens comes into play. It’s both invigorating and cleansing.

Next comes damp/relaxation—a tissue state that’s not swollen, but leaky. Fluids aren’t stuck; they’re spilling out. Membranes have lost their tone. Minerals, carried out with the fluid, are depleted. And that depletion often turns into tension—first physiological, then emotional. Yarrow tightens the tissues just enough to restore integrity. It seals. It holds. In the circulatory system, this becomes particularly useful for conditions like hemorrhoids or varicose veins, where the vessels have grown too lax to do their job properly.

Then there’s damp/stagnation, a kind of internal congestion where fluid gets trapped in tissues, unable to move. Here, Yarrow doesn’t seal—it dries. It stimulates. It circulates. The bitterness encourages secretion, the pungency promotes movement, the astringency clears excess.

And that’s the key: Yarrow isn’t stuck in one mode. It meets the tissue where it is. Hot? Cool it down. Stagnant? Move it along. Relaxed? Tighten. Depressed? Stimulate. Few plants navigate such a wide range of conditions with this kind of precision.

In a world that often divides herbs into neat categories—warming vs. cooling, stimulating vs. sedating—Yarrow refuses to be boxed in. It’s a plant of tensions, and its gift lies in knowing which to release, and which to restore.

Specific Indications

There’s a tendency in modern herbalism to sideline myth. To separate what a plant does from what it’s called, as if language and function operate in parallel lanes that never meet. But what if the name is a clue? What if the name of a plant, especially one passed down through centuries, carries something more than etymology?

Yarrow is one of those plants.

Its Latin name, Achillea millefolium, ties it directly to Achilles—the half-mortal, half-divine warrior of Homer’s Iliad. Invincible, except for the one place he wasn’t. A student of the centaur Chiron, Achilles wasn’t just a fighter—he was a healer. And according to legend, he used Yarrow to treat wounds on the battlefield. The name stuck. But so did the metaphor.

Because if you look closely, the myth of Achilles reveals a deeper truth about the plant that bears his name. It’s not just a wound healer in the physical sense. It’s a plant that speaks to something much older and more personal—the wound we all carry that doesn’t bleed, but hurts.

To understand this, you have to shift your gaze to Chiron—the mentor, the immortal teacher, the one who was pierced by a poisoned arrow and left with a wound that would never heal. Chiron, master of medicine, couldn’t cure himself. And so he did something remarkable: he offered up his immortality in exchange for the freedom of another—Prometheus, the rebel who gave fire to humanity and suffered for it.

This is the story of sacrifice. Of transformation. Of wounds that become thresholds.

And that’s where Yarrow lives—at the edge between pain and growth.

You could call it the plant of the wounded warrior. Or perhaps more precisely, the plant of the wounded healer. Because most of us aren’t just trying to recover from scraped knees or tension headaches. We’re trying to come to terms with the kind of wound that lives deeper—in the psyche, the soul. The kind of wound we spend our lives circling, avoiding, denying.

Yarrow doesn’t erase that wound. What it offers instead is courage. It helps us turn toward the places in ourselves we fear the most. The shadows. The traumas. The betrayals. The inherited pain. And slowly—quietly—it begins to transform them.

Blame becomes self-responsibility. Judgment becomes compassion. Poison becomes medicine. Weakness becomes strength.

In this way, Yarrow’s work is not just physical—it’s archetypal. It speaks to the person who is perpetually “cut to the bone,” worn down, stuck in cycles of criticism and resentment, unable to move forward because the wound still festers. Yarrow helps break that cycle. Not by numbing or escaping, but by helping us integrate the pain, draw wisdom from it, and become something more.

That’s why I consider Yarrow not only a universal medicine for the body—but also for the soul.

Every healer, at some point, must come to know this plant. Not just intellectually. But personally. It teaches us how to heal others by first healing ourselves. And it reminds us that our greatest strength may live in the very place we once thought was our greatest weakness.

Yarrow teaches that our wounds are not the end of our story. They are the beginning of transformation.

Clinical Patterns

1.Bleeding: If you had to choose one plant—just one—for a first aid kit, you could make a strong case for Yarrow.

Not because it does one thing particularly well. But because it does everything a wound needs—at every stage of healing. It’s a styptic, yes. It stops bleeding. But that’s only the beginning.

Yarrow is famous for its ability to staunch bleeding both externally and internally. From paper cuts to hemorrhages, nosebleeds to arterial wounds, this plant has a long history of rising to the moment. In folk medicine, it’s the plant you chew and slap on a wound in the field. No sterile gauze. No antiseptic swabs. Just the bitter, bristly leaf, doing its quiet, effective work.

Matthew Wood once told a story about someone who accidentally sliced their leg open with a chainsaw. It was a deep, serious wound. They packed it with Yarrow—and it worked. The bleeding stopped. The healing began. That’s not a miracle. That’s biochemistry meeting tradition.

In fact, Wood later recounted a curious phrase he once heard: Yarrow works “on the third level of the blood.” It didn’t make sense to him at first. But consider this: the vascular system has layers. Capillaries. Arterioles. Arteries. Most herbs that affect bleeding act at the capillary level—small cuts, surface wounds. But Yarrow? It goes deeper. It has a reputation for working even on arterial bleeding—where blood is under pressure, surging with each heartbeat. That’s not common. That’s extraordinary.

Let’s break down wound healing into four phases. Yarrow plays a role in every one.

Hemostasis — stopping the bleeding. That’s Yarrow’s signature.

Inflammation — the body’s way of summoning help to the site. Yarrow modulates it, so it’s effective but not excessive.

Proliferation — where tissue rebuilds, forming white granules of collagen and new blood vessels. Yarrow promotes this regeneration.

Remodeling — where scar tissue refines itself over time. Yarrow’s tissue-toning and circulatory properties support this phase too.

In other words, Yarrow doesn’t just stop bleeding. It carries the wound through the full cycle of repair.

How you use it matters. For fresh wounds, the fresh leaf is best—chewed and packed directly into the wound. For nosebleeds, a rolled-up leaf in the nostril works wonders. For deeper cuts, dried Yarrow can be rehydrated as a fomentation or poultice—but avoid powdered herbs. They can cause irritation, even trigger more inflammation by mimicking debris.

For wound cleansing, a strained tea of Yarrow leaf or flower is ideal. It’s antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and helps soften the tissue, making it easier to clear out debris and promote healing.

And Yarrow isn’t limited to the skin. It can be used internally, too. Think of oral wounds—like bleeding gums from flossing or the risk of dry socket after a tooth extraction. A Yarrow rinse can stop bleeding, fight infection, and ease pain. Michael Moore even recommended chewing the root—fresh or soaked in brandy—for sore gums and toothaches. Crude, yes. But incredibly effective.

All of this makes Yarrow what you might call a self-contained formula. A plant that disinfects, stops bleeding, tones tissue, promotes circulation, and guides the inflammatory response—all in one.

There are pharmaceuticals that do one or two of those things. But one that does them all?

You’d be hard-pressed to find it.

Unless you already have Yarrow in your kit.

2. Fever, Cold, Flu: Fever is one of the most misunderstood responses in the human body.

We think of it as a problem. A malfunction. Something to be suppressed, controlled, brought down. But from an herbalist’s perspective, that’s not how it works. Fever isn’t the enemy—it’s the intelligence of the body doing its job. Raising the temperature just enough to cook out an invading pathogen. It’s our immune system’s most ancient and elegant tool. And when you interfere too early—when you suppress it—you interrupt that process. A two-day cold becomes a ten-day flu.

So what if, instead of shutting the fever down, you supported it? This is where Yarrow comes in.

Yarrow is one of the most dependable diaphoretics in the Western herbal tradition. And not just any diaphoretic—Yarrow is both a relaxant and a stimulant. That combination is rare. It means Yarrow can help a fever do what it’s trying to do—whether it’s stuck in first gear or revving too hot.

Simon Mills, one of the great British herbalists, put it simply: “Yarrow may be used as the central ingredient in any fever-management program.” Its effects, he says, are quick. Reliable. And remarkably broad.

You see, Yarrow doesn’t just push heat out through the skin. It improves circulation, strengthens the immune response, fights pathogens, and cools the blood itself. It’s that cooling and stimulating dual action that makes it so versatile. It works in stage one of a fever (when tension and chills dominate) and stage two (when the body is hot and restless and ready to sweat).

This is why Yarrow sits at the center of a classic fever trio—alongside Peppermint and Elderflower. Together, they relax the surface, open the pores, and guide the heat up and out. It’s a simple combination, but it works.

And Yarrow’s reach goes beyond fevers. The same volatile oils that move blood and lower heat also help clear the lungs, relieve sinus congestion, and reduce inflammation. Just inhaling the steam from a hot infusion can open the airways. It’s antiseptic. It’s calming. And it goes deeper.

Two of Yarrow’s aromatic constituents—azulene and chamazulene—give its essential oil that unusual deep blue color. But more importantly, they are potent anti-inflammatories. This is why even the steam of the tea, let alone the liquid, can offer topical relief to irritated mucous membranes.

Matthew Wood uses Yarrow for everything from stuffed sinuses to bronchitis, pleurisy, and pneumonia. Even bleeding from the lungs or night sweats from tuberculosis. For those last two? He recommends a cold infusion—a gentler way to extract the plant’s properties.

And then there’s the form.

Yarrow is best taken hot when treating a fever. A hot infusion naturally supports the diaphoretic effect—not just because of the herb, but because the heat itself moves the body in the right direction. But if all you have is a tincture, there’s still a way: put the drops into hot water. The heat will activate the aromatic compounds, helping to stimulate the sweat and move the fever forward.

Micheal Moore, another trusted voice in the field, suggests pairing Yarrow with Chamomile, Fennel, or Anise—especially at the first sign of a cold. This combination can be sweetened with honey and made more palatable, even for children. The added herbs soften Yarrow’s intense bitterness while maintaining its potency.

Because that’s the thing about Yarrow. It isn’t gentle. It’s effective. But when used at the right moment, in the right way, it helps the body do what it already knows how to do—just faster, smarter, and with far less drama.

A fever, in the end, is not a sign of failure. It’s a sign of strength. And Yarrow is the herb that knows how to listen to that strength—and guide it.

3. Digestive Conditions: There’s a simple way to understand the role of Yarrow in digestion: it moves things forward.

Not just in the literal sense—though it does that, too—but in the way it clears stagnation, wakes up the system, and reminds the body how to function as a whole. It begins with the taste. Bitter. That sharp edge on the tongue is not incidental—it’s a signal. One that reaches deep into the digestive tract, down to the liver, stomach, and intestines, switching on the secretions and stirring the fire of metabolism.

As a bitter tonic, Yarrow stimulates appetite, supports digestion, and helps the gut let go of what it’s holding. But its reach is broader than most bitters. Yarrow doesn’t just stimulate secretions. It also moves venous blood through the hepatic portal system, improving the delivery of nutrients from the gut to the liver. It cools heat, disperses congestion, and eases nervous tension held in the abdomen—a tension that often goes unspoken, but lives in the gut all the same.

Matthew Wood puts it this way: “As a bitter, it increases secretion in the digestion, and with its ability to decongest the internal organs when blood is collecting in the interior, it brings blood to the surface and improves digestion and assimilation.” In other words, Yarrow doesn’t just nudge digestion along—it restores its rhythm.

Now, most bitter herbs are cooling. But with Yarrow, the pattern gets more complex. It contains pungent aromatic oils, which bring in warming, moving, carminative properties. That’s the paradox again—warming and cooling. It’s not either/or. It’s both.

And that dual action gives Yarrow a unique edge.

As a carminative, it soothes cramping, dispels gas, and settles the gut. And as an astringent, it tones loose tissues—particularly useful in cases of chronic diarrhea, bowel relaxation, or leaky gut, where the lining of the intestines has lost its structural integrity.

Yarrow tightens the seams. Strengthens the wall. Seals the barrier.

It’s also anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and hemostatic—an all-in-one plant for when the digestive system is inflamed, infected, or bleeding. Michael Moore saw its application clearly. He recommended Yarrow for stomach ulcers, for recovery from intestinal flu, and for the lingering aftermath of dysentery. He even noted its effectiveness against Shigella, the bacterial infection behind shigellosis, especially when paired with Echinacea.

That trifecta—anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, astringent—makes Yarrow well-suited for an array of digestive challenges: ulcers, infections, dysbiosis, diverticulitis, leaky gut, prolapse.

It brings tone. It brings clarity. And above all, it restores the gut’s forgotten intelligence—one bitter, pungent sip at a time.

4. Circulatory Conditions: Yarrow has a way of making opposites work together. Take the vascular system. On one hand, it tones and astringes blood vessels, tightening lax tissue in the peripheral capillaries and veins. On the other, it’s a vasodilator, opening those same vessels to let more blood move through. The result is a kind of dynamic balance: increased tone and integrity, yet greater flow. More nutrients. More oxygen. Less stagnation.

This makes Yarrow an ideal remedy for a wide spectrum of circulatory issues—varicose veins, hypotension, hypertension, thrombosis, or any condition marked by cold/depression or damp stagnation. In British herbalism, it’s often paired with Hawthorn berry (Crataegus spp.), a combination aimed at restoring both structure and vitality to the cardiovascular system.

But the picture widens. Circulatory health isn’t just about moving sluggish blood—it’s also about cooling excessive heat. Inflammation and irritation in the vascular system, left unchecked, lead to oxidative stress. Over time, this weakens vessel walls, prompting the body to respond with LDL cholesterol—its own repair material—to patch tiny “nicks” in the endothelium. Yarrow’s cooling flavonoids help prevent that cascade by reducing inflammation, astringing those micro-injuries, and restoring tone before damage accumulates.

And then there are hemorrhoids—a condition often dismissed as purely digestive because of their location, but in reality a circulatory issue. At their core, they’re a problem of venous relaxation and stagnation in the liver and portal circulation. Yarrow addresses both. The tannins tighten tissues, stop bleeding, and reduce inflammation and pain. The bitters clear liver congestion. The circulatory stimulation moves pooled blood out of the area.

Michael Moore noted that two to three cups a day of Yarrow tea could help shrink mild hemorrhoids and polyps. If they’re protruding, a sitz bath with Yarrow and other astringents—Oak, Bayberry, Witch Hazel—can offer relief. A topical oil or salve works too, but the real change comes when you treat the problem internally, not just locally. That’s where Yarrow’s value becomes clear—it doesn’t just address the symptom, it treats systemic vascular relaxation, changing the underlying pattern.

Yarrow doesn’t just work on the blood vessels. It works with them—tightening where they’re too loose, opening where they’re too tight, cooling where they’re overheated, and moving where they’ve gone still.

5. Urogenital Conditions: Yarrow’s work in the urogenital system is a study in dualities. It moves blood when it’s stagnant, tones tissue when it’s slack, and cools heat when it flares—often doing all three in the same person.

As an emmenagogue and female tonic, it has the unusual ability to both loosen and tighten. Menstrual cramps and premenstrual tension often come from congestion—liver stagnation, poor pelvic circulation, blood pooling where it shouldn’t. Here, Yarrow’s bitterness clears the liver, disperses stagnant blood, and relaxes the grip of tension. But shift the pattern—swap congestion for weakness—and Yarrow changes its stance. Its astringency tones an overly lax uterus, strengthens pelvic circulation, and addresses the kind of uterine prolapse where muscle and mucosa have gone flabby, unresponsive.

This is not just about comfort. A uterus without tone can’t generate the contractions needed to fully expel the endometrium and menstrual blood. Things slow down. They linger. And Yarrow, unlike many uterine tonics, doesn’t just firm the tissue—it also actively stimulates menstruation. That makes it valuable for opposite ends of the spectrum: amenorrhea (absence of menstruation), menorrhagia (heavy bleeding), even vaginal leucorrhea (persistent discharge).

Its role extends to uterine fibroids, especially when bleeding is bright red and excessive. Again, it’s the same pattern: Yarrow identifies the imbalance and works to restore the original rhythm—whether the problem is too much flow or too little.

The urinary tract tells a similar story. Yarrow’s value here lies in its ability to address infection and inflammation in a way that’s both direct and adaptive. Burning urination? It cools the heat. Pathogens? Its antiseptic compounds act against Candida albicans, Escherichia coli, Streptococcus faecalis—organisms commonly tied to UTIs. Bleeding? Its astringency seals the vessels.

Michael Moore used it for chronic cystitis and urethritis, even when the prostate was involved. Stephen Harrod Buhner notes its strength when tissues are loose, heavy, dragging—paired with heat, irritation, and sometimes incontinence. For men with urinary difficulty from a swollen prostate, Yarrow combines well with Cornsilk, balancing tone with ease.

Here, the form matters. In urinary tract conditions, an infusion is more than just a delivery system for Yarrow’s compounds—the large volume of water acts as a diuretic, flushing the system as the herb works. The bitterness, the astringency, the antiseptic action—they all weave together.

And the result, once again, is what Yarrow always seems to deliver: a return to balance, no matter which way the system has tipped.

6. Other Uses: Not all of Yarrow’s history is about wounds, fevers, or infections. Some of it is about beer.

Carl Linnaeus, the father of modern taxonomy, recorded an unusual tradition in the Swedish province of Dalecarlia. At weddings, the locals sometimes brewed their ale not with hops, but with Yarrow. The effect, he claimed, was unmistakable: “so that the guests become crazy.” He called the plant galentara—literally “causing madness”—and described it as stirring up the blood and making one lose balance.

This wasn’t just folklore. Linnaeus suggested that Yarrow, when combined with alcohol, intensified the effects—producing “complete and immediate intoxication,” and even “special sensations” beyond what the alcohol alone could achieve. It wasn’t a neutral additive. It was an amplifier.

Stephen Harrod Buhner traces this brewing tradition across Scandinavia and beyond. In Norway and Denmark, Yarrow was used both on its own and in combination with other herbs like St. John’s Wort and Juniper—sometimes with malt, sometimes without, to create a potent fermented drink. Across the region, its brewing role is echoed in its names: jorhumle (“earth hop”) in Scandinavia, backhumle, akerhumle, and skoghumle in Denmark (humle meaning “hop”).

The practice wasn’t confined to the North. Maude Grieve noted that Yarrow was said to have similar uses in parts of Africa. And while hops eventually took over as the dominant bittering agent in beer, Yarrow never disappeared entirely from brewing. In parts of Europe and Scandinavia, it’s still used in ales today.

Yarrow’s long association with beer and ale complicates its image. Here’s a plant that heals wounds, staunches bleeding, breaks fevers—and, under the right circumstances, fuels celebration, lowers inhibitions, and quickens the blood. In other words, Yarrow has always had more than one way of altering the human state.

Pharmacological Data

Yarrow’s long list of medicinal actions doesn’t happen by magic. It happens by chemistry—an intricate mix of compounds, each with its own role, working together in ways that explain why this plant is both so versatile and so paradoxical.

Start with the volatile oils: borneol, camphor, thujone, chamazulene, azulene, linalool, limonene, cineole. These give Yarrow much of its aromatic character, but they’re not just for scent. Borneol is antimicrobial. Cineole is antispasmodic, carminative, and antiseptic. Azulene and chamazulene—the same blue pigments found in chamomile—are soothing, anti-allergenic, and inflammation modulating. Even the warm, resinous camphor plays a role in circulation and respiratory health.

Then there are the sesquiterpene lactones—bitter, complex molecules that are inflammation modulating and antimicrobial. Alongside them, tannins—both condensed and hydrolyzable—bring Yarrow its astringency, tightening tissues and contributing to its cooling energetics.

Yarrow’s flavonoids—apigenin, luteolin, rutin—are both antispasmodic and anti-inflammatory, explaining part of its calming effect on smooth muscle. Its furanocoumarins act as anticoagulants, influencing blood flow and clotting. And coumarins themselves are anti-thrombotic, helping reduce high blood pressure by preventing excessive clot formation.

The plant’s alkaloids—achilletin, betonicine, stachydrine, trigonelline—open an entirely different chapter. Betonicine, stachydrine, and trigonelline are antipyretic and hypotensive—fever-lowering and blood pressure-reducing. Achilletin goes further: soothing the digestive system, relieving intestinal spasms, promoting bile flow, fighting bacterial invasion, and tightening tissues all at once.

Add to that alkamides for their anti-inflammatory punch, salicylic acid for pain relief and astringency, and nutrients like ascorbic acid (vitamin C) and folic acid for their supportive roles. Even the bitters—like achillein—deserve their own mention, for the way they spark digestion and liver activity.

Layer by layer, the picture emerges: a plant that isn’t powered by a single “active ingredient” but by a network of compounds—each contributing its own note to the whole. Anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial. Astringent and relaxing. Cooling and stimulating.

And that, chemically speaking, is why Yarrow so often seems to do two opposite things at once—and do them both well.

Safety

For all its range and power, Yarrow is, by herbal standards, remarkably safe. But even the safest plants have their boundaries.

The most obvious one is pregnancy. As an emmenagogue, Yarrow can stimulate menstrual flow, which makes it a poor choice for anyone carrying a child. That’s a clear line.

There’s also a note about photosensitivity—the idea that long-term use might make the skin more sensitive to sunlight. In practice, this rarely becomes an issue, because Yarrow isn’t usually taken as a single herb for long stretches. It’s more often used for short-term, acute conditions, or as part of a formula where its dose is lower.

Then there’s its effect on digestion. Yarrow increases gut motility—a plus if you’re sluggish, but a potential minus if you’re taking medications that need time to absorb. Faster movement through the intestines can mean less time for the drug to enter the bloodstream.

And of course, there are allergies. Yarrow is a member of the Asteraceae family, and for a small number of people, that’s a problem. I learned this the hard way, harvesting Yarrow flowers on the way home from a road trip. We loaded the car, and almost immediately, two of my friends started sneezing and sniffling non-stop. By the time we reached home, they were miserable—and not particularly happy with me.

On the energetic side, Yarrow is as complex as we’ve seen throughout this profile: stimulant and relaxant, astringent and tonic, warming and cooling, drying. That versatility makes it, in Ayurvedic terms, tri-doshic—capable of working with any of the three doshas. It can relax vascular tension in vata, cleanse stagnation and tighten laxity in kapha, and reduce heat and irritation in pitta.

Still, there’s a pattern worth noting: over the long term, Yarrow’s net cooling, drying, and astringent effects are more likely to aggravate vata.

As for herb-drug interactions? None are known. But as with any plant this active, “safe” doesn’t mean “without boundaries.” It just means that when you understand how it works—and when to set it aside—you can use it with confidence.

Doses and Preparation

Part of Yarrow’s appeal is how many different ways you can use it—internally, externally, fresh, dried, as oil, tea, tincture, or poultice. Few herbs are this adaptable.

Infused oil and salve. The freshly dried flowers infuse beautifully into carrier oils like cold-pressed olive. Steep them—about 1 part Yarrow to 7 parts oil—for a few weeks in a warm, dark place. The oil can be used directly on the skin or turned into a salve with beeswax, other infused oils, and essential oils. The result is a broad-spectrum topical remedy for rheumatic joints, varicose veins, bruises, aches, and small cuts.

Infusion. For tea, use 1 tablespoon fresh leaf and flower—or 2 teaspoons dried—per cup of water. Steep for 20 minutes. Cover the pot or cup to trap the volatile compounds. The taste is bitter, so honey helps if you need it. Drink hot for fever or urinary tract infection. The same tea works externally for washing wounds, soaking compresses, or preparing fomentations—cloths soaked in the infusion and wrapped around an injured area. Water extracts tend to be more astringent than tinctures, thanks to the tannins being primarily water-soluble.

Tincture. Yarrow’s flowers make an excellent tincture. Fresh flowers, ground and extracted in 60–70% alcohol, yield a fragrant, volatile-rich liquid. Dried flowers work with 50% alcohol. This range captures essential oils, tannins, and a good share of alkaloids, with the plant’s own water diluting the alcohol naturally during maceration. The tincture works internally and as a topical liniment for first aid. Dosage: 10–40 drops, up to five times a day.

Poultice. The oldest, simplest way to use Yarrow for bleeding is as a poultice. Pick fresh leaves, chew them into a mash, and apply directly to the wound. This “spit poultice” works fast, and when someone’s bleeding heavily, speed matters. If chewing feels a step too far, grind the leaves with a little water. No fresh plant? Hydrate dried Yarrow, wrap in gauze or cloth, and place it directly over the wound.

In each form, Yarrow delivers its complex chemistry—bitters, tannins, volatile oils, alkaloids—through a different doorway. Whether it’s steeped in oil, brewed into tea, extracted in alcohol, or crushed into a poultice, the core actions remain the same: tone, move, cool, and heal.

Formulation

The British Herbal Pharmacopoeia has a habit of pairing Yarrow with other plants to amplify its strengths. It’s not a soloist here—it’s a collaborator.

For fevers, it’s Yarrow with Elderflower and Peppermint—a trio as classic as any in Western herbalism. For hypertension, it’s Yarrow with Limeflowers. For coronary thrombosis, Yarrow with Nettles. Each pairing builds on a specific aspect of Yarrow’s nature, steering it toward the condition at hand.

The classic European Diaphoretic Tea for fevers is simple and effective:

1 part Peppermint (Mentha piperita)

1 part Elderflower (Sambucus spp.)

1 part Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

Fill a 1 litre jar halfway with dried herbs, add boiling water, cover, and steep for 20 minutes. Drink hot—ideally while sitting in a hot bath. It’s a formula that doesn’t just work—it works in a way you can feel, moving heat and breaking the fever.

The Influenza Formula comes from W. Burns Lingard, an English herbalist from the 1950s. It builds on the same base trio, adding two potent allies:

4 parts Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

3 parts Peppermint (Mentha piperita)

2 parts Elderflower (Sambucus spp.)

2 parts Angelica (Angelica archangelica)

1 part Boneset (Eupatorium perfoliatum)

Angelica drives blood circulation with its pungent aromatics, while Boneset offers a strong bitter, relaxant diaphoretic effect. Both bring antiseptic qualities. Together, the five herbs create a fever blend that’s more forceful, more comprehensive, and better suited for stubborn cases.

For broader infection support, there’s the Anti-Septic/Immunity Formula:

30% Echinacea root & flower (E. purpurea, E. angustifolia)

15% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

15% Boneset (Eupatorium perfoliatum)

10% Garlic (Allium sativa)

10% Lomatium (Lomatium dissectum)

10% Uva-Ursi (Arctostaphylos uva-ursi)

10% Wild Indigo (Baptisia tinctoria)

Echinacea leads, stimulating immune function. Yarrow and Boneset add diaphoretic and antimicrobial support. Garlic cleanses the blood, stimulates circulation, and works antiseptically in the lungs, gut, and urinary tract. Lomatium is specific for viral respiratory infections, Uva-Ursi for urinary tract infections, and Wild Indigo for broad-spectrum antisepsis with a strong lymphatic effect. It’s a “shotgun formula”—less about pinpoint precision, more about covering all bases when speed matters.

Dosage: For acute infection, 1 dropperful every 1–2 hours. After symptoms ease, 1 dropperful three times a day. Adjust upward if needed.

Then there’s the Urinary Tract Infection Formula—broad-spectrum antiseptic with a urinary tract focus:

20% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

20% Uva-Ursi (Arctostaphylos uva-ursi)

20% Usnea (Usnea spp.)

20% Oregon Grape (Mahonia aquifolium)

15% Cornsilk (Zea mays)

5% Juniper (Juniperus communis)

Uva-Ursi brings arbutin, a urinary-specific antiseptic. Yarrow and Juniper contribute aromatic antimicrobials excreted through the kidneys. Oregon Grape’s berberine reaches the urinary tract via the bloodstream. Usnea’s usnic acid offers broad-spectrum antisepsis for kidneys, lungs, and gut. Cornsilk softens the formula’s drying edge, soothing the bladder and ureters.

Because this blend can be drying and potentially irritating, adequate water intake is essential. It pairs well with Marshmallow root infusion for extra demulcent support.

Dosage: For acute infection, 1 dropperful every 1–2 hours. Continue with 1 dropperful three times daily for several days after symptoms clear. Best taken in a glass of room-temperature water, all at once, to deliver the medicine directly to the urinary tract.

Some herbal combinations have the elegance of simplicity—just two plants, doing one job better together than either could alone.

The Hemostatic Pair is one of them:

50% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

50% Shepherd’s Purse (Capsella bursa-pastoris)

Both are among the strongest astringents in Western herbalism, making them exceptional at staunching bleeding—whether it’s from childbirth-related uterine hemorrhage or any other kind of internal bleeding. They tone tissue, tighten vessels, and slow the loss of blood quickly. In a discipline where formulas often contain a half-dozen herbs or more, this pairing is refreshingly—and powerfully—minimal.

Then there’s the All Purpose Salve—a “herbalism 101” garden classic that works because every ingredient earns its place:

20% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

20% Calendula (Calendula officinalis)

20% Plantain (Plantago major)

20% Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)

20% St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum)

This is a vulnerary blend—meant to promote wound healing. It calms mild inflammation, stops minor bleeding, improves local circulation, and guards against infection with antimicrobial and antibacterial properties. The caveat: Comfrey heals skin fast. Too fast. On deeper lacerations or puncture wounds, it can seal the surface before the underlying tissue has a chance to breathe, trapping bacteria inside. Leaving Comfrey out makes the formula safer for deeper injuries, without sacrificing much effectiveness. Dosage: Apply as needed.

For wound cleansing, a simple Wound Wash does the job:

1 part Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

1 part Calendula flowers (Calendula officinalis)

1 part Plantain leaf (Plantago major)

Steep 1–2 tablespoons of the combined herbs per cup of water—scale up as needed. Strain, and use as a fomentation, compress, soak, or wash for wounds. It works equally well for sore, inflamed joints.

Then, for hemorrhoids, there’s the Sitz Bath Formula:

1 part Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

1 part Horse Chestnut (Aesculus hippocastanum)

1 part Oak Bark (Quercus robur)

1 part Calendula (Calendula officinalis)

1 part Plantain (Plantago major)

1 part Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia) or 1–3 drops lavender essential oil

Steep into a strong tea, then soak for 15–20 minutes. You can do this in a full bathtub or a smaller basin just deep enough to sit in. The combination tightens blood vessels, reduces inflammation, soothes tissue, and supports healing—all in one treatment.

Some formulas work because they target a symptom directly. Others work because they address the underlying pattern that causes the symptom in the first place. These hemorrhoid and cardiovascular blends do both.

The Internal Formula for Hemorrhoids from Thomas Bartrum is simple and precise:

1 part Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

1 part Witch Hazel leaves (Hamamelis virginiana)

1 part German Chamomile (Matricaria recutita)

Dosage: 1–2 teaspoons per cup of boiling water, infuse for 15 minutes. Take ½–1 cup three times daily before meals.

This is an astringent and inflammation-modulating formula. Witch Hazel tightens vessels and stops excess bleeding, while Chamomile and Yarrow—both aromatic bitters with the blue essential oils azulene and chamazulene—calm inflammation. Their combined bitter, carminative qualities also support digestion, an often-overlooked factor in hemorrhoid care.

Hemorrhoid Formula #2 pushes the astringent action further:

20% Horse Chestnut (Aesculus hippocastanum)

20% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

20% Red Root (Ceanothus velutinus)

20% Calendula (Calendula officinalis)

20% Sage (Salvia officinalis)

I used this recently for a friend whose hemorrhoids had been worsening despite a week on over-the-counter ointment. Purple, swollen, hot, burning, itchy—painful enough to disrupt sleep and sitting. Within two days of starting this formula, the swelling had dropped significantly; by day five, symptoms were gone; by day seven, the area was back to normal.

This blend is deeply astringent, but it’s also designed to move circulation through the entire portal-liver-pelvic region. Sage and Horse Chestnut are pungent circulatory stimulants as well as astringents. Yarrow and Sage, with their slight oiliness, help move the liver. Red Root tones the portal vein and intestinal tract. Calendula cools inflammation, moves the liver, and stimulates digestion. Yarrow and Calendula add bitterness for the GI tract, while Sage’s carminative quality supports overall digestive ease.

The Cardiovascular Tonic Formula takes Yarrow’s circulatory gifts and applies them to heart health:

30% Hawthorn berry (Crataegus monogyna)

20% Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

15% Linden (Tilia europaea)

15% Motherwort (Leonurus cardiaca)

10% Reishi (Ganoderma lucidum)

5% Lobelia (Lobelia inflata)

5% Cayenne (Capsicum annuum)

This is a general cardiovascular tonic. Hawthorn strengthens the heart muscle and vessels. Yarrow tones and opens the circulation. Linden, Motherwort, and Reishi bring gentle, heart-specific nervine support. Lobelia and Cayenne are the drivers—Cayenne stimulating circulation, Lobelia dilating vessels. The result is a formula that strengthens, relaxes, and nourishes the cardiovascular system while keeping blood flow smooth and steady.
