Damiana (Turnera diffusa)
We often think we know what a plant is for. Take Damiana, for example. In the world of Western herbalism, it tends to come with a label already attached: aphrodisiac. That’s the first thing you hear. The defining feature. The headline.
But that’s not the whole story.
Damiana—Turnera diffusa, also known as T. aphrodisiaca—belongs to the Passifloraceae family (once classified as Turneraceae). You’ll find it growing in the warm, sun-drenched landscapes of South and Central America, throughout western Mexico, and even across the dry regions of Texas, Arizona, and California. A hardy, bush-like shrub, it can reach up to six feet in height, producing bright yellow flowers that seem almost too vivid for such an arid climate.
It’s the leaves and flowers that herbalists use. These are the parts that hold the plant’s medicinal power. And yes, that power includes its famous reputation as an aphrodisiac. You’ll find Damiana infused into chocolates, blended into liqueurs, and tucked into so-called love potions. In that context, it’s all about desire. But what kind?
That’s the more interesting question.
Because when you look closer—when you move past the marketing and the mystique—you find that Damiana’s influence runs deeper. It’s not just about arousal in the narrow sense. It’s about connection. The kind of connection that shapes how we relate to others. How we feel in our own skin. How we navigate the complicated terrain of intimacy, not just in the bedroom, but in conversation, in presence, in being seen.
And then there’s everything else.
Damiana has a wider range of actions—some that affect mood, some that influence other body systems, all pointing toward the conclusion that this isn’t a one-note herb. It’s not just about stimulation or seduction. It’s about relationship. With others, yes—but also with ourselves.
It’s tempting to reduce herbs to their most popular uses. But Damiana resists that. It asks for a deeper conversation. And if we’re paying attention, it offers one.

There’s a kind of contradiction at the heart of Damiana. One that you can taste.
It’s floral. Intensely so. Resinous, aromatic, with a surprising hint of bitterness. When you brew it into a formula, it doesn’t sit politely in the background—it announces itself. That bright, perfumed flavor has a way of taking over the entire blend. Which is why, more often than not, herbalists use it sparingly—no more than 10% of a formula. A little goes a long way.
But that strong, unmistakable aromatic profile is more than a flavor note. It tells us something about the plant’s actions—warming, stimulating, and relaxing all at once. And here’s where things get even more interesting.
Damiana doesn’t fit neatly into one category. It’s a stimulant. And a relaxant. It tones down nervous tension while gently perking up the mind. It soothes the system even as it wakes it up.
That duality—calm and alert, grounded and energized—is part of what makes Damiana such a fascinating aphrodisiac. It stimulates blood flow to the pelvic region. It heightens sensitivity. But it also brings a sense of ease. Comfort. A kind of embodied presence. And when you think about it, isn’t that the perfect state to be in for intimacy? You want to be relaxed, but not sleepy. Stimulated, but not jittery. Present, but not tense.
This applies to all bodies—male, female, and beyond.
Its nervine actions reflect the same paradox. Damiana reduces excitability, yes, but it also brightens the mind, clears mental fog, lifts melancholy. In this way, it behaves less like coffee or energy drinks and more like Rosemary (Salvia rosmarinus): a circulatory stimulant for the brain and pelvis. One that sharpens cognition while soothing frazzled nerves.
That’s the core of how Damiana is used. But it doesn’t stop there.
Its effect on pelvic circulation gives it an emmenagogue action—it can support menstruation. That same regional affinity extends to the urinary tract, where it offers a mild diuretic effect. It even contains small amounts of arbutin—the same compound found in Uva-Ursi and Pipsissewa—which offers a urinary tract antiseptic action.
There’s more. Damiana has been used as a respiratory remedy as well—particularly for asthma, constricted bronchioles, and damp coughs. Likely, this is due to its rich aromatic profile, which, as many herbalists know, often signals an affinity for the lungs.
And then there’s the thyroid.
In some cases, Damiana has been shown to mildly stimulate thyroid activity. It’s unclear whether this happens through central mechanisms—like influencing TSH from the brain—or through direct thyroid stimulation. But clinically, it can offer gentle support for underactive thyroid function.
From an energetic standpoint, the same volatile oils that give Damiana its intense flavor also warm the body, stimulate circulation, and release tension. The result is an herb that is warming, drying, and balancing—especially when cold, damp, or tense patterns are in play.
In terms of tissue states, Damiana shines where wind/tension and cold/depression overlap. It may not be a classic spasmolytic, but it does help relax the bronchioles and the nervous system. For someone who is mentally wound up but physically cold and stagnant, Damiana brings a kind of equilibrium. It softens the mind while rekindling the fire.
And from the Ayurvedic lens? It’s warming and pungent. Which means it reduces vata and kapha, but can push pitta higher. That makes it ideal for calming anxiety and mental overactivity (vata), while also energizing sluggishness (kapha). But in hot constitutions, it might be a little too much.
So what is Damiana?
It’s an herb of paradoxes. Of tension held gently in balance. And sometimes, that’s exactly what the body—and the heart—needs.
Specific Indications
There’s a Sanskrit term in Ayurveda—prabhava. It refers to a substance’s unique or special effect. Something that can’t quite be explained by its taste, its energy, or even its pharmacology. Every once in a while, you find an herb with a prabhava that makes you pause. Damiana is one of them.
Its first signature quality is something we’ve already begun to see: that rare pairing of stimulation and relaxation in an aphrodisiac. Most herbs in this category pick a side. They stimulate circulation. Or they calm the nervous system. But not both. Damiana does both. And it’s not just a theoretical contradiction—it’s something you can feel.
The second piece is even more telling.
Damiana isn’t simply for “low libido.” That phrase is too flat, too generic. What Damiana really speaks to is a certain kind of loss of desire—one that comes not from the body, but from the mind. Think: mental fatigue, emotional burnout, neurasthenia, depression. In these cases, the spark doesn’t go out entirely—it just gets buried. And that’s where Damiana shines.
It’s for the person who wants to want, but can’t quite get there.
Maybe they’re anxious. Restless. Living in their head. That’s the excess vata picture. Or maybe they feel heavy, sad, shut down. That’s kapha. Either way, something is getting in the way—creating a kind of psychological block between desire and embodiment. Between thought and sensation. Between connection and response.
Which brings us to the deeper emotional picture.
Damiana is for the fear of intimacy—not just sexual intimacy, but emotional closeness. For the person who can’t seem to sink into their own body. Where the mind is ready, even willing, but the body isn’t responding. Or the opposite: the body wants to respond, but the mind is full of noise, hesitation, disconnection.
In that space—between desire and doubt, between presence and withdrawal—Damiana offers a bridge.
It’s particularly well-suited for those who have pulled away from others, who feel isolated or alone, even in the presence of people. It’s not a blunt tool. It doesn’t force arousal or blunt sensation. Instead, it invites you back into yourself. Softly. Subtly. In that way, Damiana becomes something more than just an herb for the bedroom.
It becomes a way back into feeling.
Clinical Patterns
1.Sexual Debility: We want herbs to behave like buttons. Press here, get this result. And when it comes to Damiana, it’s often marketed that way—as if it were nature’s magic pill for every problem in the bedroom.
But it doesn’t work like that.
A cup of Damiana tea isn’t going to make you fall in love. It won’t flood your brain with obsession. It may not even increase desire in the conventional sense. Because what Damiana really does—what it’s really doing—is something much deeper, and far more interesting.
Damiana increases your capacity to feel.
That sounds abstract. But it’s not. It’s actually quite specific. It opens the heart. Not just romantically or poetically, but physiologically and emotionally. It clears the static in the head—the mental noise, the overthinking, the self-consciousness—that keeps you from inhabiting your own body. It roots you in sensation. It draws you back into your senses.
And in that shift—from mind to body, from guardedness to openness—everything changes.
Because sexuality isn’t just about stimulation. It’s about connection. With Damiana, physical intimacy becomes infused with presence. With feeling. It elevates the experience. And that’s where the real transformation happens—not in heightened arousal, but in deeper intimacy.
This is especially relevant for the many people whose desire is buried under layers of stress and exhaustion. When the nervous system is frayed and the body is depleted, libido rarely shows up. Here, Damiana’s nervine relaxant and trophorestorative actions come into play. It soothes. It rebuilds. It helps restore desire—not by forcing it, but by making space for it to return.
In energetic terms, you could say Damiana speaks to both vata and kapha patterns of sexual debility. The burned-out, emotionally numb, low-energy state of kapha. The anxious, ungrounded, overly sensitive vata picture. In both cases, Damiana bridges the gap. It energizes without overwhelming. It calms without numbing. That paradox again.
But Damiana’s influence goes even further. It helps lower emotional defenses. It opens you up—not just sexually, but relationally. It supports vulnerability. It enhances trust. In that sense, its aphrodisiac effect extends beyond the physical. It becomes a remedy for the heart.
I’ve seen this in practice. For people with social anxiety, Damiana can be transformative—especially when paired with Kava-Kava (Piper methysticum) and Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis). For emotional guardedness and old relationship wounds, it combines beautifully with Rose (Rosa spp.), Mimosa (Albizia julibrissin), and Linden (Tilia × europaea). These are plants that help reopen the door to connection. Damiana stands right among them.
There’s even some preliminary research suggesting Damiana might work by synergizing with oxytocin—the so-called bonding hormone. That could help explain its effects on sexual sensitivity, childbirth, and even premenstrual symptoms. Though, of course, more research is needed to confirm this.
Still, the clinical picture is clear.
Damiana doesn’t just spark desire. It invites you to feel again. And that might be the most powerful aphrodisiac of all.
Up to this point, we’ve been exploring Damiana as a relaxant aphrodisiac—a plant that softens tension, lowers stress, and invites the body into a state of openness. But there’s another side to this herb that makes it especially unique.
Damiana is also a stimulant aphrodisiac.
This duality—relaxant and stimulant in one—is rare. And it’s central to Damiana’s signature. On the stimulant side, Damiana improves pelvic circulation. It drives blood to the genitals, enhances sexual sensation, and heightens sensitivity in the erogenous zones. Some sources go further, suggesting it can improve sexual peak and even intensify or prolong orgasm. It also appears to stimulate the pelvic nerve plexus—enhancing responsiveness in a way that’s especially helpful for those who report feeling little or nothing “down there.”
Of course, with stimulation comes complexity.
In male physiology, for instance, this heightened sensitivity might not always be welcome. Excess sensation can tip into premature ejaculation. So while Damiana’s stimulant effects can be powerful, they’re not always uniformly beneficial—they require context and careful use.
Still, the overall profile is compelling. Warming. Relaxant. Stimulant. Aphrodisiac. Damiana increases libido in both men and women and has been used to address everything from impotence and frigidity to coital inadequacy and sexual neurasthenia. Which is another way of saying: low libido in the context of exhaustion. Physical, emotional, mental burnout.
In that scenario, Damiana makes intuitive sense. It calms the mind. It stirs the body. It gives you energy and desire, seemingly at the same time.
But here’s the important caveat.
That burst of energy you feel? It doesn’t come from the herb. It comes from you. Damiana doesn’t give you energy. It pulls it out of your system and puts it to use. That’s not necessarily a problem—unless your reserves are already low. In those cases, a short-term boost can feel good in the moment, but it may leave you even more depleted afterward.
This is the same dynamic seen with adaptogens when they’re overused. They stimulate your capacity, but if you’re running on empty, you can end up deeper in the hole.
That’s why real herbal support—especially for someone with low libido from stress or burnout—has to be more than a quick fix. It has to be restorative. Damiana can absolutely be part of that strategy, but it needs backup. It does best alongside herbs that nourish the nervous system—Milky Oats (Avena sativa), Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora), or St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum). These herbs provide what Damiana alone cannot: replenishment.
And if the libido loss comes from somewhere else—emotional, psychological, physical—that changes the picture again. A holistic herbalist doesn’t chase symptoms. They look for the root.
One more piece here comes from David Winston, who offers a specific indication he calls “momism.” It’s the pattern he sees in women who’ve given birth and feel disconnected from their libido. And when you stop to consider it, the explanation is obvious.
Sleep deprivation. Hormonal shifts. Physical depletion. The long arc of healing from pregnancy, childbirth, and sometimes surgical trauma—episiotomies, C-sections, vaginal tears. That kind of physical and emotional transformation doesn’t lend itself to immediate sexual readiness.
And maybe that’s not a dysfunction.
Maybe it’s wisdom.
Maybe what the body is saying is simple: It’s not time yet. The libido hasn’t disappeared. It’s resting. Waiting for the right moment to return.
Damiana, when used gently, can help create the conditions for that return. Not by pushing, but by softening. Not by forcing the body to feel, but by allowing it.
That’s the kind of aphrodisiac Damiana is. Not the magic pill. Not the quick fix. But something much more human.
2. Depression and Nervous Debility: Damiana has always stood out for its relationship with the nervous system. But it’s a relationship that defies easy categorization. Some call it a trophorestorative—a term reserved for herbs that nourish and rebuild nerve tissue. But I wouldn’t go that far.
Damiana doesn’t feed the nervous system. It stimulates it. Especially when that system is tired, frayed, or failing to respond.
And yet, when you pair Damiana with true trophorestoratives—Milky Oats, for example—it shines. It doesn’t replace their nourishment. It carries it deeper. You might say it volatilizes the action of those herbs, pushing their effects further into the nervous system. In that sense, Damiana functions almost like a driver. A catalyst. It doesn’t restore on its own, but it helps restoration happen.
That makes sense when you consider its dual nervous system action. Damiana is both a central nervous system depressant and a stimulant. That tension—relaxation and activation in one—is exactly what allows it to balance the two core nervous tissue states in traditional Western energetics: wind/tension and cold/depression.
Wind/tension manifests as chronic stress, anxiety, the frayed-edge feeling of an overwound mind. Cold/depression is the opposite—lethargy, brain fog, sluggishness, a lack of mental fire. Too much heat brings overstimulation. Too much cold brings stagnation. And Damiana? It threads the needle between the two.
This is why it’s so specific for people who live in both states at once—burned-out and anxious. Depressed and sexually numb. Wired and tired.
It also explains Damiana’s historical use for “neurasthenia”—what used to be called nervous debility. In modern terms, we might describe it as the fallout of chronic stress: exhaustion, poor sleep, anxiety, panic attacks, overstimulation, and emotional flatness all rolled into one. And for that picture, Damiana has much to offer. Especially when paired with Milky Oats (Avena sativa) or Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora) in a carefully balanced formula.
But here’s the caveat—one that bears repeating.
Damiana doesn’t give you energy. It encourages your body to use what’s already there. And that can be a problem if your reserves are low. Like many stimulant herbs, it’s easy to overdo it. You feel good for a moment—maybe even energized—but that energy was borrowed. Not earned. And the crash that follows can leave you worse off than before.
That’s why formulating matters. If someone’s burned out or anxious, Damiana must be buffered by true sedative nervines. Used alone, it may drive them too hard, too fast. People with a pitta constitution, in particular, may find it overstimulating—leading to headaches, insomnia, or mental irritability.
Used wisely, though, it offers a powerful tool for the nervous system. For depression, it pairs beautifully with uplifting aromatics like Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis), Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia), and Holy Basil (Ocimum sanctum). For burnout, it combines well with adaptogens and deeply nourishing nervine restoratives.
In the end, Damiana’s role isn’t to carry the full weight of the formula. It’s the spark. The movement. The nudge. And sometimes, that’s exactly what the nervous system needs.
3. Genitourinary Stagnation and Infection: Damiana is often discussed in the context of libido and mood. But there’s another area where its influence—while quieter—is worth paying attention to: the genitourinary system.
Dr. Jill Stansbury, a well-known figure in modern herbalism, points out that Damiana can be helpful for men dealing with BPH—benign prostatic hypertrophy. These are the men who find themselves dribbling urine, struggling with flow, or feeling as though something is blocking them from within. And they’re not imagining it.
As Dr. Stansbury explains, the difference between infectious prostatitis and prostatic enlargement is important. One is marked by inflammation, pain, and signs of infection. The other—BPH—is more about obstruction. That uncomfortable sensation of a “ball” in the perineum. The stop-and-start stream. The frustrating dribble instead of a strong release. It’s not an infection. It’s a bottleneck.
Damiana helps in these cases—not by acting as a strong diuretic or clearing agent, but by supporting circulation and relieving local congestion. It helps move things along. And for many men, that small shift makes a big difference.
There’s also the presence of arbutin—a compound found in Damiana that’s known for its urinary tract antiseptic properties. It’s the same one you’ll find in herbs like Uva-Ursi and Pipsissewa. So yes, Damiana has a mild disinfectant effect on the urinary tract.
But here’s the caveat.
If you’re dealing with a full-blown urinary tract infection—burning, urgency, systemic symptoms—Damiana probably isn’t enough on its own. It’s not the heavy-hitter. It's more of a background player. A gentle assist, not the main intervention. If it’s the only herb on hand, you can use it. But if you're building a formula, other remedies will likely serve that role more effectively.
So while Damiana may not be your first line of defense, it’s still part of the conversation. Not just for sexual health, but for urinary stagnation, subtle congestion, and the low-grade issues that often get missed until they turn into something more. It works not with force, but with movement. And sometimes, that’s exactly what the system needs.
4. Pelvic Stagnation: There’s a pattern in herbal medicine that doesn’t always make it into mainstream health conversations—but it should. It’s called pelvic stagnation.
At its core, pelvic stagnation refers to poor blood flow in the pelvic region. It’s often talked about in relation to female anatomy, but the same principle applies to men—especially in cases like BPH, where obstruction and local congestion are part of the picture.
In women, pelvic stagnation can show up in a number of ways: delayed or absent menstruation (amenorrhea), abnormal growths like cysts and fibroids, or a general sense of heaviness and pain in the lower abdomen—what’s sometimes referred to as pelvic fullness syndrome. That dragging, weighted-down sensation is a signal. Something isn’t moving.
This is where circulatory stimulants, pelvic astringents, and alteratives often come into play. And Damiana fits that profile—particularly because of its warming, stimulating, and dispersing qualities. But there’s something else happening here too—something at the biochemical level that makes Damiana even more compelling.
Some of Damiana’s constituents have been shown to inhibit aromatase.
That may not sound like much at first. But aromatase is a key enzyme in hormonal health. It converts androgens—like testosterone—into estrogens. And when that conversion ramps up too high, things begin to spiral. Estrogen levels increase, which drives the production of inflammatory prostaglandins. Those prostaglandins, in turn, stimulate more aromatase. Which creates more estrogen. And so the cycle continues.
Add elevated insulin into the mix—which also stimulates aromatase—and you have the perfect storm: high estrogen and high inflammation. That duo drives a range of reproductive system conditions: endometriosis, fibroids, even certain cancers in women. In men, it’s a contributing factor in benign prostatic hyperplasia.
And here’s where Damiana steps in.
By inhibiting aromatase, it helps slow that cycle. By modulating inflammation, it eases some of the strain. And by stimulating circulation, it works to physically move what’s stuck. It’s not a cure-all. But it’s a targeted ally. Especially in formulas built to address complex hormonal and inflammatory conditions.
That said, it’s important to remember that Damiana is a warming, stimulating herb. And in cases where the system is already hot or inflamed, too much stimulation can backfire. Used improperly—or without proper formulation—it can irritate rather than assist.
But when used wisely, and in the right context, Damiana becomes more than just a nervine or aphrodisiac. It becomes a hormone-modulating, inflammation-balancing, circulation-moving herb with very real applications for pelvic health.
It’s subtle. It’s systemic. And it’s worth paying attention to.
5. Hypothyroidism: Every so often, an herb does something intriguing—something that doesn’t quite fit within its usual category. Damiana is one of those herbs.
We often think of it in terms of mood, libido, or nervous system function. But clinically, Damiana has also been observed to mildly stimulate the thyroid.
The mechanism? That’s still unclear.
Maybe it’s acting through the hypothalamus or pituitary, nudging the release of TSH. Maybe it’s interacting more directly with the thyroid gland itself. We don’t yet know. But what we do know is this: in practice, Damiana seems to push the thyroid into slightly higher gear. Not dramatically. Not aggressively. But just enough to matter—especially for those whose baseline is sluggishness, fatigue, and low metabolic tone.
That said, Damiana isn’t a primary thyroid remedy. On its own, it’s not enough.
This is where formulation becomes essential. Damiana works best when paired with herbs that have a more established track record for hypothyroidism. Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) for its adaptogenic, thyroid-supportive properties. Black Walnut (Juglans nigra) and Bladderwrack (Fucus vesiculosus) for their mineral content and historical use in thyroid conditions. Blue Flag (Iris versicolor), for sluggish metabolism. And Bacopa (Bacopa monnieri) for its balancing effect on the nervous and endocrine systems.
Damiana’s role here is more subtle. It doesn’t lead the charge—but it might just provide the spark.
Pharmacological Data
Behind every herbal action is chemistry. And Damiana’s chemistry is nothing if not complex.
Start with the volatile oils. These are the compounds that give Damiana its unmistakable aroma—that resinous, floral scent you can recognize instantly in a tea or tincture. Chemically, they include 1,8-cineole, various pinenes, thymol, and lamtha-cadinene. But the effect is simpler than the names: they stimulate. They warm. They move.
Then there’s the bitter principle—damianin. Like most bitters, its job is to wake up digestion. It nudges the liver, prompts bile production, and gets things flowing again. If you’ve ever had that experience of suddenly feeling more present, more alert after tasting something bitter, that’s the bitter reflex. And Damiana has it.
The list goes on.
Arbutin—a phenol glycoside—offers antimicrobial action, particularly in the urinary tract. Tetraphyllin B, a cyanogenic glycoside, adds another layer to the plant’s chemical personality. Hydrocarbons like triacontane. Phytosterols that may influence hormone balance. Tannins, which provide astringency and tone. And resins, which are not just aromatic, but help shield tissues from inflammatory damage.
Then there are the flavonoids—perhaps the most intriguing group of all. Acacetin, for example, is one of the compounds that inhibits aromatase, the enzyme that converts testosterone to estrogen. Apigenin does the same. Pinocembrin stands out for its neuroprotective qualities. And quercetin—well known even outside the world of herbalism—is a potent anti-inflammatory.
What we’re left with is a plant that doesn’t just act—it acts through many channels at once. Nervous system, endocrine, digestive, urinary, reproductive. Damiana doesn’t specialize. It integrates.
And it’s that complexity that gives it power.
Safety
Damiana is a plant with a warm, stimulating nature. And while that can be part of its charm, it also means there are situations where caution is not just wise—it’s necessary.
Start with pregnancy. Damiana should be avoided. Its action as a pelvic circulatory stimulant makes it inappropriate during this time, as increased blood flow to the reproductive region can pose a risk. The same caution applies in cases of heavy menstrual or uterine bleeding. More blood flow is not what’s needed in those situations.
Then there’s tetraphyllin B—a cyanogenic glycoside found in the plant. In small amounts, it’s part of Damiana’s natural chemistry. But at higher doses, it can become toxic. This is not a theoretical risk—it’s a real biochemical concern. Which means dosage matters. A lot.
Beyond that, we step into less charted territory. Despite its long history of traditional use, Damiana hasn’t been extensively studied in modern clinical research. And while that doesn’t make it unsafe, it does mean we don’t have a long list of confirmed interactions or contraindications. So far, no herb-drug interactions have been recorded.
But energetically, we do know a few things.
Damiana is warming. It’s stimulating. It moves energy in the body. For people with already high libido, this can tip the balance in the wrong direction. It can aggravate rather than support. That same warming action can also irritate sensitive tissues—especially in cases of prostatitis, pelvic inflammatory disease, or urinary tract irritation. And constitutionally, it’s not a good match for the heat/excitation tissue state, or for those with strong pitta constitutions in Ayurveda.
In short: Damiana is powerful. And like anything powerful, it requires a thoughtful hand. Used in the right context, it’s deeply supportive. But used indiscriminately, it can do more harm than good.
And that’s the real lesson here. In herbalism—as in life—it’s all about timing, context, and balance.
Doses and Administration
For all its complexity, Damiana is surprisingly simple to prepare.
The most traditional method is the infusion. One heaping teaspoon of the dried herb, steeped in a cup of hot water. Covered, of course—to keep those volatile oils from escaping into the air. Give it 10 to 15 minutes, and you’ll have a tea that’s as aromatic as it is potent.
But if you want something stronger, something longer-lasting, tincture is the way to go.
Using dried Damiana, the standard tincture ratio is 1:5 in 50–60% alcohol. That percentage matters—it’s strong enough to extract the resinous volatile oils that define Damiana’s character, but also balanced enough to pull the water-soluble flavonoids. You’re getting the full spectrum.
The typical dosage? 30 to 60 drops, taken one to four times per day.
It’s not a lot. But then again, with Damiana, you don’t need a lot. The plant speaks clearly—and quickly—when given the space to work.

Herbal formulas give us a framework to approach these questions—not with a single herb or quick fix, but with carefully combined layers of support. Damiana plays a role in several of these blends, but never alone. It works best as part of a team.

This formula, from Dr. Jill Stansbury, brings together several traditional aphrodisiacs—each from a different lineage, each with a different emphasis.
Damiana’s role is mild but meaningful. Horny Goat Weed is often used for libido support in both men and women. Shatavari and Kudzu, drawn from Ayurvedic and Thai traditions respectively, focus on female sexual rejuvenation. And Ginseng rounds it out with a more broadly stimulating effect.
The formula is taken as a tincture: one teaspoon, three times a day, over the course of three to six months. After that, it’s time to pause and reflect. Do you feel more supported? Is your system asking for more—or for less?
Depression Formula
30% Milky Oats (Avena sativa)
30% St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum)
15% Damiana (Turnera diffusa)
15% Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis)
10% Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)
This is a formula I’ve returned to again and again. Or at least, some version of it. At its core, it’s built around nourishment—true, foundational support for the nervous system. Milky Oats and St. John’s Wort make up 60% of the blend, anchoring it with deep, trophorestorative action.
But constitutions vary. In warmer individuals—those prone to irritability, heat, or restlessness—St. John’s Wort might be too much. In those cases, Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora) is a better choice. Cooling, soothing, steady.
Damiana and Lemon Balm, on the other hand, bring lift. They move. I think of them not just as nervines, but as “drivers”—they carry the actions of the more grounding herbs deeper into the nervous system. Holy Basil (Ocimum sanctum) can substitute for either, depending on the picture. And Lavender? It does what Lavender does best. It brightens the mind. Clears the air.
This is a formula for people who aren’t just tired—but dulled. Disconnected. Flattened by stress or grief or long, grey seasons. And it works not by forcing change, but by restoring presence.
Tea for Urge Incontinence
Horsetail (Equisetum spp.)
St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum)
Maca (Lepidium meyenii)
Alfalfa (Medicago sativa)
Damiana (Turnera diffusa)
Peppermint (Mentha piperita)
Urge incontinence isn’t the same as stress incontinence. The difference matters. One is triggered by movement—lifting, coughing, laughing. The other comes suddenly, with the overwhelming need to go—and the inability to hold it.
This tea is designed for the latter.
Equal parts of each herb, steeped together: one tablespoon of the mix per cup of hot water, taken three times daily. But even as you sip, keep in mind: this is not a standalone solution. Dr. Jill Stansbury, who recommends this formula, pairs it with something else—something decidedly unglamorous: 200 Kegel exercises, with each cup.
The point isn’t the tea or the exercises. It’s the combination. The consistent, daily effort to support and retrain the pelvic floor while soothing the nervous system and toning the urinary tract.
And Damiana’s role? Subtle, but essential. One more layer of support in a picture that—like so many in herbalism—is more about systems than symptoms.
