The Body’s Hidden Dial
Every system has an axis. A balancing point. And when it comes to the body’s tissues, that axis is tone.
Ignore tone, and you risk treating the wrong thing.
Because here’s the problem: a plant might be warming or cooling, moistening or drying. But none of those qualities tell you whether that plant is going to tighten or relax tissue. And that distinction matters. Give a relaxant to someone whose tissues are already loose, and you’ll make things worse. Give an astringent to someone stuck in tension, and you might just lock it in further.
This is where the wind/tension tissue state comes into view. And it’s the mirror opposite of damp/relaxation. Together, these two define the spectrum of tissue tone.
To understand the wind/tension state, you have to look at the point where the nervous system meets the muscles. Every muscle fiber in the body—whether it’s under conscious control or not—relies on nerves to tell it what to do. Nerve impulses trigger contraction. Or relaxation. But when those signals fire excessively, the result is predictable: the muscles contract and don’t let go.
That’s tension.
It shows up in two major muscle types. First, there’s skeletal muscle—the ones that move your body. Your arms, your legs, your back. These are the muscles we know best. But then there are smooth muscles—the fibers that wrap around our internal organs, vessels, and tissues. We don’t control them consciously, but they shape how the body functions every day.
Think about digestion. You don’t will your gallbladder to contract and release bile—it just happens. That’s smooth muscle, working under the command of the autonomic nervous system.
But here’s where things get interesting.
Most of our organs—like the kidneys or the liver—aren’t directly moved by nerves. But the blood vessels feeding them are. And when those vessels constrict, they deliver less blood. Less oxygen. Less nutrients. And that means the organ function suffers—even though the organ itself isn’t the problem.
That’s the real power of the wind/tension state. It doesn’t just affect muscles—it ripples outward, shaping how entire systems behave. It creates spasms, contractions, constriction. Tension, both psychological and physical.
At the root of it all is an overstimulated nervous system. A body stuck in sympathetic dominance—that fight-or-flight state where everything is braced, alert, ready. And unable to switch gears into the parasympathetic state, where rest, repair, and digestion happen.
So while the symptoms may show up in the muscles or in mood—or in the slow unraveling of organ function—the story always begins the same way: too much firing, not enough release.
And that’s why tone matters.
Because if you don’t recognize the tension, you can’t help the body relax. And without relaxation, the deeper healing can’t begin.
We tend to think of stress as something that happens in our heads. A feeling. A mood. An emotional response to modern life. But the truth is, stress begins in the body. And it’s not subtle.
It starts with the sympathetic nervous system.
When this part of the autonomic nervous system becomes dominant, a chain reaction follows. The blood vessels around your internal organs constrict. Blood is shunted away from your core and pushed out to the periphery—toward your limbs, your skin, your muscles. Heart rate climbs. Pupils dilate. Digestion slows to a crawl. The body braces.
It’s the “fight, flight, or freeze” response. And originally, it was meant for survival—sprinting from danger, preparing to defend yourself, or going completely still in the hopes of not being seen.
But here’s the problem: those life-threatening moments are rare in the modern world. We don’t run from predators anymore. Instead, we worry about inboxes, bills, deadlines, family dynamics, and the persistent weight of uncertainty. And the body—unable to distinguish between physical danger and psychological pressure—responds the same way to both.
That’s how the wind/tension tissue state becomes chronic.
Sometimes it’s triggered by the mind—anxiety, nervousness, insomnia, mental overload. Other times it’s purely physical: vigorous workouts, extreme temperatures, even a lingering fever.
And when the system gets stuck, tension takes over.
Muscles tighten. Organs constrict. Digestion stalls. The liver slows down. The gallbladder struggles to expel bile. Fats and oils, which are critical to nerve health, go undigested. And that creates a cascade: without those lipids, the myelin sheath that insulates nerves begins to weaken. And the nervous system, once again, bears the brunt.
But tension doesn’t always stay in one place.
In fact, one of the signatures of this tissue state is movement—flux. Symptoms that change, come and go, or migrate around the body. You might see alternating constipation and diarrhea. Spasms that pop up here, then disappear, only to return somewhere else. Shifting pain. Chills followed by fever, then chills again.
It’s the influence of wind. Of ether. Of change.
Matthew Wood puts it well: “There is some subtlety to this tissue state that might be missed.” He describes how repeated chills—especially during influenza or deep infections—can disrupt the autonomic nervous system. In older forms of medicine, this was a serious issue. Today, it’s less common—but not gone. There are still many people, he notes, who say: “I’ve never been the same since that fever.”
And this is why tissue state energetics matter. They help us ask a better question—not just what is happening, but why. Not just what symptoms we see, but what deeper pattern is holding them in place.
Because tension doesn’t always come from nowhere. It can arise from dryness, when tissue becomes undernourished. From oil depletion, when the nerves lose their insulation. From excess heat, which dries the system and hardens the tone.
And once you see it, the wind/tension state becomes one of the easiest patterns to recognize. Because we’ve all been there. We all know what it feels like to be locked in, bracing against something invisible. The modern world has made sure of that.
Turning Down the Volume
In the early 20th century, Joseph Thurston—one of the first to map out the six tissue states—described this one as the result of “abnormal contraction of the neuromuscular system.” It was a simple phrase, but it captured something essential: tension is not just about stress. It’s about nerves. Muscles. And the system that connects them, stuck in a loop it can’t unwind.
And so it follows that if the problem is abnormal contraction, the solution is its opposite: relaxation.
That’s why the herbal response to the wind/tension state centers on one core idea—soothing the nervous system. Not suppressing it. Not numbing it. But gently dialing it down.
The most direct tools we have for this are the nervines and antispasmodics.
Now, nervines come in three broad categories. First, there are sedative nervines—mild, calming herbs that take the edge off. Then come the hypnotics—stronger, more assertive in their action, capable of loosening the grip of stubborn tension. And finally, trophorestoratives—a wordy name for herbs that nourish and rebuild the nervous system after it’s been worn thin from long-standing stress.
All three have their place. Because in the wind/tension state, the nerves are the problem. They’re sending too many signals. They’re telling muscles to stay tight. And if we don’t treat the nerves, we can’t hope to release the contraction in the body—or in the mind.
But there’s a wrinkle.
Many nervines are energetically cooling and drying. And that’s not always helpful. In fact, this tissue state is often made worse by excess cold and dryness—what in Ayurveda would be called vata. So the solution can’t be one-size-fits-all. You have to match the plant to the person.
That’s where warming nervines come in—plants like Valerian, Asafoetida, Catnip, Kava-Kava, and Rosemary. These herbs bring heat. They stimulate while they soothe. And they do it without aggravating an already cold and dry constitution.
Then there are the antispasmodics—remedies that work directly on muscle tension. Some are also hypnotics. Others act more locally, relaxing smooth and skeletal muscle without necessarily sedating the nervous system. You can approach it from both sides: soothe the nerves and ease the muscle. Combine them, and the results are often remarkable.
In Chinese medicine, the go-to flavor for treating excess tension is acrid—a taste often described as somewhere between bitter and sour. Matthew Wood puts it even more bluntly: it “tastes like bile in the back of the throat.” Not pleasant. But effective.
And it turns out that most of our classic antispasmodic herbs share this acrid quality: Lobelia, Black Cohosh, Valerian, Kava-Kava, Hops, Skunk Cabbage. They’re sharp. Piercing. Designed to cut through constriction.
So, when the body tightens and won’t let go, when the nervous system won’t stand down, and when symptoms jump from muscle to mood to organ function and back again, this is where we turn: to nervines, to antispasmodics, to the often-unpleasant but deeply effective plants that unwind what’s been pulled too tight for too long.
Sometimes, the herbs that work best are the ones that taste the worst.
According to herbalist Matthew Wood, one of the most effective flavor profiles for treating the wind/tension tissue state is what he calls the “nauseatingly bitter” plants. These are not gentle herbs. They don’t ease into the system. They grab your attention—sharp, unyielding, and almost aggressive in their bitterness.
And yet, they do something remarkable.
These herbs seems to do something deep: they helpe get the autonomic nervous system and liver unstuck.
Because here’s what happens when the sympathetic nervous system stays switched on for too long: blood flow to the liver decreases. The organ slows down. It becomes stagnant. Toxic. And when that happens, the body tries to compensate—often through a low-grade fever, or a wave of excessive sweating. It’s trying to clear toxins the only way it can.
This is where those bitter herbs come in.
They don’t just work on the liver. They work on the entire neuromuscular network that surrounds the vasculature—relaxing it, softening it, allowing blood to move more freely into the periphery. That makes them relaxant diaphoretics—herbs that not only promote sweating, but do so by reducing tension and helping circulation reach the surface of the body.
A few key examples: Boneset, Blue Vervain, Hops, Wormwood, Blessed Thistle. Each one is bitter. Each one is powerful. Each one plays a role in clearing tension, especially when it’s entangled with liver stagnation.
But there’s a caution here.
Many of these plants are also cooling in nature—and in a constitution that’s already cold and dry, that can make things worse. So, as always, context matters. It’s not just about what the herb does. It’s about who the person is, and how their body is currently operating.
