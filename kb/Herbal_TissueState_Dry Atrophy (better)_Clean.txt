The Forgotten Half of Hydration
When we talk about dehydration, we usually think about water. We picture someone stranded in the desert, lips cracked, skin sunburned, desperately reaching for a canteen. But what if I told you that water is only half the story?
Hydration, in its full physiological sense, has a second half. And it’s one we rarely talk about: oil.
This is where things get interesting.
Every nerve in your body is coated in oil. Every cellular membrane relies on fats to communicate with its environment. And many of the most vital compounds in your body—your hormones, your neurotransmitters, your immune modulators—either travel through, or are made from, lipids. In fact, your body’s entire ability to transport and absorb nutrients hinges not just on fluids, but on the precise balance of water and oil.
So now imagine what happens when that balance tips too far in one direction.
This is the dry/atrophy tissue state. And it’s more than just feeling parched or having dry skin. It’s a state where tissues don’t just lack moisture—they lack nutrition. The secretions that power your digestion slow down. The transport systems that carry nutrients to your cells begin to falter. And over time, the tissues themselves—organs, glands, systems—start to weaken.
They atrophy.
And here’s where the effects ripple out.
Hormones, for instance, may still be produced in the adrenal glands. But without sufficient oils to ferry them through the body, they don’t reach their targets. The endocrine system misfires—not because it’s broken, but because its messengers can’t deliver the message. It’s not a failure of production. It’s a failure of movement.
It’s a kind of quiet malnourishment. A depletion that hides in plain sight.
And once you start looking for it, you see it everywhere.
Let’s zoom in.
Every cell in your body is surrounded by a membrane. That membrane isn’t just a protective barrier—it’s a gatekeeper. It decides what gets in and what gets out. It’s the cell’s voice and ears, its exchange hub, its defense system. And it’s built from oil.
More precisely: a phospholipid bilayer.
Now imagine what happens when the body is deprived of good oils—or worse, fed the wrong kinds. The membrane loses its fluidity. It becomes brittle. Less responsive. Less communicative. Nutrients can’t get in. Toxins can’t get out. The cell begins to shut down.
Not catastrophically. Not all at once.
But subtly. Systematically.
And suddenly, you're not just talking about a single compromised cell. You're talking about an entire organism—sluggish, undernourished, running below capacity. Not because something’s broken, but because everything is too dry.
And the nervous system?
The story gets even more striking.
Nerve fibers rely on a coating called the myelin sheath—essentially an oil-rich insulation that allows electrical signals to move swiftly and accurately through the body. Strip that coating away—or weaken it—and everything slows down. Misfires. Becomes frayed at the edges.
That sense of stress that never seems to go away? The scattered thinking, the restlessness, the sleeplessness?
This might be why.
Oil dehydration is rarely part of the conversation, yet it quietly underpins many of the nervous system disorders that seem so prevalent in modern life. We focus on symptoms. We medicate the effects. But we rarely ask whether the body simply needs better lubrication.
And the signs are everywhere.
Dry eyes. Dry tongue. Joints that crack and pop. Mucous membranes that fail to moisten the throat or line the gut. Stools that are hard, brittle, reluctant to pass. Skin that flakes, itches, or inflames. Exhaustion that isn’t fixed with sleep.
In Chinese Medicine, they call it yin deficiency—a poetic term that means there’s not enough water to contain the fire. And in a culture that lives on coffee, screens, deadlines, and drive… this is more common than we think.
The trick isn’t to put out the fire. It’s to restore the water.
And when that happens—when oils return, when water is replenished, when nourishment circulates and tissues plump back to life—the fire no longer rages.
It warms.
It energizes.
It comes back into balance. On its own.
When the Body Dries Out, and How Plants Can Help
In herbal medicine, the options for dryness are surprisingly narrow.
Most herbs, by their very nature, are drying. They move moisture out—through the skin, the lungs, the bowels, the kidneys. But for the dry/atrophy tissue state, we need the opposite. We need to put moisture back in. And that means turning to a special class of plants known as demulcents.
Demulcents are sweet. Not candy-sweet—but sweet in the herbalist’s sense: soft, smooth, nourishing. These are the plants that release mucilage—a slippery, gel-like substance that coats, soothes, and moistens. You know it when you feel it. Think marshmallow root. Slippery elm. Licorice. These plants don’t just hydrate—they restore a kind of fluid integrity to tissues that have gone dry, brittle, and tired.
But water alone isn’t enough.
Hydration is twofold: water and oil.
Which is why we also turn to oily plants. Not the essential oils that evaporate on your skin, but fixed oils—the kind that stay. The kind you find in seeds, nuts, and fatty roots. Burdock is a classic here. But so are flaxseeds, chia, pumpkin seeds, walnuts, and avocados. In fact, most of these are technically foods, but that’s the point: they bring back oil to a system that’s been running dry.
Still, there’s another piece.
Dryness is one half of the picture. The other is atrophy—a slow weakening of the tissues, the organs, the systems. That’s why we also need herbs that build.
And here, the picture gets more complex.
Nettles. Alfalfa. Red raspberry leaf. Horsetail. These are nutrient-dense plants, packed with vitamins, minerals, and trace elements. They’re classified as astringents or even diuretics—technically drying in nature. But their real power lies in their ability to feed and fortify. In this case, nourishment outweighs energetics. Their drying effect is eclipsed by their tissue-building properties.
So what about digestion?
That’s where the bitters come in.
Yes, bitters are drying—especially with long-term use. But in the short term, they do something fascinating: they stimulate secretions. Stomach acid. Bile. Pancreatic enzymes. That initial response actually adds moisture back into the gut. So while they may dry over time, in the immediate sense they rehydrate the very tissues that extract nutrients from food.
The takeaway is subtle, but important.
Sometimes the herbs that help dryness are themselves drying.
And sometimes the path to hydration is not through direct moistening, but through stimulating the body’s own secretions—waking up the gut, improving absorption, and bringing vitality back to the tissues from the inside out.
In that way, even bitters can be a balm for a system that’s forgotten how to nourish itself.
