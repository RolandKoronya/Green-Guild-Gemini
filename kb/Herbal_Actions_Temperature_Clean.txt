Temperature: The Forgotten Polarity
Every herbal tradition—across time, across cultures—has shared at least one fundamental idea: plants affect temperature.
It’s easy to miss just how universal this is. From China to Greece, India to the Americas, herbalists have always asked one basic question when approaching a remedy: is it hot or cold?
And yet, somehow, modern medicine doesn’t.
There’s a story I keep coming back to. I was once discussing herbal energetics with a doctor—a conventionally trained one. I brought up how plants influence not only temperature but also moisture in the body. The reaction? Laughter. Not unkind, but dismissive.
“That sounds like a relatively uneducated and elementary basis for practicing medicine,” he said.
At the time, I didn’t have a response. But later, I realized something: he was right.
It is elementary. But not in the way he meant.
Because this isn’t primitive. It’s foundational. Rooted in the Elements—those basic building blocks of nature that nearly every ancient medical system, from Ayurveda to Greek philosophy, used to understand the body and the world. Earth. Water. Air. Fire. And Ether.
In the Western tradition, Aristotle described how the four Elements are derived from four qualities: hot, cold, wet, and dry. These aren’t arbitrary categories. They’re the first observable patterns in life. The most basic distinctions we can make about how something behaves in the body. Is it warming? Does it cool? Does it dry out tissue? Does it soften it?
That’s the kind of simplicity that holds power.
The hot-cold polarity isn’t just theoretical—it’s deeply practical. It gives us a way to understand how a plant influences the whole constitution of a person, not just their symptoms. It helps us read tissue states, see patterns, and treat root causes.
The problem is, most modern herbal practice has drifted away from this. Somewhere along the way, the language of energetics gave way to the language of constituents and isolated biochemistry. Instead of asking what a plant feels like or does in the body, we ask what chemical compounds it contains. We’ve traded the forest for a list of molecules.
And that shift—quiet as it was—changed the way people use herbs.
Instead of practicing holistically, many modern herbalists now practice allopathically. This herb for that symptom. One problem, one plant. It’s a mindset borrowed from pharmaceutical medicine—and one that herbalism was never built to support.
So where do we go from here?
We return to energetics.
We remind ourselves that herbalism was never meant to be formulaic. There is no one-size-fits-all remedy. Three people can present with the exact same symptom—and yet each one may require a different plant entirely.
Why? Because the underlying imbalance is different. One person may be too hot. Another too cold. One may be dry. Another damp. And the only way to see that clearly is through an energetic lens.
When we talk about a plant’s “temperature,” we’re not referring to a literal number. We don’t mean 98.6 degrees Fahrenheit or 37 degrees Celsius. It’s not that linear. The Greeks knew this. They spoke of “qualities,” not measurements. And that’s what we need to pay attention to—the qualitative shifts a plant produces in the body.
Is it warming? Does it increase circulation, heighten metabolism, stimulate movement? Or is it cooling—slowing things down, easing inflammation, soothing overstimulation?
These are the questions that guide energetic herbalism. They’re not abstract. They’re specific, experiential, and deeply human.
Next, we’ll explore how this concept of temperature—of hot and cold—translate into how a plant operates, and how understanding it can sharpen your practice in ways no lab test or chemical breakdown ever could.
Hot: The Language of Movement and Fire
What happens when you apply heat?
The answer, at first, seems straightforward. Molecules move faster. Liquids begin to vaporize. Boundaries dissolve. But look a little closer, and something deeper begins to emerge. Heat changes things. Fundamentally. It stirs, agitates, separates. It pulls apart what is still and dormant. In physics, in the kitchen—and in the body.
This is the foundation of the hot energetic quality in herbalism.
To understand hot herbs, we need to understand fire. Not just in the literal sense, but in terms of what fire does. It consumes. It melts. It transforms. It breaks things down into simpler parts and spreads energy outward from the center.
So what does that mean when we say a plant is hot?
It means the plant has the capacity to move. To activate. To wake the system up. Not necessarily because it tastes hot—though some do—but because its effect in the body is one of warmth, stimulation, and activity.
Hot herbs distribute the body’s vital force. They push it outward. They stimulate circulation, dissolve stagnation, speed up metabolism. They bring blood to the surface. They warm what’s cold. They stir what’s sluggish. They bring life to what’s been dormant.
In traditional Western herbalism, the Greeks took this even further. They didn’t just describe a plant as hot or cold. They classified its degree of heat. There were four. First degree meant mild warmth. Fourth degree? That was intense—strong enough to burn.
Here’s how herbalist Matthew Wood describes it:
Hot in the first degree opens the pores.
Second degree thins fluids, helping them move more freely.
Third degree ramps up digestion and metabolism, pushing warmth outward through sweat.
Fourth degree can literally burn the skin—used externally to awaken deeply stagnant tissues or break down hardened growths.
The hotter the herb, the stronger its push.
But there's a nuance here.
Hot herbs are often stimulating—but not all stimulants are hot. It’s tempting to conflate the two, but they’re not interchangeable. Some stimulants are cooling. That’s why “stimulant” has its own energetic classification—it describes effect, not temperature. The overlap is common, but not complete.
So what kinds of plants fall into the hot category?
Many contain resins or pungent essential oils. Think spicy, aromatic, sometimes even sharp. They often stimulate the digestive fire—what Ayurveda calls agni. They warm the belly, increase circulation, promote perspiration, and activate the immune system through their antimicrobial properties.
They’re the antidote to cold and dampness. They counteract stagnation and depression of function. They help the body metabolize—not just food, but waste. Toxins. Emotions. They get things moving again.

Cold: The Energy of Inward Motion
If heat is expansion—movement, dispersion, activity—then cold is its mirror opposite.
Cold contracts. It restricts. It slows everything down.
You can feel this intuitively. Think of what your body does when you’re chilled: you pull inward. Wrap your arms around yourself. You become smaller, more contained. You don’t expand into the environment—you pull away from it. The energy turns downward and inward. It’s not just a sensation. It’s a shift in how your body moves through space.
And at the molecular level? The pattern repeats. Cold causes particles to slow down, draw closer, and reduce their activity. Less motion. More density.
Now translate that to the body.
Cold herbs operate on this very principle. They calm. They cool. They suppress excess heat and hyperactivity in tissues. They slow down what’s moving too fast—be it inflammation, overactive digestion, or nervous agitation.
They even work on a subtle level: thickening fluids, closing the pores of the skin, tempering the fire of metabolic excess. They are the brakes on the system, restoring order when the body’s internal pace is out of sync.
Matthew Wood gives a helpful framework here—four degrees of cold:
First degree cools gently, like salad greens on a warm day.
Second degree thickens fluids and reduces heat internally.
Third degree restrains outward flow—stopping things from leaking or rising.
Fourth degree anchors the energy downward, preventing it from shooting up in ways it shouldn’t.
And it’s that fourth degree that’s especially interesting.
Wood compares these effects to the idea of vapors—energies rising upward that shouldn't. In Traditional Chinese Medicine, they call it "rebellious qi.” In Western terms, we might call it dizziness, fainting, hysteria, PMS. Things moving up when they’re supposed to stay grounded.
Cold herbs in the fourth degree can stop that. They pull everything back down. They center. They settle. They can even numb the senses—which is why some of them, like opium, are dangerous in large amounts. Too much cold, and you don’t just soothe the body—you shut it down.
That’s the power of cold. It restores boundaries. But overused, it can cross them too.
The energy map becomes clearer: hot herbs stimulate. Cold herbs suppress. Not in a negative sense, but in the sense of slowing, softening, quieting what’s gone too far. That’s why cold herbs are used for classic “heat” symptoms: redness, pain, inflammation, overactivity, agitation.
And you can taste this dynamic too.
Bitter herbs—perhaps more than any other—carry a cooling signature. They draw the vital force downward. That shiver you feel after a strong bitter? That chill in the belly? That’s the cooling energy at work. It’s visceral.
Cooling herbs come in many forms. Alkaloids, bitter compounds, and even some essential oils. The chemistry varies—but the energetic signature is consistent: slow down, cool off, draw in.

More Than Just Hot and Cold
At this point, you might be wondering: so which herbs are hot, and which are cold?
It’s a fair question. But also—an incomplete one.
Because while it’s helpful to explore examples of warming and cooling herbs, it’s important to remember that this isn’t a tidy division. You can’t just draw a line through the middle of your materia medica and sort everything into two clean categories.
Reality is more nuanced.
The herbs listed earlier are just that—examples. A starting point. A way to begin noticing how these opposing qualities show up in actual remedies. But as you get deeper into practice, you’ll start to encounter plants that defy easy labels.
And that’s where things get interesting.
Take Ayurveda, for instance. That system introduces a concept known as vipaka—the post-digestive effect of a substance. In other words, what a plant does after it moves through the digestive system might differ from what it appeared to do at first.
This alone can turn everything upside down.
Because a plant might feel warming in the mouth, but produce a cooling effect once digested. Or vice versa. Some herbs seem to work both directions at once—stimulating and cooling, activating and soothing. It’s enough to make you pause.
And it should.
Take nettle leaf (Urtica dioica). Nettle is often labeled a stimulant. It increases liver function, boosts blood circulation, activates lymph flow. It moves. But energetically? Nettle is actually cooling. Especially when it comes to inflammation and tissue irritation. That paradox—stimulating and cooling—challenges the idea that all stimulants are hot. They’re not. And that’s precisely why this mini course includes more than just the classic four energetics (hot, cold, damp, dry). We’ve added two more energetic categories to make space for complexity.
So where does that leave us?
With a shift in approach.
Because while diagrams and lists are useful, they’re not the whole picture. The real understanding doesn’t come from reading about herbs. It comes from experiencing them.
When you meet a new plant, try this: don’t just read about it. Taste it. Sit with it. Feel what happens.
Notice:
Does your energy lift or sink?
Do you feel more focused—or more floaty?
Does heat rise to your skin, or do you feel a chill?
Does the plant feel familiar? Comforting? Or agitating?
Sometimes the best indicator is your own reaction. If you dislike a plant instantly, it might be telling you something about how it clashes with your constitution. If you feel drawn to it, perhaps it’s working to bring something into balance.
Try checking in again 15 minutes later. Then again after days or weeks of working with the same herb. What begins as a subtle shift might accumulate into something more defined. These impressions are just as valid—arguably more so—than anything you’ll read in a book.
Because this is your body. Your constitution. Your direct experience.
And that’s where the real learning lives: not just in the theories or charts—but in the quiet, felt sense of what a plant actually does when it meets you.
