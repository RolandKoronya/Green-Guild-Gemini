Introduction to the 6 Tissue States
There’s a quote from herbalist Matthew Wood that deserves a moment:
“Modern science and medicine are reductionistic… [they] ignore the possibility that there is a unified being, consciousness, identity pattern, or functional whole… Holism, in contrast, concerns itself with this underlying, unifying entity.”
It’s one of those statements that, once you read it, you can’t unsee its implications.
Because at the heart of it is a basic divide: the difference between seeing a person as a collection of parts—or as a coherent whole.
Modern medicine, by design, zooms in. It isolates. It slices the body into smaller and smaller components in order to make sense of the whole. And in many ways, it has done that job remarkably well. But in doing so, it’s also lost something. The thread that connects the pieces. The pattern that gives rise to the person.
Holistic systems, by contrast, look for that thread first.
In the world of constitutional medicine, this often shows up in the form of broad types: the Elements, the Doshas, the humors. We talk about people being vata, pitta, or kapha. Or melancholic, sanguine, choleric, phlegmatic. These archetypes help us understand the person’s nature—their default setting, their long-range patterns.
But here’s where it gets interesting.
What if the imbalance isn’t in the person as a whole… but in a specific part?
This is where most constitutional models start to fall short. Because while someone may be constitutionally dry and cold in general (a classic vata pattern), it’s entirely possible for them to develop a damp imbalance in, say, the lungs. And if we treat the whole person—rather than the part that’s actually in distress—we can easily do more harm than good.
It’s not just about the person’s innate constitution. It’s about what’s happening in their tissues right now.
In Ayurveda, this distinction is expressed as prakruti and vikruti. The former is the inborn constitution—the person’s deep-rooted pattern. The latter is their current state—the assumed constitution of the moment. One is who they are. The other is what they’re going through.
Both matter. Both must be seen.
And this brings us to something remarkable. A system of tissue-level diagnosis that emerged not from India or China, but from North America.
In the early 1900s, a group of practitioners known as the Physiomedicalists developed a system for reading the current energetic state of the tissues. They called it the Six Tissue States. It was first written down by Dr. Joseph Thurston in The Philosophy of Physiomedicalism (1900), and later taught—revived, really—by modern herbalist Matthew Wood.
It’s a model that doesn’t reject modern physiology. In fact, as Wood points out, it’s compatible with it. But it also doesn’t lose sight of the older traditions. It speaks the language of energetics and offers a kind of bridge—between systems, between eras, between ways of knowing.
And it gives us something invaluable: a framework for seeing not just the person, but the moment they’re in.
If the body is an ecosystem, then the tissues are its climate zones—each with its own patterns, tendencies, and imbalances. And like weather, those patterns rarely exist in isolation.
The Six Tissue States are rooted in three fundamental qualities: temperature, moisture, and tone. You’ve seen these before—hot or cold, damp or dry, tense or relaxed. Each exists on a polarity. But when you begin combining them, a deeper structure reveals itself.
This is where the Physiomedicalists gave us something invaluable. By organizing these three polarities into six patterns, they created a language for what imbalance feels like in the body.
Here they are:
Heat / Excitation
Cold / Depression
Dry / Atrophy
Damp / Stagnation
Wind / Tension
Damp / Relaxation
Notice the dual terms.
The first word often reflects the traditional Greco-Arabic terminology—terms like “wind” and “heat.” The second reflects North American clinical observation from the 19th and 20th centuries: words like “depression,” “atrophy,” “relaxation.” These are not poetic flourishes. They describe what the tissue is doing—not just physically, but sometimes emotionally as well.
In fact, many of these terms double as psychological states.
Tension. Excitation. Depression. These are patterns you can observe in both muscle fibers and mood. That overlap matters. Because the mind is not separate from the tissue—it’s expressed through it.
But here’s the reality: people rarely fit into just one tissue state. Most present with blends. Cold and damp. Hot and tense. Dry and tight. The goal isn’t to categorize them into a neat box—it’s to learn how to read the landscape. To see which patterns dominate, and which ones are asking to be rebalanced.
And that leads to a deeper truth about this system: it’s not about disease. Not in the modern sense.
We’re not looking to name a pathology or eliminate a symptom. We’re asking a different kind of question.
Not “what disease is this?”
But “what is the nature of this tissue imbalance?”
Because once you know that, the herbs almost choose themselves.
Take two people with bronchitis. One has a hot, dry cough that’s raspy and irritating. The other has a cold, wet cough that’s boggy and heavy. Same diagnosis. Two completely different internal landscapes. And if you treat them the same—if you default to a single remedy for a disease name—you miss the point. You miss the person.
Modern medicine sees pathogens as invaders. The treatment is often simple: kill the microbe. But herbal energetics asks a different question: Why did the body become hospitable to this in the first place? How do we shift the terrain?
This isn’t a mechanical view of the human organism. It’s an ecological one.
Now, one last thing before we move on.
When we say “hot,” we don’t just mean a fever. When we say “dry,” we’re not just talking about dehydration. These qualities are not literal—they’re experiential. “Heat” might show up as overactivity, breakdown, upward movement. “Damp” might mean sluggishness, accumulation, loss of clarity or tone. The thermometer doesn’t always tell the full story.
That’s your job: to see the underlying quality behind the symptom.

You’ll learn how they show up in different organ systems, how to recognize them, and which types of plants tend to shift them. This is where herbal energetics becomes something more than theory. This is where it becomes practical.
Because once you understand the patterns—once you see the landscape—you begin to work with the body, not against it.
