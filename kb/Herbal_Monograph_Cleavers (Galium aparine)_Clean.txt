Cleavers (Galium aparine)
If you’ve ever walked through a forest clearing or brushed up against the edge of a garden and come away with a few green hitchhikers stuck to your clothes, chances are, you’ve met Cleavers.
Also known as Grip Grass, Bedstraw, Goose Grass, Cleaverwort, Hedgeriff, and—perhaps most memorably—Sticky-willie, this plant is hard to ignore once you’ve encountered it. In Greek, it’s called Aparine. In German, Klebkraut. And in French, Gratteron. The names may change, but the experience is always the same: this is a plant that clings.
Cleavers belongs to the Rubiaceae family and uses its tiny hooks to climb and sprawl across its surroundings, often forming dense mats that dominate wherever they land. But it’s not picky about where that might be. You’ll find it in gardens, on disturbed lands, tucked into moist, shady corners of forests—places with just enough damp and shadow to make it feel at home.
Its range is impressive. Cleavers is native to North America, Europe, and Asia. And the part of the plant we use? The aerial parts, gathered while in flower. That’s when it’s most vibrant. Most alive. Most ready to offer what it has.
And that’s where the real story of Cleavers begins.

Some plants slip quietly into your life. Others walk in, sit down, and stay. For me, Cleavers was the latter.
It wasn’t dramatic. It didn’t require a revelation. But from the very beginning of my herbal journey, this modest little plant carved out a space. It grows in wild abundance in temperate Europe, trailing along hedges and popping up wherever the soil is damp and welcoming. I used to sit with it—literally—just being near it for long stretches of time. And over the years, it has become not just a plant I use in practice, but a kind of companion. Not flashy. Not loud. But deeply, quietly helpful.
What makes Cleavers so compelling isn’t just its presence. It’s what it does—and what it speaks to.
Let’s start with the obvious: taste. Cleavers has a taste that feels alive—fresh, green, and mildly sweet. There’s a salty, almost mineral-like undertone, and just the slightest hint of astringency. It’s not the kind of puckering astringency you’d find in a strong black tea or oak bark, but something subtle. You feel it in the background, not the foreground. And those qualities—sweet, salty, mildly astringent—are clues. They often signal a relationship with the kidneys, the urinary tract, and the mucosa. In other words, the inner waters of the body.
Now, in herbal terms, Cleavers is what we call an alterative. That means it helps shift things—gently moving and cleansing where stagnation has taken root. Not by forcing. Not by purging. But by encouraging motion where there has been stillness. You see this especially in the lymphatic system, the kidneys, and the urinary tract. But there’s more. Cleavers is often used as a skin tonic. Its effects ripple outward. So while its primary action is as a diuretic and lymphagogue alterative, it also supports the mucosa (thanks to that astringency), and can act as a vulnerary—a wound healer—when applied topically.
You begin to see a pattern. A plant that works with the fluids of the body doesn’t just stop at one system. It moves—literally and figuratively. As an alterative, Cleavers is sometimes called a “blood purifier.” But what we’re really talking about here is the matrix—the space between the cells, the extracellular fluids that connect and cushion and communicate. And when Cleavers works here, it’s not just detoxifying—it’s participating in a larger reset. One that eventually shows itself in the skin, in the clarity of the tissues, in the unblocking of what was stuck.
Some even speak of Cleavers as a mild nervine, capable of soothing nerve pain. And that idea makes sense, especially if you see stagnation not just as a physical condition, but an emotional one. Because that’s the other thing: Cleavers doesn’t just clear the skin. It clears the things behind the skin—the heat, the irritation, the frustration that couldn’t find a way out. When those emotions build and have nowhere to go, they look for an exit. Sometimes that exit is a rash. Sometimes it’s a flare. Sometimes it’s just that gnawing sense that something isn’t moving.
Cleavers helps it move.
And maybe that’s why I connected with it so early on. Because it doesn’t just support the body—it speaks to a truth: that healing isn’t always about what’s on the surface. Sometimes it’s what lies underneath, waiting to be seen.
Let’s talk about moisture. Not the abstract concept, but the very real, biological, and surprisingly nuanced quality that defines how Cleavers works in the body.
It’s easy to assume that a plant either moistens or dries. But Cleavers? It straddles the line.
On the surface, it’s a diuretic. Which means: it makes you pee. Fluids leave the body. And when fluids leave, dryness often follows—at least over time. But in the short term, something else happens. Cleavers doesn’t just flush the urinary system—it soothes it. It’s cooling. It’s moistening. And for someone with an inflamed, irritated, or dried-out mucous membrane in the bladder or urinary tract, it brings relief. Like watering a parched garden before the sun rises.
So is Cleavers drying or moistening?
The answer is: yes. It depends on the timeline. And context.
This duality is part of what makes Cleavers such a valuable remedy for a very specific tissue state known as damp/stagnation. It’s not just a poetic phrase—it’s a real pattern in the body. Think cold limbs, sluggish digestion, dull senses, weight gain, foggy thinking, water retention, chronic joint stiffness, low metabolism. The body isn’t processing well. Fluids aren’t moving. Elimination is sluggish. Everything’s just… stuck.
And Cleavers doesn’t fight that. It moves with it. Its alterative, lymphatic, astringent, and diuretic qualities all come into play here, working together to ease that burden and get things flowing again. This isn’t a forceful detox. It’s more like lifting a fog. Helping the body remember how to do what it’s already designed to do.
But here’s the twist. Cleavers doesn’t only serve the damp and stagnant. It also shows up for the dry and atrophic.
When the body lacks fluid—not just in the urinary tract, but systemically—it begins to collapse inward. The lymphatic system, which is built almost entirely of fluids, suffers the most. Filtration slows. Toxins build. Nutrition at the cellular level is compromised. The immune system weakens, not because the cells are gone, but because they can’t move. They’re stuck in a shallow stream that used to be a river.
And what happens then?
The glands swell. They harden. You can feel the congestion—not as heat, but as a kind of internal stiffening, like the body forgot how to breathe. Cleavers, again, speaks to this. It supports that movement. It doesn’t force hydration—it creates the conditions where hydration is more likely. Where flow becomes possible again.
So yes, Cleavers is a diuretic. But that’s just the beginning of the story. Because what it really does is pay attention. It notices where things aren’t moving, and it nudges them gently. Whether that’s too much water that needs to drain, or too little fluid in a stagnant lymph node, it knows how to adjust.
And that’s what makes Cleavers not just useful—but wise.
The lymphatic system is one of those parts of the body we rarely think about—until it stops working the way it should. It doesn’t pump like the heart or churn like the stomach. Its movement is quieter, subtler, and entirely dependent on flow. When that flow slows, things get murky. Literally.
That’s where plants like Cleavers come in.
As a lymphagogue, Cleavers specializes in movement—specifically in helping the lymphatic system drain, clear, and reset. It’s like maintenance for the immune system’s filtration system. When white blood cells have done their job and pathogens have been broken down, someone needs to take out the trash. That someone, more often than not, is the lymph.
But after a tough bout of illness—cough, flu, cold—those inner waters can become overloaded. Dead cells, leftover bacteria or viruses, cellular waste… it all builds up. The lymph slows. Nodes swell. You feel it as apathy, mental fog, fatigue, even low-grade fever. It’s not just physical stagnation—it’s emotional, cognitive, energetic.
And the real twist? That stagnation isn’t always damp. Sometimes, it's dry.
Imagine a riverbed in summer. Cracked. Still. The problem isn’t that there’s too much water—but that there’s not enough movement. That’s what Cleavers addresses. It reintroduces flow. Not with force—but with freshness. Like a stream returning after drought.
David Hoffman once described Cleavers as “perhaps the best tonic remedy for the lymphatic system.” That’s not casual praise. Because Cleavers isn’t just a mover—it’s a tonic. That means it doesn’t just push the system; it supports it. Nourishes it. It’s an alterative, yes. A diuretic too. In this context, Cleavers helps the body clear obstructions, move deposits, and excrete waste through the kidneys. It's systemic cleansing, done quietly.
It’s also what herbalists call a relaxant diuretic. Unlike stimulant diuretics—which push fluids out of a cold, sluggish system—relaxant diuretics are used when there’s heat, tension, even irritation. Cleavers cools. It calms. It soothes. That’s the difference between kicking a door open and gently turning the knob.
And then there’s the metaphorical layer—the emotional profile. Matthew Wood speaks of Cleavers as “Deer medicine.” That image matters. The Deer is tall, thin, sensitive. Prominent joints. Cleavers doesn’t overpower. It doesn’t sedate. It offers clarity. Direction. And for those who live in their heads, or in a body that always feels just a little on edge, that’s a rare and precious thing.

Clinical Patterns
1.Urinary Tract Heat and Irritation: If you were to trace Cleavers’ strongest affinity in the body, you’d land squarely in the urinary tract. That’s where it does some of its most impressive work—quietly, efficiently, and with surprising range.
We tend to think of herbs like tools, as if each has a single, obvious use. But Cleavers is more like a key that fits several locks. It’s cooling. It’s soothing. And it excels in conditions marked by heat, irritation, and inflammation—especially in the delicate tissues of the urinary system.
Take cystitis, for example. Or a urinary tract infection. Or the searing discomfort of passing a kidney stone. Cleavers shows up like a calming presence in a storm. Its cooling properties help reduce heat. Its astringency stops bleeding. And its diuretic action flushes things through. Nothing is forced, but everything begins to move in the right direction.
That diuretic quality is more nuanced than it sounds. We often associate diuretics with “more urination.” But that’s just the symptom. The real action is cellular. Diuretics help cells release excess water, which then moves through the tissues, drains into the kidneys, and is eliminated. This makes Cleavers especially useful not just in cases of infection, but in things like edema—when water is held in the tissues—and even in high blood pressure caused by poor kidney function and salt retention. In these situations, it’s not just about clearing fluid. It’s about restoring flow.
This fluid dynamic plays out differently depending on the terrain. In the dry/atrophy state, for instance, the kidneys may be weak, underfunctioning. And yet paradoxically, we still see edema. How can dryness cause water stagnation? It’s counterintuitive—until you understand the mechanism. If the kidneys can’t filter properly, water stays behind. The result is dirty water, toxic blood, even stone formation. Not from excess, but from weakness. Not from too much, but from too little movement.
Old physicians used to describe calcium oxalate stones—by far the most common kind—as linked to nervous exhaustion. The nerves weaken, the organs follow. The bladder becomes slack, its walls prolapse. Sphincters weaken, leading to incontinence or leakage. It’s not just a kidney problem—it’s a systemic issue of tone, of resilience.
And this is exactly where Cleavers shines.
Because while it works beautifully in damp, irritated, hot conditions—it’s also helpful in dry ones, when there’s loss of tone, loss of control, loss of function. What links all these cases isn’t moisture or lack thereof. It’s irritation. Heat. And often, the anxiety that rides alongside both.
That’s the pattern I look for when I reach for Cleavers: heat in the urinary tract, accompanied by tension in the nervous system. Whether it’s pain disrupting sleep, inflammation stirring up anxiety, or discomfort fraying the edges of someone’s mood—Cleavers has a way of settling things. Not sedating, but settling. Cooling, clarifying, relieving.
In a system built on water, Cleavers is the current that restores the calm.

Like Burdock, Cleavers seems to walk a delicate line between the damp and the dry. On one hand, it treats dry skin conditions surprisingly well—eczema, psoriasis, dandruff, sunburns, cracked nipples, even surface tumors. On the other, it also works on damp-heat conditions—those greasy, weeping, inflamed skin states like oily acne or wet eczema. It’s flexible, responsive. But there’s a caveat: its long-term drying effect (thanks to its diuretic nature) means that for some dry skin types, it may eventually aggravate the very terrain it temporarily soothes.
Still, the range of application is striking. Cleavers has long been used for ulcerated tissue—especially tumors or ulcers of the mouth and throat. It was once a major remedy for diphtheria, back when herbs were frontline medicine. And it’s especially useful for skin infections in hidden or bendable spaces—the backs of knees, the creases of elbows, flexor surfaces that are warm, moist, and prone to inflammation.
Interestingly, most of these applications use Cleavers internally, not topically. That’s telling. The treatment of skin, in this case, isn’t skin-deep—it’s systemic. The skin is the messenger. The lymph is the medium. Cleavers is the translator.
That said, external use has a long tradition too. In Europe, Asia, and the Americas, Cleavers has been used topically for generations—and recent research is starting to catch up.
A 2020 study investigated the immunomodulatory activity and phytochemical profile of Cleavers infusions. The results? Both raw extracts and their fractions showed significant immunostimulatory and antioxidant activity. Which means that the tradition—of applying Cleavers to skin infections and wounds—isn’t just intuitive. It’s biochemical. The study concluded that these results “partially justify the traditional use of Cleavers as a topical remedy for skin infections and wounds.”
Then came another layer.
A separate study explored a methanol extract of Cleavers for its effect on human breast cancer cells. The findings were striking: the extract was cytotoxic to the cancerous cells, but left untransformed breast epithelial cells untouched. That’s a bold result. It doesn’t make Cleavers a cancer cure—not even close—but it does open the door to new questions, new research, and a renewed look at this humble hedgerow plant.
Because what we’re seeing isn’t just a skin herb. It’s a systemic mover. A quiet agent of resolution. A gentle ally that clears what no longer belongs—whether in the lymph, in the blood, or in the skin that reflects it all.

Pharmacological Data
When we talk about what makes a plant work, we often lean on traditions, energetics, patterns. But under all that—beneath the texture, taste, and experience—there are molecules. And those molecules, as it turns out, have their own stories.
Cleavers contains a mix of plant acids—caffeic, gallic, gallotannic, p-hydroxybenzoic, salicylic, citric. These aren’t just names to memorize. They represent a spectrum of functions: antioxidant, astringent, anti-inflammatory, cooling. The kinds of things that align perfectly with what we see Cleavers doing in practice.
There are coumarins too—volatile compounds responsible for that sweet, unmistakable smell of freshly cut hay. But they do more than smell nice. Coumarins help reduce edema by lowering capillary permeability. That means less fluid leakage, less swelling. It’s this precise mechanism that gives Cleavers its relevance in treating edema, chronic venous insufficiency, and varicose veins. In short: they help fluids stay where they belong.
Then there are the iridoid glycosides—asperuloside and acumin among them. Structurally, they’re monoterpenes with a bicyclic skeleton and a sugar tail. They originate from a chemical produced by certain ant species (Iridomyrmex), used for defense. That little origin story tells you something: these are bitter compounds, designed to push back. In plants, they tend to be anti-inflammatory, sometimes antimicrobial. In Cleavers, they’re present—but their role is murkier.
Eric Yarnell, an expert in botanical medicine, has noted that the iridoids in Cleavers might not be the primary players. Their contribution isn’t entirely clear. Which, in a way, is also telling. Sometimes a plant’s impact can’t be reduced to a single chemical hero. It’s more like a conversation among constituents—some louder, some quiet, all shaping the outcome together.
So yes, Cleavers is rich in chemistry. But its real magic lies in the synergy—how all these molecules interact not just with each other, but with the terrain of the human body.
Safety
For all its gentle nature, even Cleavers has its limits. And understanding them requires a bit of nuance.
Let’s start with the textbook cautions. You’ll often find Cleavers theoretically contraindicated in cases of diabetes, because of its diuretic action. And also in edemas caused by impaired heart or kidney function. That last one—seen in many herbal manuals—is more of a blanket rule: "Avoid diuretics if heart or kidney function is compromised." It's common. It's repeated. And yet… it doesn’t quite line up.
Because traditionally, that’s exactly how Cleavers has been used. For sluggish kidneys. For fluid retention. For slow lymph and systemic dampness. So when the books say "avoid," but the lineage says "use with care," what do you do?
You pause. You pay attention. And you look at the person in front of you.
From an energetic or constitutional perspective, Cleavers leans drying over time. It’s not dramatic, but long-term use can subtly reduce internal moisture—especially in vata-type individuals, or in those already constitutionally dry. In the short term, its moistening and cooling effects are often more noticeable. But drawn out over weeks or months, its drying nature can accumulate.
Which brings us to the broader pattern: Cleavers clears fluid, cools heat, calms inflammation. But it doesn’t build. It moves.
As for herb-drug interactions? None are known. It’s a quiet plant in that regard. It doesn’t tend to interfere. It tends to support. And when it’s used thoughtfully, that support is both broad and beautifully subtle.
Dosage and Preparation
Timing matters. Especially with Cleavers.
If you want to capture the plant at its peak, you need to meet it just as it begins to flower—or slightly before. Late spring to early summer, depending on where you are. That’s the window. Miss it, and you’ll find the energy of the plant shifting. Once Cleavers begins to go to seed, its medicinal potency begins to fade.
And while some herbs can be dried and tucked away for later, Cleavers isn’t one of them. Once it dries, something essential is lost. The bright, green vitality disappears. Its medicine, in large part, lives in its freshness. That’s why fresh preparations—especially tinctures and juices—are preferred. It’s not a matter of convenience. It’s a matter of potency.
Take the tincture, for instance. A 1:2 ratio in 75% alcohol works well, but only if it’s made from the fresh plant. And even then, it seems Cleavers doesn’t like to sit too long in alcohol. Scott Kloos notes that succulent herbs like Cleavers can lose their fresh taste after just a week of maceration. To him, that taste shift signals something deeper—a loss of medicinal quality. And I’ve seen the same thing. That green, almost grassy vitality vanishes. And with it, something therapeutic slips away.
Then there’s succus—the juice. This method may sound obscure, but it’s one of the oldest ways of working with plants. In the Amazon, traditional healers take leaves, roots, even flowers, pound them in a mortar, and squeeze out the liquid. Sometimes adding just a bit of water to move things along. They would gather around 30ml at a time. Slowly. Purposefully.
Today, we have juicers. But the principle is the same.
Cleavers juice can be used internally for issues like mouth or gut ulcers—30 to 60ml, up to four times a day. It can also be preserved with alcohol or frozen in ice cube trays for later use. Few plants lend themselves well to succus preparation. Cleavers is one of them.
Topically, fresh Cleavers shines as well. Compresses and poultices can be applied to wounds, burns, inflamed skin, or blemishes. Peter Holmes even notes its use—alongside internal dosing—for skin-related lymph cancers. Again, the emphasis is on freshness, directness, presence.
And then, of course, there are the seeds.
Roasted, they’ve long been used as a caffeine-containing coffee substitute. A small nod to Cleavers’ broader versatility—and its surprising depth.
Even Culpeper, writing in 1652, understood this plant’s reach:
“The Juyce of the Herb, and Seed together taken in Wine, helpeth those that are bitten with an Adder, by preserving the Heart from the Venom… The distilled Water drunk twice a day helpeth the yellow Jaundice… and stayeth Lasks and Bloody Fluxes.”
Which is to say: people have trusted Cleavers for centuries—not just for what it is, but for how and when it is used.
The key isn’t just knowing what Cleavers does. It’s knowing when to harvest it. How to prepare it. And when to listen closely… before that green, vibrant voice fades.
Formulation
Some herbs take center stage. Others work best in the background—steady, supportive, foundational. Cleavers is one of those.
It rarely dominates a formula. But when it’s present, it shifts the entire character of the blend—moving fluids, clearing pathways, supporting gentle, deep detox. It’s a lymphatic that listens before it acts. And in the hands of experienced formulators, that subtlety becomes a strength.
Glandular Support Formula III (Sharol Tilgner)
20–30% Astragalus (Astragalus membranaceus)
15–25% Red Root (Ceanothus spp.)
15–25% Ligustrum (Ligustrum lucidum)
15–25% Eleuthero (Eleutherococcus senticosus)
10–20% Cleavers (Galium aparine)
10–20% Calendula (Calendula officinalis)
This formula weaves together lymphatics (Cleavers, Red Root, Calendula) with immune tonics and glandular supports (Astragalus, Ligustrum, Eleuthero). It targets the mucous membranes, digestive tract, liver, and glandular tissues, restoring tone and resilience—especially in those who need immune normalization without overstimulation. Cleavers brings a cooling, moving quality to balance the deeper immune activation.
Taken as a liquid extract (30–60 drops in water, three times a day) or as a tea (1–2 teaspoons per cup, up to three times a day), this is a classic example of a formula doing many things well—but in a harmonized way.

Throat Infection Formula
20% Osha (Ligusticum porteri)
20% Echinacea flower (Echinacea angustifolia)
20% Sage (Salvia officinalis)
10% Figwort (Scrophularia nodosa)
10% Cleavers (Galium aparine)

This is a formula with structure. The first trio—Osha, Echinacea, Sage—forms the lead: stimulant expectorant, immune stimulant, antimicrobial. It’s bold, warming, direct. Sage alone brings not just antiseptic power, but also astringency—especially for post-nasal drip and raw, sore throats.
Then comes the second wave: the lymphagogues. Figwort leads, with Cleavers and Calendula lending their gentle precision. This isn’t about punching through infection. It’s about supporting the cleanup crew—the lymphatic system that carries away debris and modulates inflammation.
Goldenseal rounds things out as a synergist. It adds its tonic-astringent quality to tighten and tone the mucous membranes, enhancing both antiseptic action and structural resilience.
Cleavers fits here not as a hammer, but as a guide—pointing the immune system toward clarity and drainage, especially in swollen lymph around the neck and throat.
Eczema Triplet (David Hoffman)
1 part Cleavers (Galium aparine)
1 part Nettle leaf (Urtica dioica)
1 part Red Clover (Trifolium pratense)
This is herbal minimalism done well.
David Hoffman’s eczema prescription doesn’t overwhelm. It clears. This triplet focuses on the terrain beneath the skin—blood, lymph, liver, kidneys. When that terrain is congested, skin becomes a pressure valve. Inflammation rises, irritation spreads, and chronic eruptions become the norm.
Each herb plays its role. Red Clover is a classic lymphatic and blood purifier. Nettle brings minerals and gentle diuresis. Cleavers, as always, gets things moving quietly.
Hoffman recommends 5 mL of the tincture, three times daily. And when the season allows, an infusion of fresh Cleavers and Nettle, two to three times a day. It’s spring medicine. Earthy. Timed to renewal. But more than anything, it’s gentle. That’s the key.
As Hoffman notes, stronger hepatic alteratives can sometimes aggravate eczema, especially early on. Cleavers offers a different approach: not force, but fluidity. Not confrontation, but coaxing.
