Borage (Borago officinalis)
There’s something oddly satisfying about the name “borage.” It rolls off the tongue with a kind of gentle authority. And the deeper you go into its story, the more you realize how fitting that is. This is not a shy, retiring little herb. It has a reputation—earned over centuries—as a plant of courage.
Henslow, the English botanist, suggested that the name might come from the Celtic word barrach, which means “a man of courage.” Whether or not that etymology holds up to linguistic scrutiny, it tells you something important about how people saw this plant: bold, bristly, and brave.
The Latin name is Borago officinalis, and it belongs to the Boraginaceae family, making it a close cousin to comfrey. You can see the resemblance in the texture—those same rough, hairy leaves that feel a little like nature’s sandpaper. If your skin is on the sensitive side, gloves are a smart idea.
But here’s where things get interesting: Borage thrives where other plants hesitate. It doesn’t need rich soil. It doesn’t need to be pampered. It’s native to the Mediterranean and Europe, but this annual has made itself at home across the northern hemisphere, often turning up in the most unlikely places and spreading its seeds freely, almost exuberantly.
The scent of the leaves is unexpected—somewhere between cucumber and watermelon—and that flavor carries over into the kitchen. The leaves are typically harvested just as the plant comes into bloom, but the seeds don’t all ripen at once. They take their time, ripening in sequence, which is charming botanically, though admittedly inconvenient if you’re harvesting them by hand.
And then there are the flowers. Bright, pendulous, and star-shaped, they shift from blue to pink as they mature. They’re beautiful. They’re also edible. Toss a few into a salad on a hot summer day, and suddenly the dish comes alive with color and a kind of understated elegance. Bees, unsurprisingly, love them too.
It continues to be used in traditional herbal remedies and still finds its way into gardens and kitchens. In ancient times, it was infused into ales and wines—not just for flavor, but because it was believed to instill courage and chase away melancholy.
In other words, people didn’t just use borage. They trusted it. They believed in it. And that might be the most telling detail of all.

The taste of a plant is never just about flavor. It’s a clue. A quiet signal, encoded in the chemistry of the leaf, the flower, the seed. And in the case of Borage, the signal is clear: salty and slightly sweet.
That combination—salty and sweet—might not sound like much. But in herbal tradition, these are the tastes we look for when we’re trying to build something back up. The nervous system. The adrenals. The parts of us that falter when we’ve pushed too hard for too long. These tastes aren’t just comforting—they’re nutritive. And for good reason: they feed the systems we rely on most when we’re burned out. Borage, then, isn’t just another plant. It’s a quiet kind of medicine. An adaptogen, a restorative, for moments of exhaustion.
We see these same sweet-salty signals across the respiratory tract, the digestive system, the endocrine web, and the nervous network. Even in the way it encourages lactation—as a galactagogue—Borage leans toward nourishment.
There’s more. The aerial parts of the plant carry mucilage—a softening, moistening compound that’s cooling to the body. In practice, that means relief: Borage soothes mucus membranes, eases irritation in the lungs, and works as an expectorant by softening and loosening stubborn congestion. That same mucilage helps to heal tissue—whether you apply it to the skin or take it internally to calm the digestive tract. It’s a pattern, again, rooted in that sweet taste: softening, soothing, repairing.
Then there are the salts. Not table salt, but mineral-rich compounds—potassium and calcium especially—concentrated in the leaves. These promote diuresis: the gentle flushing action of the kidneys and urinary system.
And the oil? That’s another chapter. Extracted from the seeds, Borage oil is packed with polyunsaturated fats—more, even, than Evening Primrose. What makes it exceptional is its high gamma-linolenic acid (GLA) content. This fatty acid is known to modulate inflammation in the cardiovascular system, support hormone balance, and, again, strengthen the adrenal system. We’ll come back to that.
What we’re left with is a plant whose taste profile—sweet and salty—tells us exactly what it does: nourish, restore, and repair. Through mucilage and oil, mineral salt and softness, Borage finds its way into the systems that need support most. It cools. It moistens. It calms.
Specific Indications
There’s a story—part folklore, part instinct—that a woman once slipped Borage into a suitor’s drink to give him the courage to propose. Whether or not it worked isn’t really the point. What matters is the belief that it could. That this strange, bristly plant with its soft blue flowers held something more than nutrients and oils. Something deeper.
Pliny the Elder certainly thought so. He used Borage to comfort the heart. Not the organ, necessarily, but the feeling of it. The way it tightens when sorrow comes in. The way it sinks when hope is lost. And he wasn’t alone. For centuries, herbalists, housewives, and ancient physicians turned to Borage in moments of emotional collapse—melancholy, heaviness, exhaustion. Not to sedate or distract, but to uplift.
This is where Borage begins to take shape not just as a plant, but as a kind of archetype. There’s a person behind every plant, or perhaps more accurately, a pattern—a set of symptoms, temperaments, imbalances. The “Borage person” tends to come to you burned out. Not just tired, but depleted. The kind of weariness that isn’t fixed by sleep. Energetically low. Quietly anxious. Emotionally raw. Their hearts feel heavy, their confidence frayed. And though they may not say it out loud, what they need—what they’re hoping for—is courage.
That word again. Courage. Not in the battlefield sense, but in the everyday kind: facing change, facing oneself, facing another person and saying the truth.
If you look closely, the patterns start to appear. This is someone whose systems are out of balance—too much heat (rashes, fevers, hyperthyroid), too much wind (palpitations, insomnia), too much dryness (sore throats, bronchitis). And all of it layered on top of emotional depletion. These are the conditions in which Borage does its best work.
In the 17th century, herbalist John Gerard observed that Borage “comforteth the heart, purgeth melancholy, and quieteth the phrenticke and lunaticke person.” You’d expect some exaggeration—it was the style of the time—but what’s striking is how consistent the theme remains: joy, courage, upliftment. The leaves, he said, ingender good blood. The flowers, when put in wine, “make men and women glad and merry.” Even the syrup brought light to those lost in sadness.
And then there’s the modern echo. The Wild Rose College in Canada captured it well in a monograph on Borage flower essence. The language is poetic, but precise: Borage is for the heavy-hearted. Not the broken-hearted. That’s something else. This is about the quiet burden—the lack of confidence, the depressive behavior, the soul that’s lost its enthusiasm.
What Borage offers is not just energy. It’s vitality. A lifting of the heart’s weight. It brings gladness—not the fleeting kind, but something more foundational. A rekindling. It helps restore faith in oneself and in something larger. The result isn’t dramatic. It’s subtle: a newfound lightness in the chest, a reawakening of trust. You become, perhaps, a little more magnetic. Not because you’ve changed who you are, but because Borage reminded you who you were in the first place.
Clinical Patterns
1. Melancholy and Endocrine Exhaustion: There’s a particular kind of exhaustion that doesn’t just leave you tired. It leaves you diminished. Not only in body, but in spirit—in outlook, in confidence, in your ability to find meaning in the things that once energized you. And this is where Borage quietly enters the picture.
Traditionally, Borage has been used to uplift. That’s not just metaphorical. It’s literal—an upward motion through the heart, the mind, the body. Like Lemon Balm, it can bring lightness to a melancholic state, but it does something else, something more specific. Borage finds the person who’s tired of themselves. The one who’s been driven by impossible standards, who carries an internal critic louder than any external voice. The one who lies awake at night cycling through regrets, rewrites, self-scolding.
In these moments, Borage is not alone. Combine it with Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora), and you get something quietly powerful: a pairing that can interrupt the loop. Borage lifts. Skullcap soothes. Together, they help a person step off the mental carousel and catch their breath.
But Borage doesn’t just address mental fatigue. It reaches into the emotional layers too—the heart that feels dulled, the spirit that’s frayed. There’s even a role for it in the aftermath of indulgence: a night of too much wine, a morning steeped in the kind of fog that feels more existential than physical. That dull, helpless, what-was-the-point kind of feeling? Borage knows it. And it knows how to shift it.
It’s for the person whose vacation is long overdue. The one pushing through, holding things together, until burnout is not just a possibility—it’s inevitable. In that place, the adrenals—the body’s stress regulators—start to falter. Borage helps rebuild them. It doesn’t just mask anxiety; it begins to soften its source. It restores.
Interestingly, Borage contains silicon, a mineral with a subtle but profound influence on the nervous system. That’s part of what gives it its rebuilding power. For those in a state of ongoing nervousness—an overactive mind, a constant hum of anxiety—Borage becomes a gentle anchor.
Herbalist Stephen Buhner even mentions its usefulness in treating attention deficit disorder (ADD), and notes that the dried flower has shown promise for obsessive compulsive disorder (OCD). It’s not just about mood anymore. It’s about focus. About reclaiming clarity in the midst of mental noise.
We often assume that emotional relief is too intangible to measure. But occasionally, someone runs the numbers. In one six-week, placebo-controlled, double-blind trial involving 44 patients with diagnosed OCD, researchers found that Borage made a difference—statistically and experientially. A 10-point drop in the Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale, with a 6.5-point advantage over placebo. That’s not subtle. That’s measurable calm.
And the effects don’t stop at the emotional level. Borage has shown promise for treating diabetic neuropathy—a type of nerve damage common in long-standing diabetes. There’s even a place for it in formulations for insomnia. The reach of this one plant is broader than it appears.
But perhaps its most compelling role is in the quiet restoration of the endocrine system. Herbalist Matthew Wood speculates that Borage acts high up the regulatory ladder—at the level of the hypothalamus and pituitary. These are the control towers of the endocrine system. And Borage, it seems, speaks their language. Through its influence on these upper regulators, it acts as a tonic to the adrenal cortex—the seat of our stress response.
It becomes particularly relevant after prolonged medical use of corticosteroids. Borage helps revive the adrenal glands that have been suppressed by synthetic hormones. Matthew recommends pairing it with Burdock (Arctium lappa), not traditionally seen as an adrenal herb, but—interestingly—an oil-regulating one. And in Matthew’s view, the adrenal cortex is an oily structure. Burdock’s action fits. It helps rebuild.
There’s something else about adrenal fatigue that often goes unnoticed. In its early stages, cortisol levels tend to run high. But as the condition deepens, cortisol drops. And this is where things get complicated. Cortisol isn’t just a stress hormone—it’s also the body’s own anti-inflammatory agent. When it falls too low, the body’s tolerance for inflammation drops with it. Muscle pain, joint aches, irritation—they all intensify. The threshold for discomfort shrinks.
Borage responds to this, too. It doesn’t just help repair the endocrine exhaustion; it also cools the inflamed tissue that arises in its wake. It meets both the system and its symptoms. It works at the root and on the surface.
But here’s the truth, and it’s important: no plant, no matter how powerful, can substitute for rest. For the person on the edge of collapse—burnout no longer hypothetical but actual—herbs like Borage are not enough on their own. You need to pause. To disengage from the work, the urgency, the relentless productivity. Because the deeper cure lies in lifestyle. That’s the foundation.
Alongside that pause, there’s space to build a practice—something that draws in calm, that rebuilds inner coherence. Meditation. Mindfulness. Time in nature. And of course, collaboration with a healthcare practitioner, especially one trained in herbal medicine. Borage may be a part of that protocol. But the real work begins when you stop long enough to listen to what your body has been trying to say all along.
2. Borage and the Chemistry of Inflammation: Let’s start with a number: 20%.
That’s roughly how much gamma-linolenic acid (GLA) you’ll find in Borage seed oil. It’s an omega-6 fatty acid, also found in Evening Primrose and Black Currant, but Borage seems to lead the pack when it comes to concentration. And it’s this compound—GLA—that positions Borage as one of the more compelling anti-inflammatory herbs in modern phytotherapy.
GLA works by converting into prostaglandin E1 (PGE1)—a hormone-like substance that dials down inflammation. That’s the mechanism. But the effect? That’s where things get interesting.
Borage oil has been studied across a surprising range of inflammatory conditions: cardiovascular issues like phlebitis (inflammation of veins), atherosclerosis (the hardening of arteries), and even hypertension. It shows up in thyrotoxicosis, where excess thyroid hormone fuels metabolic fire, and in dyslipidemia, where cholesterol levels go sideways.
But one of its most promising applications may be in the world of rheumatoid arthritis (RA). In 2014, a study followed 74 participants for 18 months. Each was given either 1.8 grams of Borage oil, 2.1 grams of fish oil, or both. The outcome? A reduction in RA symptoms—enough, the researchers noted, that some patients could potentially substitute these oils in place of NSAIDs, thereby sidestepping their well-known side effects.
It’s a compelling proposition. But—as always—the human body adds its own complexity. If you’re deficient in vitamin C, zinc, or magnesium, your body may struggle to absorb GLA at all. The delivery system matters.
And here’s where Borage starts to reveal its elegant internal logic. While it’s the seeds that provide the concentrated GLA, the leaves themselves are rich in exactly the nutrients that help with GLA absorption: calcium, iron, potassium, zinc, B vitamins, vitamin C, beta carotene, choline, and fiber. It’s a quiet reminder that sometimes, the whole plant contains the answer to its own puzzle.
That’s the dilemma—and the insight. Whole plant vs. extract. Most research supports the use of standardized Borage seed oil for delivering consistent GLA in therapeutic doses. But with it, you lose the synergy—the minerals, the fibers, the contextual co-factors that come packaged in the leaf.
It’s not a question of right or wrong. It’s a question of what you’re trying to do—and how deep you’re willing to go.
3. Cold Comfort: There are few sensations more unmistakable than a dry, scratchy throat that signals something is coming. A cold. Maybe the flu. The body tightens. The chest starts to feel heavy. And somewhere in the background, the immune system begins its quiet, desperate scramble.
This is where Borage has something to say.
Thanks to its cooling, moistening qualities—and its notably high mucilage content—Borage has long been used to support the lungs during respiratory illness. It doesn’t target a single condition, but rather addresses a whole family of symptoms: asthma, bronchitis, pleurisy, sore throats, and the garden variety cold or flu.
The form matters. While a tincture might help, volume matters more in acute cases. Think tea. Think syrup. Better yet, combine them. A warm infusion of Borage tea, paired with Borage syrup made from the aerial parts, offers both immediate relief and deeper support.
The flowers, interestingly, are especially diaphoretic. That means they help open the pores, stimulate sweating, and assist the body in breaking a fever. Matthew Wood notes that Borage is particularly useful when that fever is accompanied by mucus. And that makes sense—mucilage helps loosen the congestion and lift it out of the lungs.
Pair it with Marshmallow (Althaea officinalis), and the result is even more effective. The combination softens, soothes, breaks down resistance—in the best possible way.
But Wood goes deeper. He writes that “Borage strengthens nature.” It’s a curious phrase. Not clinical. Not chemical. But powerful. What he means is this: Borage helps the body build resilience. It tones the sweat pores, sharpens the immune response, and restores the ability to meet illness with strength—not just medication.
In the end, it’s not about suppressing the symptoms. It’s about supporting the body’s ability to respond.
4. The Repair Work of Borage: The body keeps score—but it also remembers how to heal. And Borage, in its quietly persistent way, seems to understand both.
Internally, it makes its way through the digestive system, not with force, but with support. For those dealing with gastritis, stomach aches, or the lingering tissue damage from irritable bowel syndrome, Borage offers a kind of relief that’s not just about easing pain—it’s about rebuilding. The mucilage, again, is key. It soothes inflamed linings, calms irritated tissue, and—at just the right dose—acts as a mild laxative, helping to bring gentle lubrication to the lower GI. For the person dealing with occasional constipation, that small nudge can be exactly what’s needed.
But Borage doesn’t stop at the gut.
On the surface—on the skin—it takes on a whole new set of roles. Atopic dermatitis, eczema, psoriasis, herpes, ringworm, nail fungus, athlete’s foot, mouth ulcers, inflamed eyes—the list grows, and Borage meets each one with the same fundamental logic: cool, soothe, restore. Its anti-inflammatory and emollient properties begin to soften what’s raw, to calm what’s reactive.
Even pimples, boils, abscesses, and rashes fall within its domain. When fresh Borage leaves are finely crushed—fine enough to neutralize the irritating hairs—and applied as a poultice, they become a balm for insect bites, stings, swelling, and bruises. Not dramatic. Not flashy. Just quietly effective.
What all of this points to is a plant that doesn't simply reduce symptoms. It participates in the rebuilding of tissue—externally and internally. It heals not by overpowering the body, but by reminding it how to repair itself.
5. The Hormonal Intelligence of Borage: There’s a moment—often late in pregnancy—when the body begins preparing for something monumental. And one of the key steps in that preparation is cervical ripening. The tissue softens. The body readies itself for labor.
It turns out that Borage seed oil can assist with that.
This isn’t folklore. It’s a practice—common and specific. At 38 weeks, an expectant mother might take 500 mg of Borage oil twice daily, or use it in a perineal massage. The idea is simple: by supporting the body’s own readiness, Borage oil may help shorten labor, reduce pain, and avoid more aggressive interventions. But timing matters. Outside of this narrow window, Borage is not considered safe during pregnancy. Here, precision is everything.
After the birth, though, Borage shifts roles—still supportive, but in a new way. It becomes a galactagogue, helping to stimulate breast milk production. And that makes sense. Borage’s defining energetic—moistening, nourishing—fits this stage of motherhood almost perfectly.
But its gifts extend further. Nervous exhaustion, mood swings, and even postpartum depression—these are not uncommon in the weeks after delivery. Borage doesn’t solve them outright. But it can soften the emotional edges, restore a sense of lightness, and support a depleted nervous system. Here, pulse dosing is recommended—short, intermittent use—due to the presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids (a detail we’ll return to in the safety section). The key isn’t constant use—it’s thoughtful use, in moments when the heart feels heavy and the body weary.
And it doesn’t stop there.
Borage oil has also proven helpful across the hormonal spectrum: PMS, perimenopause, and menopause. For mood swings, depression, hot flashes, and breast pain, it supports the transition—particularly through its action on the adrenals, which are often under strain as hormone production shifts. Adrenal fatigue is common during menopause, and Borage steps in as a quiet restorative, offering balance when the system is in flux.
It’s also been used in protocols for endometriosis, and for dysmenorrhea—the intense, recurring cramps that accompany menstruation for so many. Borage doesn’t mask the symptoms. It works with the body to ease and support—to make space for healing without forcing it.
In each of these phases—pregnancy, postpartum, menopause—Borage seems to understand what’s needed: softness, nourishment, a return to balance. It’s not dramatic. It’s not loud. But it’s deeply aligned with the rhythms of the body.
Pharmacological Data
The more you study plants like Borage, the more you realize they aren’t doing just one thing. They’re doing many things, at once, in parallel. And often, those actions begin with chemistry.
Start with the seeds.
Pressed from them is an oil composed mainly of glycerides and fatty acids, most notably linoleic acid and up to 24% gamma-linolenic acid (GLA)—a strikingly high concentration. You’ll also find oleic acid, another familiar fatty acid known for its heart-healthy profile. These are the building blocks behind Borage’s anti-inflammatory and hormone-balancing effects.
But the seeds also carry a footnote: they contain pyrrolizidine alkaloids—the same compounds found in Comfrey. The levels are low—well below accepted safety standards for most extracts—but the presence reminds us that nature rarely offers power without some measure of caution.
Now shift focus to the leaf and flower. These parts are chemically rich, and they read like a checklist of therapeutic potential:
Mucilaginous polysaccharides – soothing, softening, moistening.
Tannins – astringent and wound-healing.
Silicic acid, calcium, iron, potassium, zinc – minerals essential for rebuilding and regulation.
Saponins – likely contributors to Borage’s expectorant effect.
Allantoin – the compound behind Borage’s and Comfrey’s shared ability to heal tissue and promote cell regeneration.
And again, those pyrrolizidine alkaloids, present in both saturated and unsaturated forms.
It’s a fascinating overlap: Borage and Comfrey, both in the Boraginaceae family, both carrying these complex chemistries—healing and risk held in the same leaf.
But what about the how?
One proposed mechanism is that Borage can prolong the action of corticosterone—a hormone central to the stress response. The precise mechanism is still unknown. But the effect is visible: Borage acts as an adrenal restorative, and that restoration contributes to its broader anti-inflammatory profile.
The omega-6 fatty acids, especially GLA, regulate hormones and quell inflammation. The high calcium and potassium content promotes osmotic diuresis—which is a more elegant way of saying it helps the kidneys flush waste more effectively. And the mucilage? That brings the soothing, antispasmodic action to the mucous membranes, calming irritation wherever it goes.
In short, Borage isn’t operating through a single pathway. It’s layered, multifunctional, and—like many of nature’s best medicines—it’s quietly doing more than we think.
Safety
Every good remedy has its boundaries. And Borage, for all its nourishing, restorative, and emotionally uplifting qualities, is no exception.
The concern centers on a group of compounds called pyrrolizidine alkaloids (PAs). These occur naturally in many plants, including Borage. The concentration in commercial leaf samples tends to be low—somewhere around 2 to 10 parts per million—but that doesn't mean they're benign.
Once metabolized in the liver, these alkaloids can be converted into reactive pyrroles, which then set off a cascade of damage. The risk is serious: hepatic veno-occlusive disease, venous thrombosis, ascites, jaundice, and in the long term, an increased risk of liver cancer. It’s a potent reminder that “natural” doesn’t always mean “harmless.”
That said, there’s a crucial distinction. The oil pressed from ripe Borage seeds—the very oil used in most therapeutic applications—is devoid of these toxic compounds. The alkaloids simply don’t pass into the oil during cold-pressing. So while caution is warranted with internal use of the leaves, the seed oil remains safe, even for longer-term use.
As for the leaves, most recommendations suggest keeping internal use to no more than 4–6 weeks, or in some cases, 2–3 months at a time. It’s also worth noting that young leaves contain fewer alkaloids than older ones, and that Comfrey, a close cousin, typically contains much higher levels of these compounds.
But the warning is clear: Borage is not recommended during pregnancy, and it should also be avoided by anyone with pre-existing liver conditions—a prudent precaution in uncertain territory.
And then there’s the question of interactions.
Borage oil, interestingly, acts as a platelet inhibitor. That means it can thin the blood—a property that becomes problematic when taken alongside anticoagulant medications. In theory, it could amplify the effects of these drugs, increasing the risk of bleeding. For anyone on blood thinners, it’s not a plant to take casually. Consultation with a healthcare provider is essential.
The same caution applies if someone is taking hepatotoxic medications—drugs known to stress or damage the liver. This includes anabolic steroids, phenothiazines (used for serious psychiatric conditions), and ketoconazole, a common antifungal. In these cases, layering Borage into the mix could tip the scales toward harm.
Borage is not a dangerous plant. But it’s a complex one. Like many powerful allies, it works best when handled with awareness—respect for its gifts, and respect for its limits.
Dosage and Preparations
When it comes to Borage, freshness matters. That’s the recurring theme across nearly every form of preparation. And once you understand the chemistry—particularly the mucilage content—it starts to make sense. The dried plant loses potency fast, and in some cases, may become ineffective altogether. Some herbalists even go so far as to say that dried Borage is useless.
Start with the tincture. Because of the mucilage, high-proof alcohol isn’t ideal—there’s a risk that the polysaccharides will precipitate out, reducing effectiveness. Sharol Tilgner recommends a 1:1 ratio in 25% alcohol, which preserves the fresh plant gently. Still, for some practitioners, that feels low. A bump up to 30–35% alcohol is often preferred for better shelf life, without compromising the mucilage. 1 ml, three times a day is usually sufficient.
Then there’s tea—perhaps the simplest, and in many ways, the most traditional method. Use two tablespoons of fresh herb, infused and taken three times a day. The dried version just doesn’t hold up the same way. Grieves, writing in another era, suggested an infusion made from 1 ounce of leaves to 1 pint of boiling water, taken in wineglassful doses. You get the sense that Borage wasn’t just a remedy—it was part of daily life.
The seed oil is the modern favorite. Easy to standardize, easy to dose. 500 mg per day, or up to 1–4 capsules daily depending on need. This is where the GLA content comes into play—the part of Borage that gets most of the scientific attention.
And then the flowers. They aren’t just beautiful—they’re edible, delicate, and striking. Toss them into a salad, or eat them fresh from the garden. There’s even an older tradition of candying the flowers into a conserve, used to support recovery after long illness, or to revive the faint-hearted. Grieves noted that distilled water made from the flowers was once used for inflammation of the eyes.
For emotional and energetic support, Borage flower essence is taken in 3–4 drops, 2–4 times daily. It’s subtle, but specific—working more on mood than on organ systems.
Finally, there’s the succus—the juice of the fresh leaves. 10 ml, twice daily, taken as a tonic. This is a more old-world approach, but one that captures the living essence of the plant in its rawest form.
What’s striking about Borage is not just the variety of forms it can take, but the fact that each method reflects a slightly different facet of its personality—restorative, uplifting, moistening, anti-inflammatory. The trick is in choosing the form that fits the person, and the moment.
Formulation
Nicholas Culpepper—never one to mince words—once remarked that in his day, “the dried herb is never used, but the green.” Yet even after the plant had been reduced to ashes, he saw value. Boiled in mead or honeyed water, the residue was still used as a gargle for inflammation and ulcers in the mouth or throat.
That’s the kind of legacy Borage carries: a plant whose vitality remains—even in decay.
But today, it’s in formulations where Borage shines most. Not just as a standalone remedy, but as part of a therapeutic team, designed with intention.
Take the Sleepy Time Tincture.
25% Borage (Borago officinalis)
25% Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora)
25% Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)
25% Chamomile (Matricaria recutita)
It’s crafted for that modern paradox: the wired and tired. The mind won’t stop circling. The body’s exhausted. Sleep feels distant. This blend calms the mind, soothes the spirit, and invites rest. One teaspoon 30 minutes before bed, again at bedtime, and once more if needed in the middle of the night. It’s a slow descent into sleep, rather than a forced shutdown.
Then there’s the Nerve Trophorestorative:
33% Borage
33% Milky Oats (Avena sativa)
33% Skullcap
This is deeper work. It’s for the burned-out—those navigating chronic stress, depleted reserves, and a nervous system stretched too thin. It nourishes, restores, and over time, supports repair of the myelin sheath that protects nerve cells. It’s gentle enough for daily use, yet powerful enough to ease headaches, neurasthenia, and the slow unraveling of resilience.
The next formula is more complex: Heart and Mind, from herbalist Michael Tierra.
Borage
Hawthorn berries (Crataegus monogyna)
Red Sage root (Salvia miltiorrhiza)
Dong Quai (Angelica sinensis)
Tienchi Ginseng (Panax pseudoginseng)
Motherwort (Leonurus cardiaca)
Seneca Snakeroot (Polygala senega)
Juniper (Juniperus communis)
Codonopsis (Codonopsis pilosula)
No ratios are given. It’s a larger formula than most Western herbalists might prepare—nine herbs, several drawn from Chinese medicine. But the strategy is clear: circulation (Juniper, Dong Quai), liver support (Motherwort, Juniper), adaptogens (Tienchi Ginseng, Codonopsis, Borage), and nervine support (Borage, Motherwort, Hawthorn). The heart lies at the center, supported from every angle—emotionally, physiologically, energetically.
And the Cardiac Nervine:
20% Borage
20% Motherwort
20% Linden (Tilia europea)
20% Hawthorn flower
10% Lemon Balm
10% Rose flower
This one is subtle. It soothes the nervous system, but specifically in the heart region—where emotion and physiology meet. It’s uplifting, a Shen tonic in the Chinese tradition, offering support for those navigating melancholy, depression, or emotional palpitations. There are times when the world feels like too much—when stress outpaces rest, when depletion becomes baseline. For many, adrenal fatigue isn’t theoretical. It’s lived.
That’s where this formula comes in:
20% Burdock (Arctium lappa)
20% Reishi (Ganoderma lucidum)
20% Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)
20% Milky Oats (Avena sativa)
15% Borage (Borago officinalis)
5% Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra)
This is not a short-term intervention. It’s a long-haul restoration. A formula designed for the aftermath of burnout—or better yet, as a preventative tonic. Taken as a tea or tincture, it supports adrenal resilience over months, not days.
But there’s a caveat: Borage. Because of its pyrrolizidine alkaloids (PAs), it may be wise to pulse-dose—take breaks, allow the liver time to reset. The rest of the formula does the heavy lifting: Burdock supports detox, Reishi calms the immune system, Ashwagandha steadies energy, Milky Oats rebuilds the nerves, and Licorice acts as a cortisol modulator. And Borage? It’s the one that uplifts.
Because with the state of the world today, who couldn’t use a little more lift?
Now shift to the nursing mother. The one feeding a child from her own reserves.
Mother’s Milk is a formula of replenishment and flow:
25% Blessed Thistle (Cnicus benedictus)
25% Nettle Leaf (Urtica dioica)
20% Borage
20% Fenugreek (Trigonella foenum-graecum)
10% Fennel Seed (Foeniculum vulgare)
Each herb is a galactagogue—a promoter of breast milk. But they arrive with their own angles: Blessed Thistle and Borage provide oiliness and moisture, Fenugreek adds demulcent softness, while Fennel contributes a warming, carminative effect that can be passed through the milk—potentially soothing a colicky baby.
And Nettle? It’s the quiet engine. Packed with minerals and nutrients, it helps replenish what’s lost in the endless cycle of feeding.
Finally, the Menopause / Hot Flash formula:
25% Borage
25% Motherwort (Leonurus cardiaca)
25% Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa)
25% Blue Vervain (Verbena hastata)
Four herbs, one purpose: support the menopausal transition. Whether the symptom is hot flashes, palpitations, emotional volatility, insomnia, or nervous tension, this formula speaks to them all.
It works on the heart and nerves, but also the liver, thanks to the bitterness of Motherwort and Blue Vervain. It’s cooling, antispasmodic, sedative, and deeply grounding—designed not just to treat symptoms, but to support the shift itself.
These aren’t just combinations of plants. They’re strategies—responses to modern pressures, ancient cycles, and the timeless need for resilience.
