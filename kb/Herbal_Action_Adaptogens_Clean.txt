Adaptogens
Adaptogens may be the most talked-about herbal category in modern alternative health. You see the word everywhere—on supplement bottles, wellness blogs, even in supermarket teas. But here’s the twist: as popular as they are, adaptogens are also among the most misunderstood.
The term itself is relatively new. It didn’t come from herbalists, but from Soviet scientists in the 1960s. They were studying a plant—Eleuthero—and noticed that it didn’t quite fit into any traditional herbal category. Its effects were broad. Nonspecific. And so they coined a term: adaptogen.
By their definition, an adaptogen is a plant that increases the body’s resistance to stress. Not a particular kind of stress—just… stress. Internal, external, emotional, physical—it didn’t matter. Adaptogens were said to help the body adapt.
The idea caught on.
And for good reason. Who wouldn’t want something that buffers stress, supports resilience, and smooths out the highs and lows of daily life? Adaptogens were soon described as “normalizing,” “balancing,” and, most importantly, safe—something anyone could take, anytime.
But here’s where the story gets more complicated.
Because if we take a closer look—especially through the lens of clinical herbalism, rather than laboratory science—we start to see cracks in that narrative. And we begin to realize: maybe we need to rethink what adaptogens actually are.
The classic list of adaptogens keeps growing. But interestingly, a team of scientists—Panossian, Wikman, and Wagner—revisited the original definition. After reviewing the research, they concluded that only three plants truly met the criteria: Eleuthero, Rhodiola, and Schisandra.
Just three.
And even they questioned the idea that adaptogens were “normalizing.” They questioned the assumption that they’re universally safe. They questioned the very framework that put so many plants into this one oversized category.
Take Panax ginseng, for example. It’s often described as adaptogenic. But there’s nothing normalizing about it. It’s stimulating. Warming. And for someone with a hot constitution, it can actually make things worse.
So what’s going on here?
The answer lies in where the idea came from. The adaptogen model is scientific in origin, and that has consequences. It’s rooted in reductionism—looking at isolated compounds, standardized extracts, and measurable endpoints in the body. It’s not about the whole plant. And it’s definitely not about the whole person.
In other words, it’s not herbalism.
That’s why this matters. Because if we’re going to use adaptogens—and we are—we need to understand them not just through the lens of science, but through the lived experience of people. We need to know who they help, when they help, and why they sometimes don’t.
Paul Bergner put it succinctly: adaptogens can increase resistance to stress—but only in the short term.
So maybe it’s time to stop treating adaptogens as miracle herbs. Maybe they’re not universal balancers. Maybe they’re powerful, complex plants with specific uses, specific effects, and specific people they’re suited for.
The mechanisms behind adaptogens are still not fully understood. Theories abound—some suggest they modulate hormonal output, tweak metabolism, stabilize blood sugar, influence the liver, nervous system, immune function, or even cognition. And maybe they do all of that. Or maybe, like so many herbs, they defy being pinned down to one function.
What makes this category so unusual is its diversity. Adaptogens are not a single type of plant with a single type of effect. They’re a constellation—each with its own energetics, affinities, and specific indications. It’s the closest thing Western herbalism has to what Chinese medicine calls qi tonics—plants that rebuild a constitution that is weak, tired, cold, or depleted.
But here’s the part that often gets missed.
Adaptogens are not free energy.
That surge you feel? It’s not a gift. It’s a loan.
If someone is dealing with deep, chronic fatigue—burnout, immune dysfunction, or serious systemic illness—adaptogens may not help. In fact, they can sometimes make things worse. That’s because these plants don’t necessarily restore your energy. What they often do is override the body’s natural signals that say: rest. They disable the brakes.
The result? You feel like you can keep going. And so you do. But the energy isn’t actually there. You’re borrowing against a depleted system. And eventually, the bill comes due.
This phenomenon—pushing past your body’s limits—is called overreaching. It’s been observed with Eleuthero. Research on Rhodiola shows it can stabilize cortisol in the short term, but the long-term picture is less clear. Schisandra appears to reduce cortisol early on, only to increase it later. That’s not support. That’s stimulation.
And this is the paradox: the very herbs people take to help their adrenals may, over time, exhaust them further.
Traditional systems knew this. In China and India—where tonic herbs have a long, nuanced history—these plants were never used casually. They were given to specific people, for specific reasons, and for a limited time. There were protocols. There were cautions. And most importantly, there was context.
In the West, that context is often missing.
We’ve taken a scientific term—adaptogen—and turned it into a catch-all. And in doing so, we’ve flattened out the individuality of the herbs themselves.
That’s a problem.
Because not all adaptogens are created equal. Learning one adaptogen—say, Ashwagandha or Eleuthero—and then assuming the same applies to all the rest is like tasting one spice and thinking you understand cuisine.
As Paul Bergner puts it: “Each herb should be investigated for its own effects, side effects, contraindications, and mechanisms, and utilized for its uniqueness.” Just because Tulsi, Chaga, Schisandra, Shatavari, and Cordyceps are all described as tonics doesn’t mean they do the same thing—or that they’re appropriate for the same person.
Paul Bergner has a way of saying things that changes how you think. His take on adaptogens did exactly that for me.
“The term adaptogen,” he writes, “is not well defined by science outside of tonic effects. It ignores the possible consequences of overstimulation, of masking effects, rebound effects after use or abuse, and promotes overgeneralization both in contemporary herbal literature and in scientific writings on the concept. The term adaptogen should be abandoned in favor of the categories of tonic or rasayana herbs in traditional systems.”
At first glance, it might sound extreme. But look closer—and the logic begins to unfold.
The way adaptogens are commonly taught—especially in modern wellness culture—makes them nearly impossible to use well in a clinical setting. They’ve become a kind of herbal shortcut. An energy hack. A way to “boost performance” or “improve fatigue” with no side effects, no rebound, and—perhaps most seductive of all—no lifestyle changes required.
It’s the promise of something for nothing.
That’s the danger. The now-discarded pieces of the original adaptogen definition have crept into herbal thinking as a kind of magical belief: take this herb, and you’ll feel better—no cost, no caution. But, as Paul points out, misuse of tonic herbs can carry real consequences. Sometimes minor. Sometimes not.
That’s why I agree with him: the term adaptogen should be retired.
Not because the herbs don’t work—but because the word misleads. It strips the plant of its complexity. It detaches the remedy from its context. It invites overuse, or worse, use in the wrong situation.
Instead, we might turn to more grounded, traditional frameworks—chi tonics in Chinese medicine, or rasayanas in Ayurveda. In those systems, tonics are given carefully, with a clear understanding of who they’re for, how long they should be used, and when they should be avoided. They’re not blanket remedies. They’re personalized tools.
Let me offer a different way to think about it.
Imagine a garden hose. Water flowing through that hose represents your vitality—your life force, your energy. Now imagine the water isn’t coming out the end. You feel fatigued. Depleted. Tired. There are three possible explanations:
The well is dry.
Something’s blocking the hose
Or there’s a kink somewhere in the hose.
Each one looks the same from the outside: the energy isn’t flowing. But each one requires a completely different solution.
And here’s the crucial part: in the modern world, most people don’t have a dry well. They’re not truly empty. More often, there’s a kink in the system. Or something stuck. Some tension, some stagnation—something that’s blocking flow.
And this is where the adaptogen story breaks down.
If you take an adaptogen in this situation, so you start filling the well with more water, it won’t matter how much water is in the well. It won’t matter how wide you open the valve. The hose is still kinked. The blockage is still there. And the water—the energy—still won’t move.
To fix it, you have to go deeper.
If the issue is tension, or your hose in kinked you need to relax the system. Use nervines, antispasmodics. If the problem is stagnation, or something’s blocking your hose, you need to clear it. Use alteratives—cleansing herbs that support the organs of elimination and remove excess waste.
In short, you have to treat the obstruction, not just the fatigue.
Imagine forcing more water through a garden hose that’s already kinked. You haven’t solved the issue—you’ve just added pressure. Eventually, the hose bursts. Now the water—your vital force—is leaking out everywhere, but going nowhere useful.
Sure, at first, someone might feel more energized. They think it’s working. But it’s not. Because the underlying cause—whether it’s tension, or stagnation—hasn’t been addressed. And the system, now flooded with more pressure than it can handle, starts to fray.
So when do adaptogens help?
When someone is genuinely depleted.
When the well has run low—not because of one bad week, but because of years of stress, poor sleep, weak nutrition, and nervous exhaustion. In that context, adaptogens can help refill the well. They can support energy slowly, gently. They can open the valve—just a little—and help vitality begin to flow again.
That’s how they were traditionally used.
In Chinese medicine, chi tonic herbs were rarely given to the young. They were reserved for the middle-aged or elderly—people whose vital energy had begun to wane. In Ayurveda, rasayanas were prescribed with intention. Often after purification therapies like panchakarma, when the system had been cleansed and was ready to receive tonification.
In other words, these plants were never meant to be taken casually. They were tools. Used for specific people, in specific situations, for a specific amount of time.
But that’s not how they’re used today.
Today, adaptogens are pitched as quick fixes. As free energy. As a way to feel better without changing anything—no sleep, no rest, no diet, no deeper examination of the cause of fatigue. Just a capsule, a tincture, a powdered blend.
And that’s the real problem. Not the herbs themselves—but the way people use them.
So let me be clear: I’m not anti-adaptogen. These are powerful, beautiful plants. In the right context, they can be profoundly supportive. But I am against misuse. Against treating these herbs like a natural version of a pharmaceutical. Against taking them without considering why fatigue is happening in the first place.
True healing doesn’t come from bypassing the body’s signals. It comes from listening to them.
And when adaptogens are indicated, they should be part of a broader recovery plan—one that includes:
8+ hours of sleep every night
Daily rest (not just sleep—horizontal, quiet time)
Nutrient-dense, whole foods
Regular, moderate movement
Proper hydration
Reduced caffeine, alcohol, and drug intake
These are the real foundations of energy. Because we’re not tired from an adaptogen deficiency. We’re tired from how we live.
So use these herbs thoughtfully. Study them individually. Understand their energetics, their temperature, their moisture qualities, their organ affinities. And above all—don’t assume that just because two plants are called “adaptogens,” they do the same thing.
They don’t.
