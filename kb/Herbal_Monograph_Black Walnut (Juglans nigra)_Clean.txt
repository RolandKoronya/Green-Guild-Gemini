Black Walnut (Juglans nigra)
We tend to think of walnut trees in terms of their nuts. The smooth, golden-brown orbs that arrive each autumn, their shells cracked open at the kitchen table, their soft flesh a staple in holiday baking. But there’s another walnut—darker, more pungent, less forgiving—that holds a very different kind of value.
Black Walnut, or Juglans nigra, is not a subtle tree. Native to the eastern woodlands of North America, it demands space. It needs sun. It needs soil that is deep and moist and untroubled. Try disturbing its roots, or placing it in the shade, and you’ll find it withers. But when given what it needs—light, air, water, silence—it thrives. Hillsides, streambeds, open fields. That’s where you’ll find it.
By the 1600s, it had made its way to Europe. Not because of its medicine, interestingly, but because of its looks. The Black Walnut became fashionable—its towering frame and deeply furrowed bark seen as noble, its wood prized by carpenters. And somewhere in that transition from forest to garden, from wilderness to estate, people began to take note of something else: its healing properties.
There’s an art to harvesting Black Walnut, and no one described it better than Maud Grieve. Writing in A Modern Herbal, she captured both the technique and the subtlety: Pick the leaves singly, in the warmth of June or July. Only in fair weather. Only after the dew has gone. East winds help. So does patience. Leaves stained or wet are useless. Dry them outside, yes—but not in full sun. Half-shade is better. The color holds. The texture remains. They must be elevated—1, maybe 1.2 meters above the ground—to catch the air. And when the evening damp threatens, they must be brought in quickly. The process is precise. The result: potent.
It’s worth noting here that most people are more familiar with Juglans regia, the English walnut. That’s the one you’ll find in grocery stores. The one whose nuts are mild and familiar. But Juglans nigra—Black Walnut—is another story altogether. It’s not just a tree. It’s a medicine chest.
The fresh green hull, the leaves, the bark of the root, even the bark of the tree—each carries its own potency. Each is chosen for a reason. Taken together, they offer something formidable. In states of dampness, toxicity, or stagnation—Black Walnut shines. This isn’t folk wisdom clinging to the past. It’s a knowledge that persists because it works. Because people kept using it. And because, after all these years, it still asks for the same things it always has: good soil, full sun, and a little respect.

In the early days of North American herbalism, there was a tree that healers turned to again and again. Not the Black Walnut—not yet—but its paler cousin, the Butternut (Juglans cinerea). For colonial herbalists, Butternut bark was indispensable. It was gentle. It moved things. Used as a mild alterative for skin issues, and as a laxative and bitter tonic for sluggish digestion, Butternut offered exactly what was needed: consistency, reliability, and a little push when the system slowed down.
But times changed. And so did the trees.
By the 20th century, Butternut had fallen out of favor. Not because it stopped working—but because it started disappearing. A fungal disease swept through its population, leaving conservationists worried and herbalists looking elsewhere. What they found, in the Black Walnut (Juglans nigra), was not just a substitute—but a remedy of greater power.
Black Walnut is not a gentle herb. It’s bitter, and not in a subtle way. There’s an aromatic quality, yes—but the bitterness dominates. That bitterness cools. The aromatic oils, on the other hand, warm. Together, they strike an odd kind of balance. A cooling bitterness that draws down excess, and a warming fire that stokes digestion. This interplay makes Black Walnut something rare: a bitter that stimulates. A carminative that tones. It works on multiple levels.
And if the taste puts you off—you’re not alone. It’s astringent too, which sharpens the flavor into something unmistakably medicinal. Few people enjoy it. But then again, few people enjoy chemotherapy either. Sometimes power doesn’t taste sweet.
Chemically, Black Walnut is formidable. Antifungal. Antibacterial. Antiamoebic. Anthelmintic. These are strong words. In herbalism, there’s a tendency to avoid them—too clinical, too aggressive, too “anti.” But in this case, they fit. Black Walnut doesn’t just nudge the body toward wellness. It goes after invaders. Worms. Fungi. Bacteria. Parasites. It doesn’t negotiate.
Its astringency tightens and tones tissues—especially in the gastrointestinal tract. Its alterative properties work systemically, purifying the blood, clearing stagnation, and nudging the whole system toward health. But this isn’t a plant you take casually. Too much can turn the stomach. Nausea is common when the bitter goes too far. Moderation is not just suggested—it’s required.
Still, at the right dose, Black Walnut shines. It acts as a bitter carminative, stimulating the liver and gallbladder to produce more bile. That means better fat metabolism. That means smoother digestion. That means a system running clean, not clogged.
In a sense, it’s not just a medicine. It’s a reset button.
If there was one voice in the early 20th century herbal world who wouldn’t stop talking about Black Walnut, it was Dr. Edward Shook. But not in the way you might expect. While many herbalists focused on the hulls—the thick, pungent casing that surrounds the walnut—Shook favored the leaf. For him, it wasn’t just about fighting pathogens or clearing infections. It was about toning. Tightening. Restoring tissues that had grown slack.
And that’s part of what makes Black Walnut so interesting. On one level, it fights. It confronts illness directly. But on another, more structural level, it rebuilds. It pulls things back together.
In the digestive tract, Black Walnut’s role is unmistakable. It works like a bitter carminative, stimulating bile and easing digestion. At the same time, it acts as an astringent, tightening lax tissues and reducing excessive secretions. That combination—stimulate and tone—is rare. Most herbs do one or the other. Black Walnut does both.
But its influence doesn’t stop at the gut. Its alterative properties—those subtle shifts in blood quality and tissue integrity—make it useful for the liver and skin as well. And since those two systems often mirror one another, the result is a kind of dual-action support. Clear the liver, and the skin follows.
There’s another system Black Walnut seems to reach, though more quietly: the endocrine system. In particular, the thyroid. Herbalists have noticed a pattern—an affinity. No one’s entirely sure how it works. But there’s something there.
From an energetic standpoint, Black Walnut is contradictory. It’s aromatic, which is warming. But it’s also bitter—which cools. And in the end, the bitter wins. The plant is energetically cooling and drying, with a tonic and astringent nature.
Black Walnut shines in what herbalists call the damp-stagnation tissue state. This is where fluids slow, where waste products accumulate, and where the body starts to create little pockets of stillness—blockages that welcome microbes, infections, imbalances. The old word for this was “bad blood.” Not as a literal statement about the blood itself, but more a metaphor for what collects between the cells when elimination is compromised. The liver slows. The lymph backs up. The bowels clog. And what the body can’t push out through its normal channels—it pushes through the skin.
This is where Black Walnut steps in. It cleanses. It tones. It helps move what’s stuck.
Often, this picture overlaps with low thyroid function. Slow metabolism. Poor circulation. A sense of heaviness in the body—not just physical, but systemic. And while Black Walnut can support these states, it’s most appropriate when there’s also an active infection in the picture—fungal, bacterial, parasitic. It doesn’t just clear sludge. It clears invaders.
As for the leaf—the part Dr. Shook prized—it finds its place in another kind of imbalance: damp relaxation. These are the tissues that have lost their tone, their grip. The leaf is especially good here. You’ll find it in formulas for vascular atony, for prolapsed bowels, for flaccid lungs, a weak heart, or the kind of uterine laxity that leaves everything feeling unsupported. The leaf pulls it all back in.
That’s the story of Black Walnut. Not a sweet herb. Not a gentle one. But one that, in the right hands, knows how to hold the line.
Specific Indications
In modern herbal practice, Black Walnut is something of a paradox. On one hand, it’s considered standard—almost expected. On the other, it’s approached with caution. The leaf and hull are widely used, yes—but always with a kind of reverence. Not because the plant is rare, but because it’s strong.
Patricia Kyritsi Howell describes all this with a clinical simplicity: “This is not the kind of herb you throw into a formula casually. It has a presence. A sharpness. And so, more often than not, it plays a supporting role. A small part of a larger symphony. It’s buffered—softened—by gentler herbs, so that its harsh edges don’t overwhelm the body.
Internally, Black Walnut is used to chase out the things that don’t belong. Systemic yeast infections. Intestinal worms. Parasites. These aren’t everyday complaints, and Black Walnut isn’t an everyday herb. But in the right formula, at the right dose, it becomes something potent and precise.
There’s a kind of duality in how it’s used. In small, regular doses, it functions as a tonic. A cleanser. Something that clears the blood, steadies the system. But scale it up, and the picture changes. Taken in larger amounts, Black Walnut becomes a laxative—strong, unmistakable, impossible to ignore.
Topically, it holds just as much force. A wash or salve made from the green hulls treats ringworm, scabies, cradle cap, and other fungal infections. Here again, the plant isn’t merely soothing—it’s decisive. The decoction of the leaf has long been used as a vaginal douche, offering relief from discharge or itching. And once more, the theme reappears: cleansing, clearing, cutting through.”
Jill Stansbury adds more detail—more texture. She points to acne, comedones, and the kind of skin eruptions that show up when the gut is off. She sees Black Walnut as a bridge between digestion and the skin, especially when the signs are clear: seborrheic dermatitis with crusts behind the ears, papular eruptions, pustules that itch and irritate. These aren’t subtle symptoms. And Black Walnut doesn’t offer subtle answers.
Some trees are communal. They share space, intertwine roots, invite other plants to grow near them. The Walnut is not one of those trees.
The Walnut produces a compound called juglone. It seeps into the soil and discourages competition—literally. Most plants won’t grow within 60 feet of it. And if you look closely, you’ll notice something else: insects don’t linger there either. The Walnut stands alone. Not as an accident of ecology, but as an expression of its very nature.
This expelling quality, so visible in the landscape, echoes in the plant’s medicinal action. Black Walnut drives things out. Parasites. Ringworm. Fungal invaders. It doesn’t coexist. It makes space.
There’s something almost mythic about that.
Ancient cultures certainly thought so. To them, the Walnut wasn’t just food—it was food of the gods. Its Latin name says as much: jovis glans, the nut of Jupiter. Power. Vitality. A gift from above. Crack one open and you find a kernel folded into shapes eerily similar to the human brain. A coincidence, maybe. But not one people overlooked.
Dr. Edward Bach, the English physician who founded the Bach flower remedies, saw something else in Walnut. Not just a medicine, but a metaphor. For him, Walnut—specifically Juglans regia—was for people who needed clarity. The kind of clarity that allows you to step out of old patterns. Break away from the past. Carve a path for yourself, even when others can’t—or won’t—see it.
There’s a psychological mirror here. The same tree that repels nearby growth, that refuses to share its space with weeds or insects, becomes a symbol for the kind of person who is too easily influenced. Someone who needs help establishing boundaries. Making decisions. Standing in their own truth.
The Walnut clears space—physically, emotionally, energetically. It pushes out what doesn’t belong. And whether you’re talking about fungi in the gut or doubts in the mind, the gesture is the same.
Make room.
Clinical Patterns
1. Damp-Heat, Bad Blood, & Systemic Toxicity: There’s a pattern that shows up again and again in traditional systems of medicine. The symptoms vary. The language shifts. But the underlying imbalance remains strikingly consistent. In Ayurveda, it’s called damp-heat. In old Western herbal texts, it might be labeled “bad blood.” Today, we’d likely describe it in terms of systemic inflammation, sluggish metabolism, compromised liver function, or autoimmune disturbance.
But no matter what you call it, the signs are hard to miss.
This is the pattern where stagnation and heat collide. Where the body's ability to process and eliminate waste slows, and the waste starts pushing back. Joints ache. Digestion falters. Skin flares. The bowels back up. There’s a stickiness to it all—a residue of unfinished processes.
And at the center of this pattern is something subtle but crucial: toxicity. Not the kind that comes from outside—pesticides, pollution, chemical spills. This is something deeper. Endogenous. Generated from within when the metabolic fire dims and the body's systems lose their edge.
Ayurveda has a word for this: ama. It's the residue of incomplete digestion. The fog that settles when the fire goes out. Over time, ama builds. It clogs the liver. It gums up the kidneys. And what was once a clean-running engine becomes something closer to a swamp.
The bowels, too, play a role. Chronic constipation slows the entire system, forcing waste products back into circulation. The old physicians had their own language for this. “Excess humors in the blood.” “Toxemia.” “Autointoxication.” Today, you might hear the phrase “leaky gut.” Different terms. Same pattern. And beneath it all, the same fundamental truth: stagnation breeds toxicity.
When this toxic buildup persists, the immune system begins to respond—not with precision, but with fire. The body inflames. Heat rises. Joints swell. Skin erupts. The waste moves outward, toward the surface, and if it can’t be eliminated fully, it settles—in the joints, in the connective tissue, even in the heart. The signs ripple outward.
This is where Black Walnut enters the picture.
Maude Grieve, writing in A Modern Herbal, described its bark and leaves as alterative, laxative, astringent, and detergent. But those are just words until you look closer. She saw results—in scrofula, in eczema, in the kind of skin eruptions that don’t heal. In indolent ulcers. The kind of complaints that signal something deeper than a rash.
And chemically, Black Walnut has the firepower to match. The bitter naphthoquinones found in its leaves give it anti-fungal and antiparasitic effects—both topically and internally. Yeast infections. Candidiasis. Ringworm. Dermatitis. These aren’t surface-level symptoms. They’re expressions of a body trying to eliminate what it can’t process.
Thomas Avery Garran, writing from a Chinese medicine perspective, framed it succinctly: “Clears heat, dries damp, and resolves toxicity.” He saw Black Walnut as a cold herb—one that cuts through inflammatory heat with clarity. One that, when used both internally and externally, has a distinct purpose: to clear the waste, break the fever, and help the system reset.
It’s not a gentle process. But when the system is backed up—when fire meets swamp—gentleness doesn’t always get the job done.
Long before Black Walnut made its way into clinical texts or apothecary drawers, it had a place in the hands of those who lived close to the land. In American Indian and local folk medicine, its usefulness wasn’t theory—it was practice.
The leaves were decocted into antiseptic washes for sores and infected skin. Sometimes, they were dried, ground into powder, and mixed with salt—a simple, improvised salve applied to whatever needed clearing. The juice from the black hull? Believed to cure herpes, eczema, and ringworm. During the Civil War, even battlefield medicine made room for it. Soldiers treated infected wounds with a blend of Black Walnut leaves and bergamot oil.
And it wasn’t just for people. The same boiled leaves were used on horses to keep fleas at bay.
What’s striking isn’t just the range of uses—it’s how consistent they are with what we know now. Athlete’s foot, cradle cap, ringworm. All damp, stagnant, often fungal conditions. In these cases, a simple fomentation using a tincture or decoction of Black Walnut can be powerfully effective.
Just one note of caution: the hulls stain. Skin, cloth, fingers—nothing is safe from the deep, dark brown they leave behind. It’s a small price to pay for their potency.
But the skin is only the surface.
The pattern that calls for Black Walnut runs deeper: a slow heaviness that sits in the joints, the belly, the mind. It’s not just about symptoms. It’s a whole state of being. Swelling. Irritation. Inflammation. Sluggishness. Poor bile flow. Water retention. Cloudy thoughts. This is damp-heat in motion—where swelling and fluid build-up meet inner fire, and the result is something both bloated and inflamed.
And Black Walnut, with its bitter, astringent, cooling energy, knows exactly what to do. It dries the damp. Cools the heat. Moves the waste. It prepares metabolic debris for elimination through the bowels, the liver, the skin. It clears catarrh. It reboots the system.
This pattern—the one that responds so well to Black Walnut—isn’t rare. In fact, it’s surprisingly common in conditions like hypothyroidism, where metabolism slows, circulation stagnates, and the immune system grows either hyperactive or confused. The outward signs show up as eczema, impetigo, boils, abscesses, athlete’s foot, cysts. Joint pain. Fibromyalgia. And underneath it all, often a sluggish digestive system or a long-standing GI infection.
Black Walnut finds its place here. Both internally and topically.
For the skin, it’s used in everything from ringworm and athlete’s foot to shingles and herpes. These last two—viral conditions with neurological components—require more than a single herb. But Black Walnut still plays a part. Especially when paired with allies like Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra), diluted Lemon Balm oil (Melissa officinalis), Calendula (Calendula officinalis), Plantain (Plantago major), or St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum).
The lesson here is simple, though easy to overlook: sometimes the most powerful remedies are the ones that have been with us the longest. Quiet. Staining. A little bitter. And exactly what the body needs when it’s forgotten how to move waste, how to clear heat, how to come back into balance.
2. Infectious Amoebas, Worms, Parasites, and Bacteria: Let’s talk about worms.
Not metaphorical ones. Not the kind that symbolize guilt or anxiety or moral decay. Real worms. The kind that live in your intestines. The kind that, for much of the world, are a daily reality—and for others, a distant and uncomfortable idea.
Intestinal worms are a form of parasite. They tend to be found in tropical and subtropical regions, but they’re not confined to those places. Some species exist in the United Kingdom. And when they do take hold—whether abroad or at home—the effects can be long-lasting. Digestive issues. Systemic symptoms. A general sense that something isn’t quite right.
How do you get them? The routes are many, but most come down to hygiene. A person with worms uses the bathroom and doesn’t wash their hands. They touch a doorknob. Or a piece of fruit. Or the food on your plate. That’s one route. Others include touching or ingesting contaminated soil or water. Walking barefoot. Eating undercooked beef, pork, or freshwater fish. Even raw vegetables rinsed in dirty water.
It’s easy to forget that food hygiene is a luxury—a modern invention unevenly distributed. In many parts of the world, sanitation remains inconsistent. And that’s where most cases arise.
Now, here’s the twist.
In recent years, the holistic health world has seen a boom in “parasite cleanses.” People buying bottles and protocols and bitter tinctures, convinced that worms are lurking in their gut—even if they’ve never left the UK.
Let’s be clear: worms are real. Infections happen. But so does fear-mongering.
In the UK., the odds of getting worms from buying an orange at Waitrose are vanishingly small. Not impossible, but close. Symptoms like diarrhea and weight loss can stem from dozens of other causes. So before you reach for a parasite cleanse, ask a more grounded question: Have you traveled recently? Have your symptoms followed a trip abroad?
That’s when concern becomes appropriate. Not when Instagram tells you it’s time.
The signs of a true parasitic infection are clear, if not always pleasant: actual worms or eggs visible in the stool. A red, itchy rash in a worm-like shape. Prolonged digestive symptoms—sickness, stomach ache, diarrhea lasting more than two weeks. Sudden, unexplained weight loss.
In those cases, you act. And this is where Black Walnut enters.
It’s known for its antiparasitic effects—but that’s only part of the picture. Beyond worms and amoebas, Black Walnut is powerfully antibacterial and antifungal. It can be effective in treating diarrhea caused by Campylobacter or Helicobacter pylori, both of which are tied to ulcers and gastritis. It also has a potent effect on Candida albicans—the yeast that, when left unchecked, causes systemic infections far beyond the gut.
Take the sinuses, for example. Chronic sinus infections are notoriously difficult to treat with antibiotics. Why? Because many of them aren’t strictly bacterial. They have a fungal component. And antibiotics don’t touch fungi. Which is why, for some people, round after round of prescriptions doesn’t work.
Black Walnut might.
A simple rinse—30 drops of tincture in a neti pot with a little salt—has been observed to clear longstanding sinus infections with remarkable speed. Herbalist Paul Bergner offers a nasal spray formula that combines glycerin, Yerba Mansa (Anemopsis californica), and Goldenseal (Hydrastis canadensis). It’s a formula that works. But given what we know about Black Walnut, it might do just as well—or better—in Goldenseal’s place.
Because when it comes to stubborn, recurrent infections—the kind that don’t respond to standard treatments—sometimes what’s needed isn’t stronger medicine.
It’s smarter medicine.
David Winston doesn’t mince words when he talks about Black Walnut. He calls it what it is: a strong antifungal, a strong antibacterial, and—importantly—a vermifuge. That last term might sound antiquated, but it gets to the heart of the matter: Black Walnut is built to expel. Internally, it’s used to treat amoebic infections like Giardia, Blastocystis hominis, and Dientamoeba fragilis. It also targets roundworms, pinworms, and systemic candidiasis. But it doesn’t work alone. Winston pairs it with Elecampane, Sweet Annie, or Quassia—bitters with their own sharp edges, their own capacity to unsettle unwanted guests.
And it doesn’t stop with pinworms.
Black Walnut is also effective against tapeworms. Against E. coli. Against Klebsiella, Salmonella, and Blastocystis hominis. The spectrum is wide. The activity is real. But so is the challenge.
Here’s the rub: Black Walnut tastes awful.
This is more than a matter of preference. It’s a practical issue—especially when it comes to children, who are the most likely to be infected with pinworms. The treatment requires time, consistency, and repeated doses. Compliance is crucial. But getting a child to tolerate something as bitter and astringent as Black Walnut, day after day, is a logistical puzzle many parents can’t solve.
So the herb works. But that doesn’t mean it’s easy.
Beyond its antiparasitic strength, Black Walnut also supports digestion in broader ways. As an astringent, it tightens and tones the intestinal lining, reducing permeability—important in conditions like GI candidiasis and protozoan overgrowth. As a bitter, it stimulates the release of bile. And as a mild carminative—thanks to its volatile oils—it helps soothe digestive discomforts: gas, diarrhea, nausea, and abdominal pain.
That last part, of course, depends on dose. Too much, and nausea becomes a side effect, not a symptom.
Its bitter nature has another benefit: it helps relieve liver congestion. When bile production is low, the signs are subtle but clear—light-colored, marbly stools, chronic constipation, sluggish digestion. Black Walnut addresses all of these by doing what bitters do best: clearing stagnation, opening blocked pathways, and restoring flow to the hepatobiliary system.
This isn’t theory. It’s tradition. Historically, Eclectic physicians used Black Walnut root bark for precisely these reasons—to relieve intestinal inflammation, to slow diarrhea, to heal the gut from within.
Even among the parts of the plant, there are nuances. The leaf, for example, is more pungent, more drying, and notably more antispasmodic than the hull. It quiets the gut differently. It tones where the hull purges. And taken in a high enough dose, a strong infusion of the whole plant becomes purgative—a complete reset for a system in distress.
That’s the pattern here. Black Walnut works because it clears. It closes what has become too loose. It drives out what doesn’t belong. It supports what’s struggling to do its job. And it does all of this with a sharpness, a bitterness, a kind of severity that leaves no room for ambiguity.
You’ll know when it’s working.
3. Hypothyrodid: The endocrine system doesn’t shout. It doesn’t pulse or bleed or ache in the ways we’re used to noticing. It whispers. It coordinates. It governs through chemistry. And among all the glands that make up this delicate internal symphony, one stands out for its sweeping influence over energy, temperature, and pace: the thyroid.
The thyroid gland is shaped like a butterfly—an anatomical metaphor too perfect to ignore—resting beneath the larynx along the trachea, quiet and unassuming. But inside, two types of cells are constantly at work. The follicle cells produce thyroglobulin, which binds to iodine to form thyroid hormones. The parafollicular cells produce calcitonin, which helps regulate calcium levels in the body.
It’s a system of dual functions—metabolic and mineral. And possibly more. Some have speculated that the thyroid secretes trace iodine directly into the blood headed for the brain. Given its proximity to the carotid arteries, it’s an elegant theory. But at this stage, it remains just that—a theory.
When thyroid function begins to falter, the reasons can be many. Sometimes the problem starts high up—in the hypothalamus, which might underproduce thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH). Or it’s the pituitary, not releasing enough thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH). More often, the problem is in the thyroid itself: the gland can’t produce enough thyroid hormone (TH), sometimes due to a lack of iodine, sometimes for reasons less clear.
Ironically, even when iodine deficiency does show up as a root cause, it’s less common now than it once was—thanks to the quiet public health success story of iodized salt. But in those rare cases, it reveals itself in a now-familiar way: goiter. The swelling of the thyroid as it tries—and fails—to convert colloid into active hormone.
The body, for its part, responds logically. The pituitary senses the deficit and ramps up TSH. The thyroid, already struggling, tries to respond—but can’t. Colloid builds up. The swelling worsens. Eventually, under the constant strain, thyroid cells can begin to atrophy.
TH is not a single substance but two: triiodothyronine (T3), the more active form, and thyroxine (T4), its more abundant precursor. When levels of these hormones drop, the entire body feels it. Cellular metabolism slows. Energy drops. Systems start to lag.
In Ayurveda, there’s a name for the “digestive fire”: agni. It’s the principle of metabolic heat—the force that allows cells to process nutrients, eliminate waste, and keep the body in motion. When agni weakens, what follows is stagnation. The same “bad blood” syndrome mentioned earlier. Metabolic waste accumulates. Cells become heavy with unfinished tasks.
The result? A classic damp-stagnation pattern. Torpor. Cold. Fatigue. And a system that, quite literally, forgets how to move.
When the thyroid slows, everything slows with it.
Circulation becomes poor. Fingers and toes go cold. Constipation settles in. The skin dries out. The tissues grow thin and malnourished. And yet, paradoxically, weight begins to accumulate. So does water. The body, despite moving slower, becomes heavier. More burdened. More damp.
This kind of damp accumulation creates more than just physical discomfort. It creates a perfect environment for bacteria to thrive. Infections become harder to shake. Recovery drags on. The immune system falters—not in its defenses, but in its ability to act with precision and speed.
In traditional terms, this is when alteratives come into play.
The blood, thick with waste, begins to choke the system. The hypothalamus—the control tower of hormonal regulation—struggles to read its signals. In Ayurveda, this is the presence of ama, the byproduct of weak agni, or low digestive fire. The solution is simple in principle, though complex in practice: cleanse the blood. Rekindle the fire.
The pattern—once labeled “bad blood syndrome”—lines up eerily well with the modern picture of hypothyroidism. It was called “bad” not in a moral sense, but in a material one. The blood was seen as impure, congested with waste, sluggish with sugar and inflammatory mediators. Chronic skin conditions often followed.
The roots of thyroid deficiency vary. In many cases today, it’s autoimmune—chronic inflammation gradually dismantling the gland’s ability to function. In more extreme cases, environmental damage plays a role. After nuclear fallout, radioactive iodine floods the environment. For people who are iodine-deficient, this becomes dangerous. The thyroid, hungry for iodine, absorbs the radioactive version—and is slowly destroyed by it. But if iodine levels are already sufficient, the body is more likely to reject the toxic variant.
This is where food becomes medicine: kelp, nori, and other seaweeds offer the kind of iodine the body knows how to use—clean, abundant, protective.
Sometimes, the thyroid isn’t the problem. The pituitary is. When the pituitary fails to send adequate signals—specifically, TSH—the thyroid remains underactivated. But in most cases of true hypothyroidism, TSH is elevated. The pituitary is trying. It’s just not getting results. T3 and T4 remain low. The system stalls.
Which brings us, again, to Black Walnut.
There’s a quiet piece of folk medicine, passed along in the Southern United States, that herbalist Phyllis Light helped bring back into view: Black Walnut hulls were traditionally used to treat goiter. The reasoning? Iodine. The black hulls, rich in this mineral, helped nourish the thyroid in times of deficiency.
But the implications run deeper.
If Black Walnut hulls truly do contain iodine—and there’s good reason to believe they do—then their use may address not only iodine-deficiency hypothyroidism, but the broader cascade of imbalances that result from a sluggish thyroid: dampness, stagnation, torpor.
Its bitterness stimulates digestion. It relieves constipation. It opens the portal circulation. It clears the liver. It detoxifies the extracellular fluids—the blood, the lymph. It dries the damp. It cools the heat. And all the while, it offers a gentle nudge to the thyroid itself.
Not a cure-all. Not a miracle. But a pattern-matcher. A plant whose actions line up, step for step, with the body’s needs when the fire is low.
Pharmacologica Data
Let’s step back and ask a simple question: Why does Black Walnut work?
What is it about this plant—its bark, its hulls, its leaves—that gives it such broad, unmistakable potency? The answer, as always, begins with chemistry.
Black Walnut is rich in a class of compounds known as naphthoquinones. The most prominent of these are juglone, hydrojuglone, and nucin. These aren’t obscure phytochemicals tucked away in fringe journals—they’ve been studied, and consistently shown to possess clear, measurable actions: antiprotozoal, antifungal, antibacterial, and antiamoebic.
In other words, they expel what doesn’t belong. Precisely, effectively, and without subtlety.
There’s a sensory clue to their power too: they’re bitter. Intensely so. The kind of bitterness that makes you pause, wrinkle your face, and remember that this is medicine—not food.
Then come the tannins. These are the compounds that give Black Walnut its astringency. They tone tissues, tighten membranes, reduce secretions. It’s what makes the remedy feel drying, even before you understand why.
And rounding out the profile is its essential oil content—specifically the monoterpenes, with a noticeable lean toward pinene. These are the aromatic molecules. They’re what you smell when you break open a fresh hull. But they’re not just for show. These compounds, too, have antifungal and antibacterial effects. Some act as expectorants. Others help modulate inflammation.
So what we have in Black Walnut is a layered defense. The naphthoquinones attack pathogens directly. The tannins tighten the body’s boundaries. The monoterpenes calm, clear, and cleanse. It’s not a single mechanism—it’s an orchestrated system.
Safety
Every strong medicine has its limits. Black Walnut is no exception.
Used in excess, it pushes too hard. Nausea. Diarrhea. Vomiting. These aren’t rare side effects; they’re signals from the body that too much has been taken, or that the preparation wasn’t properly balanced. For that reason, Black Walnut is typically paired with a warming carminative—something to soften its edge, to balance its cold, bitter nature. Ginger, fennel, or cinnamon often play that role.
The key is dosage. Stay within range, and Black Walnut works as intended. Step outside those boundaries, and it lets you know.
Pregnancy and nursing? Here, the warnings are cautious, not categorical. There’s no firm evidence of harm—but the herb is strong, and that strength, in the absence of clear safety data, warrants restraint. So the general guidance remains: avoid internal use during pregnancy and lactation. Topically, too, it has its caveats. Some people—especially those with sensitive skin—may experience irritation from the hulls or concentrated preparations.
And one practical note: if you plan to handle fresh Black Walnut hulls, wear gloves. The stain is not metaphorical. It’s real. It’s deep. It lingers.
As for herb-drug interactions? At this time, none are known.
Which, in the end, says something about the plant itself. Black Walnut is potent, yes. But not unpredictable. Not erratic. It follows a clear pattern. And with the right respect and preparation, it works in a way that few other herbs do—decisively, but not destructively.
Dosage and Preparation
Before you take Black Walnut, pause.
This is not a gentle herb. High doses—especially of the tea or tincture—can have a purgative effect. And not the mild kind. We’re talking about severe diarrhea. Vomiting. A system in full evacuation mode. Which is why the rule here is simple: start low. Stay within range. Don’t assume more is better.
Topical use is a little safer. You can apply Black Walnut to the skin, no problem—at least physiologically. But cosmetically? That’s another story. The stain it leaves behind isn’t subtle. You may find yourself walking around with dark patches on your hands or arms that don’t fade for weeks. This is a medicine that leaves its mark.
Tincture
If you’re making a tincture, fresh hulls are the gold standard. The dried version simply doesn’t compare. The ideal preparation is a 1:2 ratio, with the final alcohol content sitting at 40%. That number, by the way, accounts for the water already present in the fresh herb—so don’t just pour in 40% alcohol and call it a day.
David Winston, one of the more respected voices in modern herbalism, suggests a dosage of 1 to 1.5 ml—about 30 to 45 drops—three times a day. He also recommends adding 10% glycerin to your tincture. Why? Tannins. Without glycerin, they may precipitate out, compromising the stability of the preparation.
Decoction
Technically, you can make a decoction from the dried leaves or hulls. But drinking it? That’s another matter. The flavor is, by all accounts, horrendous. Bitter, astringent, and just plain hard to swallow. For most people, getting down a full cup is a test of willpower. Multiple cups a day, over time, borders on heroic.
Still, if you’re determined: use 1 teaspoon of dried herb per cup of water. Bring to a boil, cover, and simmer gently for 15 minutes. Then strain. If you must, drink half a cup up to three times a day. But remember—fresh is best. Water-based extracts of the dried herb will always fall short next to a properly made tincture.
Fomentation
For external use—skin washes, compresses, douches—you’ll want a decoction of the green hulls. Cover them with water, bring to a boil, and simmer for 20 minutes. Strain, cool, and apply. You can also use the tincture topically, though again, expect staining. Skin, clothes, towels—none are safe. The color lingers.
Topical applications are most useful in two distinct patterns:
Skin conditions marked by damp-heat.
Fungal, parasitic, or bacterial infections.
In these cases, Black Walnut doesn’t just soothe. It clears. It expels. It reclaims the terrain.

One of the most revealing things about an herbalist’s practice isn’t just the individual herbs they use—but how they combine them. Which plants are paired together. Which ones are given dominance. Which are used sparingly, like punctuation marks at the end of a sentence.
Shingles Formula
25% Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)
25% St. John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum)
25% Echinacea (Echinacea angustifolia)
20% Black Walnut Hull (Juglans nigra)
2–5% Cayenne (Capsicum annuum)
This is a formula built for complexity. Shingles isn’t just a viral condition—it’s one that impacts the nerves, the skin, and the immune system all at once. So here, you get Passionflower for calming the nervous system. St. John’s Wort, which has both antiviral and nervine actions. Echinacea, which supports immune function and modulates inflammation. And then comes Black Walnut—not as the star, but as the cleaner. The one that clears damp-heat and fights any secondary fungal or bacterial overgrowth.
Cayenne is the wildcard. Its heat can vary dramatically by batch, so dosage is flexible—between 2% and 5%. You adjust based on how much stimulation is needed.
But this internal formula isn’t meant to work alone. It’s paired with a topical salve: Cayenne for circulation, Lemon Balm essential oil for antiviral activity, Licorice for inflammation, and berberine-rich herbs like Goldenseal or Oregon Grape to disinfect the skin. And to complete the picture? Decoctions of Nettle and Burdock, taken daily, help cool and drain metabolic waste from the system.
Because shingles doesn’t just live on the skin. It moves through the body.

Homemade Nocino
4 quarts Green Walnut hulls (Juglans nigra)
2 Cinnamon sticks (Cinnamomum verum)
1 tbsp Cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum)
1 tbsp Cloves (Syzygium aromaticum)
1 tbsp Ginger (Zingiber officinale)
1 tbsp Juniper berries (Juniperus communis)
1 tbsp Orange peel (Citrus sinensis)
Vodka or white wine
This is medicine disguised as pleasure. A traditional Italian digestif, Nocino uses many of the same herbs that appear in therapeutic formulas—but it shifts their context. The bitter Black Walnut is still the core, but here it’s blended with warming spices, citrus, and alcohol. You quarter the hulls, mix in the spices, submerge them in vodka or white wine, and let them steep for eight weeks.
After that? You strain, bottle, and let it age. Sip it with dessert. Pair it with chocolate or cheese. Or use it as a digestive remedy—30 to 60 drops when discomfort arises.
Sometimes healing is a shot of bitterness. Other times, it’s a quiet ritual.
