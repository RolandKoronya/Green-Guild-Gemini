If you want to understand herbs—really understand them—you have to start by asking a deceptively simple question:
What does this plant actually do to the body?
That’s what an herbal action tells you. Not just what the plant is for, but how it changes something in the body. And that distinction matters more than you might think.
An action describes a physiological shift. A real, observable change. It’s the difference between saying “this herb is for the lungs” and “this herb stimulates the cough reflex to help clear mucus.” One is general. The other is specific. And specificity is everything when it comes to working with herbs effectively.
You’ll come across names like carminative, diaphoretic, expectorant. They may sound old-fashioned, but they tell you something essential: how a plant changes function.
A carminative, for example, relaxes tension and boosts circulation in the gut. It helps release gas. An expectorant stimulates the lungs to move mucus up and out. These actions are tangible. You can feel them, observe them, and trace their effects through the body.
But here’s where things start to shift.
In modern herbalism, the landscape has become crowded with new “actions.” More and more, they reflect scientific discoveries—biochemical pathways, isolated compounds, pharmacological effects. And while those insights can be fascinating, they also risk obscuring the original concept of what an herbal action really is.
Because traditional herbal actions—what we might call vitalist actions—weren’t just about mechanisms. They were about felt experience. They told you not just what a plant did, but how it influenced the system as a whole—physically, energetically, even emotionally.
So it helps to make a distinction.
Many of the newer terms you’ll see—especially in modern materia medica—aren’t really actions at all. They fall into three broad categories:
Biochemical mechanisms – Like “COX-2 inhibitor.” That might be useful to know, especially if you're studying pharmacology. But it doesn’t tell you what you’ll feel in the body—or how the herb changes the flow of energy or tone.
Disease or symptom-based labels – These are easy to spot. They almost always start with “anti-”: anti-tumor, anti-migraine, anti-inflammatory. They name the problem—but not the process. They tell you what the herb is used for, not how it works.
Organ affinities – Terms like hepatic or stomachic fall into this group. They point you to the right organ, which is helpful. But again, they don’t say how the herb acts when it gets there.
That doesn’t mean these terms are useless. Far from it. They’re important pieces of the picture. But in a traditional profile, they belong in a different section. They’re descriptors—not actions.
Why does this matter?
Because a vitalist action doesn’t just tell you what a herb does. It tells you what kind of impact that action has on the system as a whole. Not just the physiological change—but the energetic shift that follows.
Take a diuretic. It moves fluid out of the tissues and through the kidneys. That’s the obvious part. But the deeper effect? The body loses moisture. It dries out. And that, in energetic terms, means the system is shifting—quietly but measurably—toward dryness.
Let’s say you look up “herbs for cough.” You find a list of 20 plants. But those 20 expectorants don’t work the same way. Some are warming, some cooling. Some stimulate, others soothe. Without knowing how each plant works—and what kind of cough you're dealing with—you’re just guessing.
This is the heart of it.
You can say an herb is anti-inflammatory or liver-supportive—but until you understand why it reduces inflammation or how it supports the liver, you don’t really know the plant.
And that’s why vitalist actions matter. They offer a map—not just to what a herb does, but to how it changes the body, the experience, the constitution.
And once you start thinking this way, everything starts to click. You don’t just memorize herbs. You begin to understand them.
